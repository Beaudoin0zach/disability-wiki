# Media, Arts & Culture

Disabled people create and consume media. This section provides access to disability-centered media, celebrates disabled creators, critiques harmful representation, and documents disability culture through arts and creative expression.

## About This Section

Media shapes how disabled people see themselves and how society sees us. When media presents disabled people as tragic, inspiration, or invisible, it affects our self-image and opportunities. This section highlights media by disabled creators, critiques problematic representation, and celebrates disability arts and culture.

## Books by and About Disabled People

**Disability memoirs**:
- First-person accounts of disability
- Beautifully written, often powerful
- Learn from others' experiences
- Range from clinical to lyrical

**Disability fiction**:
- Disabled characters in stories
- Representation in narrative
- Disability as normal part of character (not plot point)
- Disability-centered science fiction and fantasy

**Disability theory and justice**:
- Academic and accessible writing on disability
- Disability justice frameworks
- Critical analyses
- Historical perspectives

**Comics and graphic novels**:
- Accessible visual medium
- Often creative disability representation
- Humor, honesty, complexity

**Children's books**:
- Disability representation for kids
- Books by disabled authors/illustrators
- Normalizing disability for all children

## Representation Matters

**Inspiration porn**: Using disabled people's accomplishments as motivation for non-disabled people ("if they can do it despite disability, you can do anything")
- Dehumanizing
- Often focuses on mundane tasks as "amazing"
- Centers non-disabled people's feelings
- Problem: not about disabled person's experience

**Tragedy narratives**: "Poor disabled person suffered and overcame"
- Suggests disability is tragedy
- Suggests individual willpower solves disability
- Ignores systemic barriers
- Often told by non-disabled people

**Savior narratives**: Non-disabled person rescues disabled person
- Centers non-disabled people
- Ignores disabled people's agency
- Creates problematic power dynamics

**Invisible/absent**: Disabled people not shown at all
- Suggests disability doesn't exist
- Creates ignorance
- Limits disabled people's sense of possibility

**Good representation**:
- Disability as normal part of life
- Disabled people with agency
- Complex, nuanced portrayals
- Disabled creators in control of stories
- Diversity of disabilities and experiences

## Disability Culture

Disability has culture:

**Humor**: Disability jokes, often dark and honest

**Communication**: In-group references, language, norms

**Values**: Disability justice, community care, accessibility

**History**: Movement history, activism

**Traditions**: Disability culture events, conferences, online spaces

**Pride**: Disability pride, celebration of disability identity

Disability culture is real and vibrant. It's different from mainstream culture in ways that matter.
