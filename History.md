# DISABILITY HISTORY & CULTURE

Disabled people have always existed. For most of human history, we were often integral to our communities—valued for our knowledge, skills, and perspectives. Understanding this history challenges the idea that disability is new, that it's tragedy, or that modern "accommodation" invented inclusion.

This section explores how different societies have understood and supported disabled people across history and cultures, centering disabled people's own stories and contributions.

## WHY DISABILITY HISTORY MATTERS

### Challenges the Tragedy Narrative
Most disability media tells stories of tragedy and "overcoming." Real history shows:
- Disabled people have always been valuable
- Accommodation is ancient, not modern invention
- Many societies actively included disabled people
- We lost inclusive practices through industrialization, colonialism, ableism
- Disabled people organized, advocated, created culture

### Provides Disability Pride Foundation
When you know disabled ancestors and peers built things, survived, thrived:
- Disability becomes normal part of human experience
- You see your own capacity reflected back
- Community feels deeper (lineage, history, culture)
- Internalized ableism weakens

### Informs Contemporary Disability Justice
Understanding how colonialism created disability racism, how capitalism created "disposable" people, how eugenics wasn't aberration but logical outcome of ableism:
- You see systemic nature of oppression
- Individual accommodation seems insufficient
- Systemic change becomes imperative
- Connection to all justice movements clear

### Reclaims Knowledge Disabled Communities Have Lost
When institutionalization and colonialism scattered disabled knowledge:
- How to be Deaf (before oralism forced assimilation)
- How blind people navigated (before braille/technology replaced cultural knowledge)
- How Neurodivergent people organized societies
- How disabled people managed disability without medical intervention

## MAJOR THEMES ACROSS HISTORY

### Inclusion Was the Norm, Not Exception

**Pre-industrial societies typically:**
- Valued different skills and perspectives
- Found practical accommodations
- Integrated disabled people into community
- Sometimes held disabled people in high regard (shamans, healers, spiritual leaders)

**What changed:** Industrialization required standardization, speed, "productivity"—things disabled people often didn't fit into. Capitalism decided whose bodies were valuable.

---

### Disability is Often Created, Not Natural

**Important distinction:**
- **Impairment** = biological reality (deaf, blind, mobility difference, neurodivergence, chronic illness)
- **Disability** = social oppression response to impairment

A blind person in society that values visual info and provides no accommodations = disabled.
Same blind person in society that provides accessible technology and values non-visual skills = not disabled (just has impairment).

**Historical example:** Deafness was impairment in all societies. Disability (social oppression) varied:
- In some Indigenous societies: valued as healers, had specific roles
- In deaf community (self-created): had own language, culture, community—no disability
- In oral/hearing-dominant societies: often disabled due to lack of accommodations

---

### Disability Community is Ancient

- Deaf schools formed communities (19th century onwards)
- Blind people's organizations (19th century onwards)
- Disabled people organized politically (20th century)
- Culture developed (language, values, practices)

We are not new. Our community is not new. Our organizing is not new.

---

### Disabled People Were Never Passive

- Fought back against institutionalization
- Organized for rights
- Created culture and community
- Refused genocide (eugenics resistance)
- Built movements
- Changed society

Current disability rights movement built on decades of activism and resistance.

## INTERSECTIONALITY IN DISABILITY HISTORY

Disability history cannot be separated from:
- **Race/ethnicity** — Colonialism, slavery, genocide all used disability; disabled POC face compounded oppression
- **Gender** — Disabled women's history different from disabled men's; reproductive control
- **Sexuality & Gender Identity** — LGBTQ+ disabled history often erased
- **Class** — Disability deeply connected to poverty; wealthy disabled treated differently
- **Indigeneity** — Indigenous approaches to disability different; colonialism destroyed these
- **Geography** — Global South disability history vastly different from Western

## DISABILITY ACROSS TIME PERIODS

### Hunter-Gatherer Societies (Most of human history)
**General patterns:**
- Mobile, small groups
- Valued different skills
- Disabled people often included
- Accommodations based on need/culture
- Some evidence of care for severely disabled
- Variation: some inclusive, some not (no universal pattern)

**Examples:**
- Archaeological evidence of disabled people living long, integrated lives
- Some skeletal remains show healing care over long periods
- Oral histories of integrated disabled people

### Agricultural Societies (8000 BCE - Industrial Revolution)
**General patterns:**
- Settlement allowed specialization
- Disabled people could do different work
- Often valued (healers, crafts people, spiritual roles)
- Community-based support
- Some exclusion/infanticide (not universal)

**Examples:**
- Egyptian evidence of disabled people in society
- Indigenous American farmers integrated disabled people
- Medieval monasteries (care mixed with segregation)

### Industrial & Colonial Era (1750-1950)
**What changed:**
- Factories needed "normal" bodies
- Speed, repetition, standardization valued
- Disabled people didn't fit
- Solution: Separate into institutions
- Colonialism spread this globally, destroyed inclusive practices

### Modern Era (1950-present)
**What changed:**
- Disabled people resisted institutions
- Fought for rights
- Created culture and community
- Built movements
- Changing society (slowly)

## WHY HISTORY IS ACTIVISM

Understanding disability history is not academic. It's political because:
- Shows disability rights are possible (societies have done it before)
- Shows ableism is specific to certain systems (capitalism, colonialism, industrialism)
- Reclaims disabled pride and culture
- Connects disabled liberation to all liberation
- Empowers organizing for change

## LEARNING FROM HISTORY

### What We Can Reclaim
- Community-based support (vs. individualized "accommodations" only)
- Disabled people as contributors, not burden
- Multiple ways of knowing and being valued
- Cultural pride and identity
- Collective responsibility for each other

### What We Must Not Repeat
- Eugenics ideology (still present in genetic testing, prenatal screening, etc.)
- Institutionalization (still happening; must end)
- Infanticide or "mercy killing" narratives
- Colonialism destroying inclusive practices
- Ableist violence

### What We Build Forward
- History-informed disability justice
- Intersectional understanding (disability + all oppressions)
- Global South leadership and knowledge
- Centering disabled people (especially most marginalized)
- Systems change, not just individual accommodation
