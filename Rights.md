Disabled people have legal rights—and it's worth understanding them.

## BY COUNTRY & REGION

### UNITED STATES
The US has one of the most developed disability rights frameworks:

- **[[ADA: Americans with Disabilities Act]]** — Comprehensive federal law covering employment, public services, transportation, communications
- **[[Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act]]** — Education and federal programs; broader than ADA
- **[[IDEA: Individuals with Disabilities Education Act]]** — Education K-12; free appropriate public education (FAPE)
- **[[Fair Housing Act]]** — Housing and rental rights; protection from discrimination
- **[[Air Carrier Access Act]]** — Air travel rights; airline accommodations
- **[[ADA Title I: Employment]]** — Workplace rights; reasonable accommodations; discrimination
- **[[ADA Title II: Public Services]]** — Government services; public transportation
- **[[ADA Title III: Public Accommodations]]** — Businesses and private organizations; accessibility requirements
- **[[State/Province Disability Laws]]** — Each US state has additional protections beyond federal law

---

### CANADA
Federal and provincial protections with different frameworks by province:

- **[[Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms]]** — Constitutional protections; equality rights
- **[[Canadian Human Rights Act]]** — Federal anti-discrimination law; "duty to accommodate"
- **[[AODA: Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act]]** — Ontario's specific law; most developed in Canada
- **[[Provincial Disability Laws]]** — Each province has different frameworks and protections
- **[[Employment Rights in Canada]]** — Workplace accommodations; federal and provincial standards

---

### UNITED KINGDOM
Unified framework covering England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland:

- **[[Equality Act 2010]]** — Primary disability rights law; covers employment, education, services, housing
- **[[Human Rights Act 1998]]** — Additional protections; Article 8 (private life); Article 14 (non-discrimination)
- **[[Employment Rights (UK)]]** — Workplace protections; reasonable adjustments
- **[[Education Rights (UK)]]** — K-12 and higher education; local authority duties
- **[[Scotland/Wales/Northern Ireland: Regional Variations]]** — Some variations in how laws apply regionally

---

### AUSTRALIA & NEW ZEALAND
Different frameworks in each country:

**Australia:**
- **[[Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (DDA)]]** — Primary disability rights law
- **[[National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS)]]** — Unique model; funding system for supports
- **[[Australian Human Rights Act]]** — Additional protections

**New Zealand:**
- **[[Human Rights Act 1993 (NZ)]]** — Primary protections; disability discrimination prohibited
- **[[Health and Disability Commissioner (NZ)]]** — Oversight for healthcare

---

### EUROPEAN UNION
EU framework + individual member state laws:

- **[[European Accessibility Act]]** — Digital and physical accessibility requirements
- **[[EU Equal Treatment Directive]]** — Anti-discrimination framework
- **[[Reasonable Accommodation in EU]]** — Requirements for all member states
- **[[EU Member State Laws]]** — Individual countries have varying protections
  - Germany, France, Spain, Italy, Poland, etc. all have different specific laws

## UNDERSTANDING YOUR RIGHTS

**Important note:** These are your rights, but they're only as good as enforcement.

Many laws look great on paper but aren't enforced equally. This varies by:

**Geography:**
- Wealthy countries tend to enforce better
- Global South often has weaker enforcement
- Even within wealthy countries: some states/provinces enforce better than others

**Disability type:**
- Physical disabilities more recognized
- Neurodivergence often overlooked
- Invisible disabilities frequently dismissed
- Rare conditions especially under-protected

**Race & identity:**
- Racism and other discrimination intersect with disability rights violations
- Disabled people of color face compounded barriers
- LGBTQ+ disabled people may face additional discrimination
- Undocumented disabled people have fewer protections

**Wealth:**
- Rich people can sue more easily than poor people
- Ability to hire lawyers matters
- Some organizations provide free legal help (limited)

**Why this matters:** Laws change because disabled people fought for them. Enforcement improves because disabled communities organize and advocate.

## FILING A COMPLAINT

If your rights are violated, you have options:

1. **Document what happened** — Date, time, who, what happened, witnesses
2. **Report internally** — Many places have complaint processes
3. **File formally** — EEOC (US), Human Rights Commission, etc.
4. **Get legal help** — Organizations can help you navigate

## INTERSECTIONALITY & RIGHTS

Disability rights laws sometimes miss intersecting identities:

**Racial discrimination + ableism:**
- Disability laws don't always address racism
- Need both disability rights AND racial justice lens

**Gender discrimination + ableism:**
- Sexism and ableism often combined
- Disabled women face specific barriers

**Immigration status + disability:**
- Undocumented disabled people have fewer protections
- Fear of deportation affects help-seeking
- Some programs not available to undocumented people

**LGBTQ+ identity + disability:**
- LGBTQ+ disabled people face compounded discrimination
- Some disability spaces not LGBTQ+-friendly
- Need both disability AND LGBTQ+ rights frameworks

**Class + disability:**
- Poverty intersects with disability
- Poor disabled people have harder time accessing justice
- Economic barriers to fighting discrimination

## GLOBAL PERSPECTIVE

**These laws exist mainly in wealthy Western countries.** 

Reality check:
- World's most disabled people live in countries with NO disability rights laws
- Limited enforcement even where laws exist
- Legal systems often tied to poverty and colonialism
- Informal economies where laws don't apply
- Colonial legacies shaping current legal systems

**This wiki is working to include Global South resources**, but it's a major gap we're addressing. If you're in Global South, please contribute your knowledge of:
- How laws actually work (or don't) in your country
- Disability rights movements in your region
- Cultural approaches to disability different from Western models
- What real protections exist vs. what's on paper
