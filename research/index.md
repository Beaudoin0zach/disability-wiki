# Research & Data

Disability research has a complicated history. Much research has been conducted on disabled people without us, treating us as subjects rather than partners. This section provides access to disability research, highlights ethical disability research, and teaches how to think critically about disability data.

## About This Section

"Nothing about us without us" applies to research. Disabled people should lead disability research, not just participate in it. Research should serve disabled communities, not just academic careers or medical industries.

This section covers where to find disability research, how to understand research critically, ethical research approaches, and how to participate in research if you choose.

## History of Disability Research

**Medical research on disabled people**:
- Often conducted without consent
- Abusive and exploitative
- "Doctor's knows best" approach
- Disabled people as passive subjects

**Disability studies emergence**:
- Disabled scholars questioning research practices
- Focus on disability from insider perspective
- Social model research instead of medical model
- Self-advocacy and peer research

**Current approaches**:
- Participatory research (disabled people as partners)
- Community-based research
- Disability justice research
- Peer research and lived experience expertise

## Disability Statistics

How many disabled people are there?

**It depends on**:
- How disability is defined
- What conditions are counted
- Age of population surveyed
- Country and context
- Question phrasing

**Common statistics**:
- WHO: Over 1 billion people have disabilities (15% of global population)
- U.S.: Around 26-27% of adults have disabilities (varies by survey)
- Much higher among elderly
- Invisible disabilities significantly undercounted

**Disability disparities**:
- Disabled people have higher poverty rates
- Lower employment rates
- Less education
- Higher health risks
- Face discrimination

**Important caveat**: Statistics about disability often serve specific purposes (funding, policy, research). Question whose interests are served.

## Critical Disability Research

Good disability research:

**Centers disabled people**:
- Led by disabled researchers
- Includes disabled people in design and analysis
- Asks questions disabled people want answered
- Benefits disabled communities

**Uses participatory methods**:
- Disabled people as co-researchers
- Peer research
- Community validation of findings
- Shared decision-making

**Recognizes disabled expertise**:
- Lived experience as valid knowledge
- Values disabled people's interpretations
- Includes disabled people's perspectives
- Respectful of disability identity and culture

**Is ethical**:
- Informed consent
- No coercion
- Accessible participation
- Confidentiality protected
- Benefits disclosed

**Considers intersectionality**:
- Addresses racism, transphobia, classism alongside ableism
- Includes multiply-marginalized disabled people
- Recognizes how systems of oppression intersect

## Problematic Disability Research

Red flags for problematic research:

**"Cure" focus**: Research aimed at eliminating disability rather than improving quality of life for disabled people

**Deficit focus**: Only highlighting problems, not strengths or positive aspects

**No disabled leadership**: Researchers talking about disability without disabled people involved

**Exploitation**: Using disabled people for data without benefit to disabled community

**Lack of accessibility**: Can't participate if disabled; materials not accessible

**Erasure of disabled voices**: Findings presented without disabled people's input or critique

**Perpetuating stereotypes**: Reinforcing harmful ideas about disability

**No accountability**: No mechanism for disabled people to challenge findings

## Participating in Research

If you decide to participate in research:

**Ask questions**:
- Who is conducting the research?
- Will disabled people lead/be partners?
- What's the purpose?
- How will results be used?
- What are the risks?
- What are the benefits?
- Will participants receive results?
- Who approved the study (IRB)?

**Your rights**:
- To say no
- To withdraw anytime
- To understand what's happening
- To ask questions
- To have interpretation/AAC if needed
- To compensation for participation
- To confidentiality

**Good practices**:
- Research provides accessible participation
- Compensation for time
- Accessible environment
- Choice in how you participate
- Clear communication
- Easy exit without penalty

## Interpreting Research Critically

When you read disability research:

**Ask about methods**:
- Who conducted it?
- Who participated?
- How were disabled people treated?
- Were conflicts of interest present?

**Question findings**:
- What does the data actually show?
- What interpretation is presented?
- Could alternative interpretations fit the data?
- Whose interests does this serve?

**Consider context**:
- How has similar research been used?
- What systemic issues does this research ignore?
- Who benefits from these conclusions?

**Look for intersectionality**:
- Are multiple disabled communities represented?
- Are race, class, gender, other identities addressed?
- Are findings different for different groups?

**Notice what's missing**:
- Whose voices aren't included?
- What aspects of disability aren't studied?
- Who gets to define the problem?

## Participatory Research

Participatory research approaches:

**Community-based participatory research (CBPR)**:
- Community members are co-researchers
- Community defines questions
- Community approves results
- Results shared with community first

**Peer research**:
- Disabled people research disability
- Often more trusted by disabled community
- Recognizes lived experience as expertise
- Often more accessible and ethical

**Collaborative research**:
- Researchers and community work together
- Shared decision-making
- Mutual learning

These approaches produce better research and better outcomes for disabled communities.

## Disability Advocacy and Research

Using research for advocacy:

**Know the research**: Understand findings and limitations

**Use research strategically**: Reference good research in advocacy

**Challenge bad research**: Publicly critique harmful research

**Fund good research**: Support researchers working ethically

**Document lived experience**: Peer research and stories are also evidence

**Stay critical**: Don't accept research uncritically just because it supports your position
