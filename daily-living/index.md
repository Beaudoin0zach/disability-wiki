# Daily Living & Lifestyle

Daily life with disability requires adaptive strategies, accessible equipment, and sometimes creative problem-solving. This section covers practical guidance on everything from personal care to hobbies, helping you maintain independence and quality of life.

## About This Section

Daily tasks that non-disabled people do without thinking—bathing, cooking, getting dressed—may require planning, adaptive equipment, or support for disabled people. This isn't tragedy; it's adaptation.

This section celebrates the creativity, resilience, and ingenuity of disabled people solving daily living challenges. We cover assistive technology, adaptive clothing, accessible cooking, exercise, hobbies, travel, and enjoying life with disability.

## Assistive Technology

Assistive Technology (AT) is any device, software, or equipment that helps you do what you need to do. Examples:

**Mobility AT**: Wheelchairs, scooters, walkers, canes, adaptive bikes

**Communication AT**: AAC devices, screen readers, speech-to-text

**Hearing AT**: Hearing aids, cochlear implants, captioning systems

**Vision AT**: Screen readers, magnifiers, white canes, guide dogs

**Cognitive AT**: Planners, reminders, organizational systems

**Self-care AT**: Grab bars, reaching tools, adapted utensils, shower chairs

Most disabled people use multiple forms of AT. AT changes your life—not magically, but meaningfully.

## Personal Care Adaptations

Personal care includes bathing, grooming, toileting, and dressing. Adaptive strategies:

**For mobility disabilities**:
- Roll-in showers with grab bars
- Accessible toilet/bidet
- Adaptive clothing easier to put on/take off
- Reaching tools for grooming
- Wet room setup (waterproof floor)

**For fine motor disabilities**:
- Larger, easier-grip tools
- One-handed scissors/utensils
- Velcro instead of buttons
- Elastic waistbands
- Pre-cut washcloths

**For cognitive disabilities**:
- Visual schedule/checklist
- Labeled containers
- Simplified routines
- Reminders/alarms
- Adaptive clothing

**For sensory disabilities**:
- Scent/texture considerations
- Temperature control
- Accessible design

## Cooking with Disability

Cooking accessible looks different for different disabilities:

**Mobility adaptations**:
- Reachable cabinets (some high, some low)
- Lightweight cookware
- Pre-cut or frozen vegetables
- One-handed food prep tools
- Rolling cart/stool
- Accessible counter height

**Chronic illness/fatigue adaptations**:
- Meal prep on good days
- Frozen meals (no shame)
- Slow cooker/instant pot (minimal work)
- Simple recipes
- Accepting that cooking fluctuates

**Cognitive adaptations**:
- Simple recipes
- Visual instructions
- Labeled containers
- Grocery lists
- Simplified shopping

**Access to food**:
- Grocery delivery
- Food assistance (SNAP)
- Community gardens/coops
- Free meal programs
- Accessible restaurants

Nutrition is important. So is not starving yourself. Use whatever resources you need.

## Exercise and Adaptive Sports

Movement is important, but "exercise" looks different for disabled people.

**Key principle**: Listen to your body; don't push through pain or fatigue.

**Adaptive exercise**:
- Swimming/water aerobics (low impact)
- Wheelchair sports
- Adapted yoga/tai chi
- Stretching (within limits)
- Walking with aids
- Adapted dance
- Adaptive martial arts

**Pacing and energy management**:
- Exertion might cause a flare later
- Progress is measured in what you CAN do, not comparison to others
- Rest is part of recovery
- Inconsistency is normal

**Disability sports**:
- Wheelchair basketball
- Adaptive skiing
- Hand cycling
- Wheelchair dancing
- Disability sports leagues
- Adaptive surfing/climbing
- Inclusive recreational programs

[Learn more in Exercise & Adaptive Sports →](/daily-living/exercise-and-adaptive-sports)

## Travel with Disability

Travel is possible with planning:

**Before you go**:
- Research accessibility of destination
- Book accessible accommodations
- Arrange transportation
- Pack adaptation gear (meds, equipment, backup supplies)
- Get travel insurance
- Inform doctor of travel plans

**During travel**:
- Take breaks as needed
- Use accessibility accommodations
- Don't over-schedule
- Allow for flare days
- Connect with local disability community if possible

**International travel**:
- Accessibility varies by country
- Some medications illegal in some countries
- Travel visas may ask about disability
- Language barriers with accessibility communication

**Travel planning resources**:
- Accessible travel guides
- Disability organizations in destination
- Travel accessible websites
- Connecting with local disabled community

## Accessible Fashion and Adaptive Wear

Clothing should work with your body, not against it.

**Adaptive clothing options**:
- Front-zip bras (one-handed)
- Velcro instead of buttons
- Elastic waistbands
- Loose fit (easier to pull on)
- Front-closure shirts
- Magnetic zippers
- Seated fit (different proportions for wheelchair users)
- Sensory-friendly fabrics (no tags, soft)
- Sleeveless or adapted sleeves (for wheelchair users)

**Where to find adaptive wear**:
- Specialized adaptive brands
- DIY modifications
- Extensions and adapters
- Thrift stores and alteration
- Custom tailoring

**Clothing for different situations**:
- Professional clothes that work in your wheelchair
- Accessible activewear
- Sensory-friendly everyday wear
- Accessible formal wear

You deserve to look good and feel confident.

## Recreation and Hobbies

Disability doesn't stop hobbies. They may look different, but joy is still possible.

**Adapted recreation**:
- Video games with adaptive controllers
- Reading with large print or audio
- Art with adaptive tools
- Music (playing, listening, creating)
- Photography with accessible equipment
- Writing/journaling
- Crafts with modified techniques
- Gardening (raised beds, accessible tools)
- Online community and gaming

Your hobbies matter. They provide meaning, escape, creativity, and connection.

## Pets and Service Animals

Living with animals brings joy and companionship.

**Pets with disability**:
- Pet care modifications (if mobility limited)
- Lower grooming expectations
- Pet care assistance (dog walker, pet sitter)
- Pets providing emotional support (even if not trained service animal)
- Accessible pet equipment

**Service animals**:
- Trained to perform specific tasks
- Public access rights
- Different from emotional support animals

See [Service Animals →](/transport/service-animals) for more information.

## Maintaining Independence

Independence doesn't mean doing everything yourself. Independence means:
- Making decisions about your life
- Controlling your environment
- Choosing your supports
- Having autonomy in daily living

Asking for help is independence. Using AT is independence. Accepting support is independence.

---

## Celebrating Life with Disability

Disability is hard. It's also not all-consuming. You can:
- Find joy and meaning
- Pursue interests and hobbies
- Build relationships
- Create and express yourself
- Contribute to community
- Feel satisfaction and pride
- Live a good life

Disability doesn't define your life, but it shapes it. Both things can be true.
