# Twitter/X Communities

**What this page covers:** Finding disability communities on Twitter/X, using hashtags and movements, and building disability organizing on the platform.

**Important context:** Accessibility team disbanded 2022-2023. Captions removed from Spaces (Feb 2023). Despite challenges, disability Twitter remains irreplaceable for real-time organizing, activism, and mutual aid.

**Quick find:** Search disability hashtags (#DisabledTwitter, #CripTheVote, #ActuallyAutistic). Follow disability organizations and activists. Join Twitter Chats at scheduled times.

---

## Why Twitter/X for Disability Communities

**Strengths:**
- Real-time connection enabling fast organizing
- Direct access to media, officials, celebrities
- Mutual aid networks (money, resources, support)
- Diagnostic help (people share experiences)
- Years of established community connections
- Public organizing (visibility, accountability)
- Disability movements documented in real-time

**Limitations:**
- Accessibility declining (team disbanded)
- Algorithm chaos since 2022 acquisition
- Toxic potential (harassment, arguments)
- Misinformation spreads fast
- Real-time can be overwhelming
- Takes constant engagement
- Screenshots can be weaponized
- Community can feel performative

**Context:** Despite challenges, many disabled organizers remain because alternatives haven't replaced it. Years of community built here can't be easily moved.

### Following Strategy

**Start by following:**
- Disability organizations (ASAN, ADAPT, Disability Visibility Project)
- Individual disabled activists and writers
- Content creators discussing your condition
- Journalists covering disability
- Other disabled people in your life

**Find major accounts:**
- Follow "followers of followers" of disability orgs
- Look at who retweets disability content
- Check lists that disability organizations have created
- Use search for "[condition] + activist" or "[condition] + writer"

### Joining Twitter Chats

**Popular disability Twitter chats:**
- **#spooniechat** - Weekly Twitter chat about chronic illness (specific time weekly)
- **#DisabilityChat** - General disability discussion (various times)
- **#NeuroChat** - Neurodiversity-focused chat
- Condition-specific chats (search your condition + "chat")

**How to participate in Twitter Chat:**
1. Search for hashtag + "chat schedule" to find time
2. Join at scheduled time (usually 30-60 minutes)
3. Follow conversation thread
4. Answer moderator's questions with Q1, Q2, Q3, etc.
5. Include chat hashtag in your response
6. Moderator retweets interesting responses

**Benefits:**
- Real-time community
- Timed participation (doesn't require all-day engagement)
- Discover new people
- Collective wisdom on specific topics

## Twitter/X Platform Norms & Culture

### Disability Twitter Etiquette

**Use CamelCase hashtags:**
- Correct: #DisabledTwitter, #ActuallyAutistic
- Incorrect: #disabledtwitter, #actuallyautistic
- Why: Screen readers read each capital letter separately (accessibility)
- Example: #DisabledTwitter = "Disabled Twitter" (readable)
- Example: #disabledtwitter = "d-i-s-a-b-l-e-d-t-w-i-t-t-e-r" (not readable)

**Add alt text to EVERY image:**
- Navigate to image after tweeting
- Click "Edit" → "Add description"
- Be descriptive: what, who, context
- Character limit is generous (use it!)
- Example: Not just "image" → Instead: "Screenshot of tweet with graph showing disability statistics by country"

**Content warnings for difficult topics:**
- Use warning language clearly before spoiler
- Format: "CW: [trigger] // [content]"
- Example: "CW: medical trauma // This thread discusses doctor abuse"
- Allows space before difficult content

**Indicate content type:**
- [VIDEO] - Before tweeting video
- [AUDIO] - Before tweeting audio
- [THREAD] - Multi-part tweet
- Helps accessibility and engagement

**Minimize decorative emoji:**
- Don't use: 👏 between 👏 words 👏
- Screen readers announce each emoji (extremely annoying)
- OK to use: Single emoji at end of tweet
- Problematic: Emoji as substitute for words

**Respectful disagreement:**
- Assume good intent
- Ask clarifying questions ("Can you say more about...?")
- Don't quote-tweet to mock
- Public debate of ideas = OK, personal attacks = not OK

**Identity-first language in disability Twitter:**
- Preferred: "autistic person" (identity-first)
- Less preferred: "person with autism" (person-first)
- Note: Different preferences by condition and individual
- Best: Ask or use person's stated preference

### Disability Twitter Movements & Campaigns

**#CripTheVote** (founded January 2016)
- Focus: Disability political engagement and voter information
- Function: Non-partisan, educational, voting access
- Activity: Increased during elections
- Join by: Using hashtag, following accounts, participating in Twitter chats

**#DisabledandCute** (founded by @Keah_Maria)
- Focus: Celebrating disabled beauty, countering ableism
- Function: Visibility, resistance, joy
- Activity: Regular posts celebrating disabled people
- Join by: Sharing disabled selfies with hashtag

**#MillionsMissing** (ME/CFS awareness campaign)
- Focus: Highlighting people too sick to protest
- Function: Awareness for severe ME/CFS
- Activity: Especially May 12 (ME/CFS International Awareness Day)
- Join by: Sharing stories of bedridden/severely ill people

**#SayNoToABA** (anti-conversion therapy movement)
- Focus: Opposition to Applied Behavior Analysis as compliance-based therapy
- Function: Education, activism against coercive practices
- Activity: Constant (autism community priority)
- Join by: Educating about ABA harms, supporting anti-ABA content

**Condition-specific campaigns:**
- Search "[Your condition] Twitter" for movements specific to your disability

## Accessibility Features & Limitations

### Built-In Accessibility Features

**You can use:**
- Alt text for images (add manually)
- Image descriptions
- Captions for videos (manual, not auto)
- Dark mode
- High contrast mode
- Reduced motion option
- Keyboard shortcuts (press ? for list)
- Accessibility settings (Settings → Accessibility)

**Accessibility settings available:**
- Reduce motion
- Display font size
- Dark mode
- High contrast toggle

### Screen Reader Compatibility

**Desktop:**
- JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver all work reasonably
- Generally screen-reader friendly
- Better on Chrome/Firefox than Safari
- Navigation works well

**Mobile:**
- TalkBack (Android) - okay accessibility
- VoiceOver (iOS) - less accessible
- Mobile app generally less accessible than desktop

### Critical Limitations

**Accessibility team disbanded:**
- Late 2022/early 2023 (under new ownership)
- Means fewer accessibility improvements
- Bug fixes slower
- Features may not consider accessibility

**Twitter Spaces captions removed:**
- February 2023 - real-time captions removed from Spaces (audio feature)
- Major accessibility loss
- Spaces now less accessible for Deaf people
- (This was widely criticized by disability community)

**Some features still not fully accessible:**
- Some buttons unlabeled
- Certain navigation paths not screen-reader friendly
- Nested threads can be confusing
- Mobile app substantial limitations

### Workarounds for Accessibility Issues

**If you use screen reader:**
- Test different browsers
- Desktop better than mobile
- Use keyboard shortcuts
- Report problems to Twitter/X

**If you struggle with text:**
- Desktop only
- Use browser zoom (Ctrl +/-)
- Dark mode may help
- Take breaks often

**If you can't see:**
- Ask community to describe videos/images
- Expect alt text (not guaranteed)
- Report missing alt text
- Use accessibility tools

## Building Disability Communities on Twitter/X

### Starting a Hashtag

**Choose hashtag:**
- Clear, memorable
- Related to disability or cause
- CamelCase (critical!)
- Short enough to type repeatedly
- Not already used for something else

**Define purpose:**
- What's this hashtag for?
- Who should use it?
- What conversations will it hold?
- How will it be different from existing hashtags?

**Promote hashtag:**
- Tweet with hashtag multiple times
- Ask key accounts to use it
- Provide examples of how to use
- Connect to movement or campaign
- Ask community to spread word

**Maintain hashtag:**
- Use consistently
- Promote during relevant times
- Retweet good uses
- Respond to tweets using it

### Hosting Twitter Chats

**Plan the chat:**
- Pick consistent time (weekly, monthly)
- Announce 1-2 weeks ahead
- Create hashtag if not existing
- Choose 3-4 discussion questions
- Write questions in advance
- Schedule promotional tweets

**During the chat:**
- Post Q1 (first question)
- Wait 15-20 minutes
- Participants respond with A1
- Retweet interesting responses
- Post Q2, Q3, Q4 at intervals
- Thank participants at end

**After chat:**
- Share transcript (text version)
- Thank key participants
- Post summary highlights
- Announce next chat

**Example Twitter Chat Schedule:**
- Every Thursday 8pm EST
- 1 hour long
- 4 questions posted 15 min apart
- 50-500 participants typical

### Creating Curated Lists

**Curate disability accounts:**
- Create public list of disability advocates
- Add mix of organizations and individuals
- Add diverse voices (BIPOC, LGBTQ+, Global South)
- Make list public with description
- Share link in bio

**Why create lists:**
- Gives curators visibility
- Helps new people find accounts
- Creates community resource
- Can highlight underrepresented voices

**List ideas:**
- "[Condition] Advocates"
- "Disability Justice Leaders"
- "Black Disabled Creators"
- "Queer Disability Activists"
- "International Disability Organizers"

### Building Engagement

**Share personal stories:**
- Tweet about your disability experience
- Use relevant hashtags
- Include content warnings for heavy topics
- Amplify others through generous retweets

**Amplify others:**
- Retweet people you follow
- Tag relevant accounts
- Share others' resource lists
- Give credit generously

**Provide value:**
- Share resources and tips
- Answer questions in your area of expertise
- Point people toward better resources
- Create threads (multi-part tweets) on topics

**Engage authentically:**
- Reply to others' tweets
- Ask genuine questions
- Build relationships, not just followers
- Participate in conversations, don't just broadcast

**Maintain sustainable engagement:**
- Don't tweet every waking hour
- Take breaks if overwhelmed
- Mute accounts that trigger you
- Remember Twitter isn't your job

## Using Twitter/X Responsibly

### Privacy & Safety

**Remember:**
- Tweets are permanent and searchable (even if deleted, archived)
- Screenshots can be shared anywhere
- Don't assume anonymity
- Real-time can lead to impulsive tweets

**Protect yourself:**
- Don't share identifying details
- Assume anything you tweet is public
- Take breaks from heated debates
- Mute/block liberally
- Report harassment to Twitter

### Setting Healthy Boundaries

**Disability Twitter can be:**
- Emotionally intense
- Focused on problems/crises
- Activist/organizing-heavy
- Triggering if you have trauma

**Set boundaries:**
- Decide how much time you'll spend
- Take media breaks
- Curate your feed (follow positive accounts)
- Mute certain keywords
- Use "I'm taking a break" tweets
- Seek mental health support if needed

### Recognizing When It's Too Much

**Signs to take a break:**
- Exhaustion from debates
- Triggering from discussions
- Anger at "performative" accounts
- Overwhelm from constant crisis
- Anxiety about missing news
- Sleep disruption from tweets

**What to do:**
- Mute app notifications
- Log out
- Unfollow accounts causing stress
- Take 1 day, 1 week, 1 month
- Come back when ready
- Adjust how much you engage

## Specific Disability Community Norms

### Autism Twitter Norms

**#ActuallyAutistic:** Autistic people only
- For autistic people discussing autistic experience
- Non-autistic people: use #AskingAutistics if asking questions
- Identity-first language strongly preferred
- Anti-ABA (conversion therapy) stance strong
- Acceptance narrative ("autism is okay")

**Sensitive topics in autism community:**
- Heavy opposition to "functioning labels" (high/low functioning)
- Rejection of inspiration porn narratives
- Anti-cure discourse
- Emphasis on accommodation, not "fixing"

### ADHD Twitter Norms

**#ADHDTwitter:** Very large, diverse community
- Lots of memes and humor
- Executive dysfunction deeply understood
- Medication discussions open
- Rejection sensitivity dysphoria discussed
- Relatability valued

**Features of ADHD Twitter:**
- "I forgot to eat" understood and laughed through
- Time blindness acknowledged
- Hyperfocus celebrated
- Stimming normal
- Masking acknowledged and validated

### ME/CFS Twitter Norms

**#mecfs / #MillionsMissing:** Severe illness focus
- Heavy emphasis on post-exertional malaise (PEM)
- Opposition to CBT/graded exercise as treatment
- Validation of severe/bedridden patients
- Overlap with Long COVID community
- Emphasis on research funding

### Pain Community Norms

**#ChronicPain / #spooniechat:**
- Medication access advocated for
- Pain legitimacy centered
- Healthcare provider criticism allowed
- Practical tips and support
- Energy/spoon management discussed

## Disability Type Accessibility

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**
Platform heavily text-based (accessible). Videos: request captions in quote tweets if not provided. Spaces (audio feature) now inaccessible after captions removed—major concern for Deaf community.

**Blind/Low Vision:**
Alt text critical (report posts without it). Desktop better than mobile. Links should be descriptive. Screen readers work reasonably on desktop Twitter/X.

**Neurodivergent:**
Short format works well for some ADHD brains. Threads helpful for deep dives. Hyperfocus on special interests natural. CamelCase hashtags essential (otherwise screen readers can't read).

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:**
Real-time disability Twitter can be triggering. Mute/block liberally. Crisis resources available in many disability accounts' bios. Community generally supportive of mental health.

**Hidden/Invisible Disabilities:**
Community validates invisible illness. "You don't look sick" conversations common. Advocacy strong around invisible disability recognition.

**Multiply-Marginalized:**
Seek accounts specific to your intersections: #DisabledWomenOfColor, #BlackDisabledLivesMatter, #DisabledLatinx, etc. These communities exist and center you.

## Global Perspective

### Twitter/X Usage Globally

**Dominance varies:**
- Heavy in Western countries (US, UK, Australia, Canada)
- Growing in Global South
- Competes with WhatsApp, Instagram, TikTok in some regions
- Less dominant in non-English-speaking countries (China, Russia don't have widespread access)

**International disability organizing:**
- Global South disability activists use Twitter
- Search in Spanish, Portuguese, French, etc. for communities
- International disability organizations active
- Mutual aid networks operate globally

**Non-English disability communities:**
- Spanish #DisabledTwitter exists
- Portuguese disability community
- French disability community
- Other language communities smaller but growing
