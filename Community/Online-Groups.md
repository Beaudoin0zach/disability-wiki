# Online Communities: Finding Your People

**What this page covers:** Where to find disability communities online, how to evaluate them, and which platform works best for different needs.

---

## Why Online Community Matters

Online disability communities provide:
- **Connection without gatekeeping** - No requirement to be "sick enough" or have official diagnosis
- **24/7 accessibility** - Support when you need it, regardless of time zone
- **Anonymity when you need it** - Protect your privacy while connecting authentically
- **Diverse representation** - Communities organized by condition, identity, culture, and values
- **Practical knowledge** - Collective expertise from people living your reality
- **Peer support** - From people who actually understand, not professionals explaining
- **Reduced isolation** - Essential for homebound, rural, or geographically isolated disabled people

---

## Choosing Your Platform

Different platforms serve different needs:

| Platform | Best For | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reddit** | Anonymous discussion, large communities, diagnostic help | 24/7, huge reach, highly engaged, searchable threads | Less real-time, sometimes raw tone |
| **Discord** | Real-time connection, voice chat, gaming community | Neurodivergent-friendly, voice options, close-knit communities | Requires account, slightly steep learning curve |
| **Facebook** | Local connection, parent networks, event planning | Largest user base, easy discovery, robust features | Privacy concerns, often older demographic |
| **Twitter/X** | Organizing, public advocacy, diagnostic exchange | Real-time, direct access to officials/media, activist energy | Toxic potential, algorithm changes, accessibility declining |
| **Specialized platforms** | Peer support, one-on-one mentoring | High moderation, trained facilitators, structured support | Smaller communities, may have costs |

## Online Communities by Platform

### Reddit: Large, Anonymous, Searchable

Best for: People wanting deep dives into topics, diagnostic help, 24/7 engagement

**Finding communities:** Use Reddit's search bar with condition name. Explore "Related Communities" in subreddit sidebars. Use Google: `site:reddit.com [condition] support`

**Largest disability subreddits:**
- **r/autism** (500K+) - Autistic people discussing actual autistic experience
- **r/ADHD** (1.2M+) - One of Reddit's largest mental health communities
- **r/disability** (25K+) - Cross-disability discussions
- **r/ChronicPain** - Pain management strategies and resources
- **r/EhlersDanlos** - EDS-specific community
- **r/ME_CFS** - Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome
- **r/POTS** - Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome

**Norms to know:**
- Identity-first language preferred ("autistic person" not "person with autism")
- Strong opposition to ABA therapy in autism communities
- Self-diagnosis welcomed
- Mix of humor, venting, and practical advice
- Moderation varies; some communities highly managed, others more open

**Pros:** Anonymity, searchable content, massive reach, 24/7, highly engaged members  
**Cons:** Less real-time, sometimes chaotic moderation, permanent and searchable posts

**Related page:** [[Reddit Communities]]

---

### Discord: Real-Time, Voice-Enabled, Neurodivergent-Friendly

Best for: Real-time chat, voice participation, gaming communities, close-knit groups

**Why Discord attracts disability communities:** Gaming origins = naturally welcoming to neurodivergent users. Voice channels enable those who struggle with typing. Body doubling features. Highly customizable. Active moderation possible.

**Finding communities:** Use DISBOARD.org (largest Discord directory). Search by tags (autism, ADHD, chronic-illness). Check Reddit communities for linked Discord servers. Search "discord [condition] support"

**Major disability-focused servers:**
- **Stim City** - Autistic adults 21+, special interests, social events
- **Autistic Haven** - All ages, body doubling, accepts self-diagnosis, LGBTQ+ friendly
- **ADHD Dopamine** - 50+ ADHD channels, featured by Discord
- **Chronic Pain Café** - "Like your favorite café on a rainy day"
- **Cane & Disable** - Screen reader compatible, verified access
- **The Nature Preserve** - Cozy, mandatory descriptions, disability-friendly

**Norms to know:**
- Rapid-fire emoji reactions = screen reader hostile (avoid)
- Alt text required for all images
- Content warnings for difficult topics
- Voice channel "body doubling" is common and valued
- Self-diagnosis welcomed
- Often smaller, closer-knit than Reddit

**Pros:** Real-time, voice options, highly moderated, neurodivergent-friendly, gaming integration  
**Cons:** Requires account, less anonymous, mobile less accessible, smaller communities

**Related page:** [[Discord Servers]]

### Facebook: Large Reach, Local Connection, Event Planning

Best for: Local community, parent networks, event planning, discovering new groups

**Why Facebook still matters:** Largest user base globally. Easy group discovery. Event features enable real-world connection. Robust file sharing and documentation. Less technical barrier than other platforms.

**Finding communities:** Use Facebook's group search. Browse "Suggested Groups." Ask in existing communities. Search "[your city] [condition] support group"

**Major disability groups:**
- **Ask Disabled Folks** - Collective wisdom, cross-disability
- **Actually Autistic Adults and Allies** - Peer-led, large
- **Chronic Illnesses** (1.4M+ members) - Huge reach
- **ADHD Support** - Many location-specific variations
- **Disabled Parenting Project** - Parents with disabilities
- **EDS Life Hacks** (1.5M+ members) - Practical strategies

**Norms to know:**
- Mix of parent-led and peer-led groups; check which before joining
- Often larger and more diverse than other platforms
- Mix of venting, resources, and organizational/event focus
- Moderation varies widely
- More demographic diversity (older members, caregivers)

**Pros:** Largest reach, easy discovery, local options, event planning, robust features  
**Cons:** Privacy concerns, algorithm changes, often less disability-affirming culture

**Related page:** [[Facebook Communities]]

### Twitter/X: Organizing, Advocacy, Real-Time Activism

Best for: Public advocacy, organizing, direct access to officials, real-time activism

**Important context:** Accessibility team disbanded 2022-2023. Captions removed from Spaces (Feb 2023). However, disability Twitter remains irreplaceable for real-time organizing and years of built community.

**Finding communities:** Follow disability organizations and hashtags. Use #DisabledTwitter or #DisabilityTwitter. Search #CripTheVote. Join Twitter Chats at scheduled times.

**Major disability hashtags and movements:**
- **#CripTheVote** - Nonpartisan disability political engagement
- **#DisabledandCute** - Celebrates disabled beauty, counters ableism
- **#ActuallyAutistic** - Autistic self-advocates (for autistic people)
- **#ADHDTwitter** - Main ADHD community
- **#MillionsMissing** - ME/CFS awareness for bedridden patients
- **#spooniechat** - Weekly Twitter chat about chronic illness

**Key disability leaders to follow:**
- @autselfadvocacy - Autistic Self Advocacy Network
- @DawnMGibson - Chronic health advocate
- @imani_barbarin - Black disabled activist
- @Keah_Maria - Disability culture, #DisabledandCute creator
- @NeuroRebel - Neurodiversity scholar

**Norms to know:**
- Use CamelCase hashtags (#DisabledTwitter not #disabledtwitter)
- Add alt text to ALL images
- Content warnings for difficult topics
- Real-time organizing happens here
- Community accountability also happens here

**Pros:** Real-time activism, direct access to officials, years of community built, organizing potential  
**Cons:** Accessibility declining, algorithm chaos, performative allyship, harassment risk

**Related page:** [[Twitter/X Communities]]

## Evaluating Online Communities: Red Flags & Green Flags

### Red Flags ⛔

- **Inactive moderation** - No recent mod posts or responses
- **Hostile tone** - Mean-spirited, dismissive of experiences
- **Medical gatekeeping** - "You're not sick enough," questioning diagnosis
- **No accessibility commitment** - Describes images with just "image," no content warnings
- **No confidentiality policy** - Could screenshot and share your story publicly
- **Ableism unmoderated** - Ableist jokes or language not addressed
- **Aggressive recruitment** - "You MUST come," sales pressure

### Green Flags ✓

- **Active moderation visible** - Regular mod posts, quick responses to issues
- **Clear accessibility statement** - Images described, captions available, content warnings
- **Welcoming of self-diagnosis** - Don't require official diagnosis
- **Privacy policy stated** - Clear confidentiality rules
- **Diverse representation** - Mix of disabilities, races, genders, classes
- **Respectful conflict resolution** - Issues addressed privately and fairly
- **Regular engagement** - Discussions, activities, member spotlight

## Community Types Across Platforms

### Peer-Led Communities (By & For Disabled People)

These are run by disabled community members, not professionals. Strengths: authentic experience, disability-affirming culture, quick to adapt to community needs. Variations: Some all-ages, some 18+, some LGBTQ+-focused, some multiply-marginalized focused.

**Examples:** r/ActuallyAutistic, Autistic Haven Discord, Ask Disabled Folks Facebook

### Professionally-Facilitated Communities

Led by therapists, social workers, or healthcare providers. Strengths: structured format, crisis support, professional guidance. Limitations: can be more clinical, may require fees, less peer-led feel.

**Examples:** Many hospital-affiliated groups, some therapy-based Discord servers

### Condition-Specific Communities

Focused on one disability or related cluster. Benefits: deep expertise, highly relevant, understand nuances. Limitations: smaller communities, less intersectional focus.

**Examples:** r/EhlersDanlos, #MillionsMissing (ME/CFS), ADHD Dopamine Discord

### Intersectional Communities

Center specific multiply-marginalized experiences: disabled women of color, disabled LGBTQ+, disabled immigrants, poor disabled people.

**Examples:** @DisabledLatinx, Disabled & Cute, Gimp Girl Community

## How to Join: Step-by-Step

### 1. Find the Community
Use search methods for your platform (see above). Read the description to understand focus. Check member count and recent activity.

### 2. Evaluate Before Joining
Review recent posts/comments for tone and values. Check if they welcome self-diagnosis. Look for accessibility commitment. Verify confidentiality policy.

### 3. Lurk First (If Possible)
Read posts without participating to understand community norms. See how moderators handle conflict. Notice who's represented and whose voices are centered.

### 4. Join and Introduce Yourself
Most communities have an introduction thread or welcome channel. Share what you're comfortable with (can be brief). Don't over-share on first post.

### 5. Observe Norms Before Posting
Each community has unspoken rules. Spend first week just reading. Notice: how people give advice (gentle? prescriptive?), how they discuss diagnosis (respectful? gatekeeping?), tone of humor, how conflict is handled.

### 6. Participate at Your Comfort Level
Start by liking/reacting to posts. Move to commenting on others' posts. Eventually share your own experience. No pressure to be highly active.

### 7. Decide if It's Right for You
After 2-3 visits, reflect: Does this feel safe? Do I feel welcomed? Is my disability type/identity represented? Keep searching if not—many communities exist.

## Starting Your Own Online Community

### When to Start

Only start a new community if:
- You've searched thoroughly and can't find existing group for this specific need
- You have realistic capacity to moderate consistently
- You have co-moderators (not doing it alone)
- You can commit for at least 6 months

### Where to Start

**Easiest:** Facebook group or Discord server (no coding required)  
**Most control:** Your own website or forum  
**Most reach:** Reddit (if subreddit isn't too saturated)  
**Most real-time:** Discord or Twitter

### Key Planning Steps

1. Define specific purpose (what gap exists?)
2. Research thoroughly (what already exists?)
3. Identify your audience (who needs this?)
4. Choose platform
5. Create clear community guidelines
6. Recruit co-moderators
7. Start small (better to grow slowly than fail under pressure)

## Accessibility Notes

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**
Most platforms now support video captions; check settings. Twitter/X captions removed (concerning). Discord servers vary in accessibility. Request interpreter or CART if needed.

**Blind/Low Vision:**
Screen readers work on most platforms (Reddit, Discord, Facebook accessibility good; Twitter/X inconsistent). Alt text is critical—report servers/communities that don't provide it.

**Neurodivergent:**
Discord is often most welcoming. Shorter paragraphs and clear formatting help. Content warnings essential. Body doubling features valuable.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:**
Crisis resources should be easily accessible. Content warnings for trauma, loss, suicide. Supportive community culture helps. Crisis hotline numbers prominently displayed.

**Multiple/Hidden Disabilities:**
Different communities serve different needs at different times. Many people belong to multiple communities. That's normal and healthy.

## Disability Type Accessibility

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**
This page is text-based; most communities listed welcome Deaf people. Verify video caption availability before joining. Request accessibility supports.

**Blind/Low Vision:**
All information here works with screen readers. All linked communities should have alt text for images; report if they don't. Large print guides available for some communities.

**Neurodivergent:**
Long section above? Can jump to specific platform sections. Clear structure with headers for scanning. Bullet points for quick reference.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:**
Your community needs are real and valid. Many communities welcome mental health disability explicitly. Seek out affirming spaces.

**Hidden/Invisible Disabilities:**
Your disability is real even if invisible online. Communities don't require proof. Self-diagnosis welcomed in most spaces.

**Multiply-Marginalized:**
Seek out intersectional communities that center your specific experience, not just disability. Your full identity matters.

## How Organizers Can Use This

**If you're starting a community:**
- Use this page to understand landscape and gaps
- Reference Best Practices for structure
- Review platform-specific pages for nuts-and-bolts
- Don't create just to create; ensure genuine need

**If you're directing people to communities:**
- Share platform-specific pages based on person's needs
- Help people evaluate communities using red/green flags
- Validate that finding the right community takes time
- Create lists of disability-affirming communities for your region/condition

**If you're advocating for better platforms:**
- Document accessibility failures
- Demand better moderation tools
- Push for disability-led design of platform features
- Support accessible alternatives

## Global & Intersectional Perspective

### Western Online Communities
Most established and numerous. English-language dominant. Often reflect Western individualism and medical model. Growing intersectional focus but still primarily white, educated representation.

### Global South & Non-English Communities
Growing but still underrepresented. Many countries have active communities on WhatsApp, Telegram, or regional platforms. International communities on Reddit and Discord expanding. Translation tools help bridge gaps.

### Multiply-Marginalized Spaces
Essential for disabled people of color, disabled immigrants, disabled LGBTQ+ people, disabled poor people. Seek these out if mainstream disability communities don't feel safe. These spaces exist; you may need to search differently (#DisabledLatinx, Black Disabled Lives Matter, etc.).
