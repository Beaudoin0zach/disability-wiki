## WHAT IS DISABILITY MENTORSHIP?

Disability mentorship involves a one-on-one connection between:
- **Mentor:** More experienced in navigating disability (not necessarily older)
- **Mentee:** Seeking guidance and learning from lived experience
- **Relationship:** Ongoing over weeks, months, or even years
- **Approach:** Peer-to-peer (disabled to disabled), lived experience prioritized
- **Format:** Can be formal programs or informal organic relationships

---

## HOW MENTORSHIP DIFFERS FROM OTHER SUPPORT

**Mentorship vs. Support Groups:**
- Mentorship: One-on-one personalized relationship
- Support Groups: Group dynamics, shared experience with multiple people

**Mentorship vs. Therapy:**
- Mentorship: Peer wisdom and lived experience
- Therapy: Professional training and clinical expertise

**Mentorship vs. Coaching:**
- Mentorship: Often unpaid, relationship-based, long-term
- Coaching: Often paid professional service, goal-focused

**Mentorship vs. Sponsorship:**
- Mentorship: Developmental relationship, skill-building
- Sponsorship: Advocacy on your behalf, career advancement

## TYPES OF DISABILITY MENTORSHIP

### By Focus

**Diagnosis-specific mentorship:**
- Experienced person with same/similar condition guides newly diagnosed person
- Focus: Navigating diagnosis, accessing resources, connecting with community
- Example: "I was diagnosed with ME/CFS 10 years ago; let me share what I learned"
- Best for: People newly diagnosed; need condition-specific knowledge

**Community integration mentorship:**
- Helps disabled person navigate systems and services
- Focus: Accessing benefits, finding resources, understanding systems
- Example: Mentor helps with SSDI/SSI application and benefits navigation
- Best for: People new to disability systems; navigating new country/community

**Life stage mentorship:**
- Guides through specific transition (school, work, adulthood, aging)
- Focus: Preparation, navigation, problem-solving for that life stage
- Example: High school student with disability mentored through college transition
- Best for: People facing major transitions; need developmental guidance

**Work/employment mentorship:**
- Supports workplace navigation, disclosure, accommodations
- Focus: Career development, workplace access, disability-related workplace issues
- Example: Mentor helps with job search, interview prep, accommodation requests
- Best for: People entering/re-entering workforce; navigating disclosure

**Parenting mentorship:**
- Parent with disability mentoring another parent with disability
- Focus: Balancing parenting and disability management, accessing support
- Example: Mentor shares strategies for managing flare-ups while parenting
- Best for: Parents with disability

**Advocacy & organizing mentorship:**
- Experienced disability advocate mentors emerging leader
- Focus: Skills development, systems understanding, movement participation
- Example: Mentor teaches advocacy skills and connects to movement
- Best for: People interested in disability justice work

**Leadership & professional mentorship:**
- Disabled professionals mentoring emerging leaders
- Focus: Career navigation, professional development with disability
- Example: Disabled lawyer mentoring law student with disability
- Best for: People in professional fields; career advancement

## GETTING MATCHED WITH A MENTOR

### Formal Program Process

Most established programs follow similar steps:

**1. Application/Registration**
- Provide basic information about your interests and needs
- Explain what you hope to gain from mentorship
- Share your availability and preferences (format, frequency, topics)
- Sign confidentiality agreement
- Sometimes: Submit essays or have interviews

**2. Matching Process**
- Program staff review applications
- Match based on: condition, experience, goals, personality fit, location (if in-person)
- Facilitate introduction
- Both parties agree to match (can decline and request new match)

**3. Onboarding**
- Orientation for mentee and mentor
- Goal-setting conversation
- Establish communication preferences
- Set expectations and frequency
- Clarify roles and boundaries

**4. Active Mentoring Period**
- Regular check-ins (weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly)
- Track goal progression
- Provide support and guidance
- Make adjustments as needed
- Mentor provides resources, listening, problem-solving

**5. Conclusion**
- Natural ending when goals achieved
- Transition planning if appropriate
- Option to continue or conclude relationship
- Some programs offer alumni connection

---

### Informal Matching

**Starting organic mentorship:**

**1. Identify potential mentor**
- Someone with experience you need
- Someone who demonstrates values you respect
- Someone accessible and willing to help
- Not necessarily "official" mentor role

**2. Respectful approach**
- Reach out explaining interest in their perspective
- Be specific about what you want to learn
- Make it easy to say no
- Example: "I've been following your work and really respect your approach. Would you ever be open to an informal chat about navigating [topic]?"

**3. Establish relationship**
- Start with one meeting or conversation
- See if chemistry exists
- Discuss frequency and format
- Be clear about expectations and time commitment
- Example: "Would you be open to a monthly coffee/call?"

**4. Ongoing connection**
- Regular but flexible contact
- Respect for mentor's capacity
- Gratitude and reciprocity
- Natural evolution of relationship

## WHAT GOOD MENTORS LOOK LIKE

### Essential Qualities

**Experience and knowledge:**
- Lived experience with disability or similar condition
- Time (usually 2+ years) navigating what you're facing
- Practical knowledge to share
- Access to useful resources
- NOT: Need to be expert; lived experience IS the expertise

**Character:**
- Respectful and non-judgmental
- Reliable and responsive
- Humble (willing to admit what they don't know)
- Advocacy-oriented toward disabled people
- Ethical boundaries
- Recognizes their own limitations

**Communication:**
- Good listening skills
- Asks questions rather than just giving advice
- Explains things clearly
- Responsive to messages and communication
- Flexible communication style
- Accessible (can communicate in your preferred way)

**Wisdom:**
- Recognizes diversity of disability experience
- Doesn't assume their way is the only way
- Encourages mentee to make own decisions
- Knows limits of their expertise
- Connects to resources when needed
- Shares mistakes and learning, not just successes

---

### Red Flags in Mentors 🚩

Avoid mentors who:
- Push specific treatments or products
- Make medical claims or offer medical advice
- Dismiss your experience ("You're not disabled enough")
- Are unavailable or unreliable
- Create boundary violations (too personal, inappropriate)
- Gatekeep knowledge
- Claim to have "the answer" (one right way)
- Exploit (asking for money, using for labor)
- Dismiss self-diagnosis or diagnosis journey
- Promote harmful approaches
- Use mentorship to make themselves look good
- Pressure you to adopt their lifestyle/choices

## BEING A MENTOR

### Why Become a Mentor

**Benefits:**
- Help others navigate what you've learned
- Build leadership skills
- Strengthen your own disability knowledge
- Create meaningful connections
- Give back to disability community
- Feel valued for lived expertise
- Build social connections
- Contribute to disability justice work

**Common concerns:**
- "I'm not expert enough" — Lived experience IS enough
- "I might hurt someone" — Having boundaries and knowing limits helps
- "I don't have time" — Flexible mentorship accommodates disabilities
- "I'm not sure I'm good at it" — Most people are better than they think
- "I have too much going on" — You can set realistic boundaries

---

### Becoming a Mentor

**Formal programs:**
- Many organizations have mentor training
- Typically 2-4 hours of orientation
- Cover: listening skills, boundaries, confidentiality
- Clarify mentor role and responsibilities
- Connect to mentor support network
- Some require ongoing training

**Starting informally:**
- Reflect on what you've learned
- Consider what others ask you about
- Identify your comfort areas
- Establish boundaries proactively
- Know when to say no
- Be willing to say "I don't know"
- Start small (one mentee)

---

### Good Mentorship Practices

**Setting boundaries:**
- Be clear about availability
- Decide on communication method and frequency
- Explain what's not your role (therapy, medical advice)
- Permission to take breaks
- Clear exit strategy if needed
- Self-care for mentor burnout prevention
- Example: "I'm available for text check-ins Sundays and Wednesdays; emergencies call crisis line"

**Communication best practices:**
- Active listening (hearing not just waiting to talk)
- Asking questions to help mentee problem-solve
- Sharing relevant experiences without over-focusing on yourself
- Respecting confidentiality absolutely
- Being accessible (various communication styles)
- Following up consistently
- Honoring "crip time" (disability time management)

**Supporting without fixing:**
- Recognize mentee's own expertise about their body and life
- Provide information, not directives
- Encourage critical thinking
- Support decision-making without judgment
- Celebrate mentee's progress
- Hold space for difficult emotions
- Avoid "savior" mindset

**Knowing your limits:**
- Crisis vs. mentorship (refer crisis to professionals)
- Medical advice vs. lived experience sharing
- Your expertise vs. other domains
- Your emotional capacity
- When to refer to other resources
- Permission to end mentorship if it's not working
- Self-care when you're struggling

## PEER SPECIALIST & MENTOR TRAINING

### What is Peer Specialist Training?

Peer specialists are formally trained disabled people who provide support:
- Unlike informal mentors: Complete formal training programs
- Get paid (sometimes) through organizations
- Work with agencies or organizations
- May meet licensing or certification requirements (varies by state)
- Use evidence-based peer support approaches

---

### Examples

**Mental Health Peer Specialists (MHPS):**
- Support people with mental illness
- Complete 40-75 hours training (varies by state)
- Often become certified
- Work in mental health centers, hospitals, community organizations

**Peer Recovery Specialists:**
- Focus on substance use recovery
- Complete formal training and certification
- Work in treatment centers and community programs

**Peer Advocates:**
- Advocate for disabled people
- Receive training in advocacy and systems navigation
- Work with disability organizations

**Certified Disability Specialist:**
- Various certifications available
- Usually require 100+ hours training
- May require exam and continuing education
- Specific to region/country

---

### Training Opportunities

**National programs:**
- Mental Health America — MHPS training directory
- SAMHSA — peer support training resources
- NAMI — Peer Specialist Certificate training
- Disability advocacy organizations — mentor training

**Finding training in your area:**
- Contact local mental health agencies
- Ask disability organizations
- Search state licensing boards
- University disability programs
- Community colleges

## GLOBAL PERSPECTIVE: MENTORSHIP WORLDWIDE

Mentorship looks different globally:

**Wealthy Western countries:**
- Formal mentorship programs abundant
- Professional facilitators common
- Clear career/professional development path
- Often structured and documented

**Middle-income countries:**
- Mix of formal and informal mentorship
- Often through disability organizations
- Family-based mentorship models also important
- Growing peer support infrastructure

**Lower-resource countries:**
- Informal mentorship primary
- Community-based learning
- Family caregiving as mentorship model
- Elder knowledge important
- Collective vs. individual focus

**Global South disability mentorship:**
- Often intertwined with economic survival
- Family structures primary support
- Community collective responsibility
- Different definition of "disability expertise"

## INTERSECTIONALITY IN MENTORSHIP

Multiply-marginalized disabled people benefit from:
- Mentors who share their identities (race, gender, sexuality, class, etc.)
- Understanding of how multiple oppressions intersect
- Knowledge of specific barriers faced
- Modeling of thriving as multiply-marginalized disabled person
- Connection to movements beyond disability

**If mentor doesn't share your identity:**
- Can still mentor you
- But may have gaps in understanding
- Important they acknowledge this
- Open to learning from you about your experience
- Committed to supporting your full self

## STARTING A MENTORSHIP PROGRAM

If your organization or community wants to start mentorship:

**Planning:**
1. Assess need (do disabled people in your community want this?)
2. Define scope (what type of mentorship? for whom?)
3. Recruit mentors (start with 3-5 committed people)
4. Design matching process (how will you pair people?)
5. Create training for mentors (what do they need to know?)
6. Set clear guidelines (confidentiality, boundaries, contact frequency)
7. Plan support for mentors (they need support too)
8. Evaluate success (are people getting what they need?)

**Resources:**
- Check if existing organizations in your area offer training
- Disability mentorship organizations may have toolkits
- This wiki's "Community & Advocacy" section has organizational resources

## QUALITIES OF GOOD MENTORSHIP RELATIONSHIPS

**For mentee:**
- Feel heard and understood
- Learning and growing
- Safe to share struggles and successes
- Mentor checks in regularly
- Mentor respects your autonomy
- Building confidence

**For mentor:**
- Feel valued and appreciated
- Mentee taking responsibility for their growth
- Clear boundaries being respected
- Energy given and received
- Making a difference visible
- Enjoying the relationship

**For both:**
- Mutual respect
- Reliable communication
- Authenticity and trust
- Alignment of values
- Celebration of progress
- Space for mistakes and learning

---

## ENDING MENTORSHIP RELATIONSHIPS

**Natural endings:**
- Goals achieved
- Life circumstances changed
- Time to move on
- Moving to different geographic area
- Life stage ended (graduation, job found, etc.)

**Ungraceful endings:**
- Incompatibility discovered
- Trust broken
- Boundaries violated
- No longer serving either person

**Parting well:**
- Acknowledge what you learned from each other
- Don't ghost (communicate)
- Leave door open if appropriate
- Gratitude
- Maybe occasional check-ins (if both want)
