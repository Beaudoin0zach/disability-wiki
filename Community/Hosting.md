# Hosting Accessible Events for Disabled People

**What this page covers:** Planning and executing accessible disability events—whether small support group meetings, conferences, workshops, social gatherings, or fundraisers. Focus is on removing barriers so disabled people can actually participate.

**Quick principle:** Accessibility isn't an afterthought or special accommodation. It's built into planning from day one. When you do this, events work better for everyone.

---

## Why Accessibility Matters for Disability Events

**The problem:** Disability events often fail disabled people through inaccessible venues, no captions, inflexible schedules, no childcare, exhausting agendas, and lack of accommodation planning. This is discrimination by the community meant to serve disabled people.

**The goal:** Events that center disabled access from the beginning. Where disabled people can actually show up, participate, and be safe.

**Who this serves:**
- People with mobility disabilities
- Deaf and hard of hearing people
- Blind and low vision people
- Neurodivergent people
- People with pain or fatigue
- People with mental health disabilities
- Multiply-marginalized people
- Everyone benefits from accessibility

---

## Before You Plan: Core Accessibility Principles

### Universal Design for Events

**Universal Design means:** Designing for the broadest range of people from the start. Not "regular event + accommodations for disabled people." Instead: "event built for access from conception."

**Examples:**
- Captioning benefits: Deaf people, people in noisy environments, people processing auditory information
- Multiple ways to participate: In-person, virtual, asynchronous benefits people with mobility issues, energy limitations, childcare needs, geographic distance
- Scent-free: Benefits asthmatics, people with fragrance sensitivity, people with MCAS
- Flexible seating: Benefits people with pain, mobility disability, sensory issues, neurodivergent needs
- Clear communication: Benefits neurodivergent people, people with processing differences, people with hearing loss, everyone

**Result:** Accessibility benefits disabled AND non-disabled people. Good design is just good design.

### Disability Justice Principles Applied to Events

**Leadership:** Disabled people lead event planning and execution, not just attend.

**Intentionality:** Accessibility is planned, not reactive. Budgeted, staffed, prioritized.

**Accountability:** Clear communication about accessibility. Follow through. Take feedback seriously.

**Transformation:** Events enable disabled people to lead, connect, and organize—not just consume.

## Physical Event Planning: The Checklist

### 6-8 Weeks Before Event

**Venue selection—accessibility is non-negotiable:**

- [ ] Physically accessible entrance (no steps, or ramp available)
- [ ] Accessible parking nearby (free or validated if possible)
- [ ] Street parking accessible to venue
- [ ] Accessible bathroom on same level as event
- [ ] Elevator access if multi-floor
- [ ] Temperature control possible (not locked in)
- [ ] Flexible restroom access (not locked/limited hours)
- [ ] Service animals explicitly welcomed
- [ ] Scent-free (no fragrances in building, ask tenants if needed)
- [ ] Quiet space available for breaks/sensory regulation
- [ ] Clear pathways (36" minimum width for wheelchairs)
- [ ] Accessible parking clearly marked
- [ ] Accessible entrance clearly marked and easy to find

**Communicate accessibility in event details:**

- [ ] Write clear accessibility statement (see template below)
- [ ] List all accessibility features
- [ ] Explain how to request accommodations
- [ ] Provide advance materials/schedule
- [ ] Give contact info for accessibility questions
- [ ] Post on multiple platforms (not just one channel)
- [ ] Repeat accessibility info regularly (people miss it)

**Arrange logistics:**

- [ ] Confirm accessible transportation options available
- [ ] Plan for parking assistance if needed (staff to help with accessible parking)
- [ ] Arrange childcare (if event allows children, need accessible childcare)
- [ ] Plan food (see dietary/allergy section below)
- [ ] Confirm scent-free throughout (check with janitor, building management)
- [ ] Plan layout for accessibility (wide aisles, accessible seating throughout)

### 4 Weeks Before Event

**Communication accessibility:**

- [ ] Arrange captioning/CART for spoken content
- [ ] Arrange ASL interpreters (if applicable)
- [ ] Plan for large print materials
- [ ] Plan for digital materials (screen-reader compatible)
- [ ] Arrange plain language version of materials
- [ ] Test all materials for accessibility

**Sensory accommodation:**

- [ ] Plan lighting (no strobe/flashing, dimmable if possible)
- [ ] Plan sound (avoid high-pitched feedback, keep under 85dB if possible)
- [ ] Plan fragrance policy enforcement
- [ ] Arrange quiet/rest space
- [ ] Plan for service animal relief areas

**Food accessibility:**

- [ ] Ask for dietary restrictions/allergies
- [ ] Plan allergen-free options
- [ ] Label all food clearly (ingredients, allergens)
- [ ] Plan vegetarian/vegan options (common with disability)
- [ ] Arrange separate serving areas if cross-contamination risk
- [ ] Provide eating utensils, napkins, trash access

**Staffing:**

- [ ] Recruit accessibility staff (not as add-on job)
- [ ] Ensure disabled people on accessibility team
- [ ] Brief staff on accommodation requests
- [ ] Train staff on disability etiquette
- [ ] Assign bathroom/rest space monitoring
- [ ] Assign parking/entrance support if needed

### 2 Weeks Before Event

**Finalize accessibility requests:**

- [ ] Follow up with people requesting accommodations
- [ ] Confirm all services booked (interpreters, captioners, etc.)
- [ ] Confirm dietary restrictions/allergies
- [ ] Finalize materials in alternate formats
- [ ] Confirm staff understanding of requests

**Promote accessibility:**

- [ ] Send final reminder about accessibility features
- [ ] Give deadline for accommodation requests (if not already passed)
- [ ] Provide final schedule with break times
- [ ] Confirm transportation info shared widely
- [ ] Share accessibility statement again

**Test accessibility:**

- [ ] Walk venue with wheelchair (or simulate: pushable chair)
- [ ] Check all pathways for obstacles
- [ ] Confirm parking accessible
- [ ] Confirm bathrooms accessible
- [ ] Test microphone and sound system
- [ ] Test captioning system
- [ ] Confirm quiet space is usable

### Day Before/Day Of Event

**Final setup:**

- [ ] Test all technology (mics, captioning, video if virtual)
- [ ] Set up accessible parking clearly marked
- [ ] Confirm accessible entrance clearly marked
- [ ] Set up quiet/rest space with supplies (water, seating, etc.)
- [ ] Set up bathroom amenities (grab bars accessible, soap/towels accessible)
- [ ] Set up seating for various accessibility needs
- [ ] Set up food with clear labels
- [ ] Brief staff on final plan
- [ ] Confirm interpreters/captioners present

**During event:**

- [ ] Have staff greet people at accessible entrance
- [ ] Offer to assist with parking if needed
- [ ] Direct to accessible bathrooms clearly
- [ ] Remind about break times and quiet space
- [ ] Monitor accessibility throughout
- [ ] Remain flexible (issues come up, adapt)
- [ ] Thank accessibility team/volunteers

## Physical Accessibility: Detailed Guidance

### Parking & Arrival

**What disabled people need:**
- Accessible parking spaces (van-accessible if possible)
- Close to building entrance
- Drop-off area (people too fatigued/in pain to walk from regular parking)
- Staff to help with parking if needed
- Clear signage
- Easy-to-find entrance

**How to provide:**
- Reserve 2-3 accessible spaces (more for large events)
- Mark clearly with painted spaces + signage
- Arrange drop-off area near accessible entrance
- Have staff available to help people park/find parking
- Provide written directions to accessible parking
- QR code linking to parking info

### Seating & Standing

**What disabled people need:**
- Seating throughout (not just at tables for speakers)
- Variety: chairs with arms, without arms, high enough to stand from, low cushioned for people with pain
- Standing room (some people can't sit long)
- Standing room with back support
- Spaces for wheelchairs/mobility devices (not segregated)
- Easy access to seating (not blocked by others)

**How to provide:**
- Arrange chairs in various configurations
- Have 20-30% extra seating available
- Scatter mobility device spaces throughout (not confined to back)
- Avoid theater-style seating (hard for disabled people to leave if needed)
- Allow people to move around easily
- Provide cushions, blankets if people want them

### Bathrooms

**What disabled people need:**
- Accessible (grab bars, adequate space for wheelchairs, accessible sinks)
- Readily available (not locked, not single-occupancy with long waits)
- Sufficient quantity (disabled people may need bathroom more frequently)
- Clear signage
- Easy-to-find
- Stocked (toilet paper, soap, towels)
- Gender-neutral option if possible

**How to provide:**
- Choose venue with accessible bathrooms on event floor
- Ensure bathrooms clearly marked
- Stock with supplies regularly
- Have staff available to assist if needed
- Consider adding porta-potty if outdoor event (ensure accessible)
- Allow flexible bathroom access (don't restrict to scheduled breaks)

### Temperature & Environment

**What disabled people need:**
- Controlled temperature (not too hot or cold)
- Air circulation (some people have breathing issues)
- Humidity controlled (can affect mobility)
- Not too loud
- Not too bright (no strobe/flashing)
- Smell-free (scent-free)
- Clear pathways
- Exits clearly marked

**How to provide:**
- Choose venue with temperature control
- Monitor temperature during event, adjust as needed
- Ensure adequate ventilation
- Keep sound level reasonable (use mics to avoid people shouting)
- Use regular lighting (avoid strobe/flashing)
- Enforce scent-free policy strictly
- Mark emergency exits clearly
- Keep pathways clear of obstacles

### Service Animals

**Policy:**
- Service animals always welcome (no question)
- Clarify: service animals, not support animals or pets
- Provide relief areas
- Keep other animals away from service animals
- Don't pet or distract service animals

**Implementation:**
- Post clear policy about service animals welcome
- Have outdoor relief area marked/accessible
- Assign staff to manage bathroom areas for service animal relief
- Educate other attendees (not to pet service animals)
- Keep separate area from regular bathroom if possible

## Communication Accessibility: Detailed Guidance

### Captioning & Interpreters

**When needed:**
- Whenever someone is speaking
- Video content
- Audio materials
- Pre-recorded content

**Options:**
- **CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation):** Professional real-time captioning. Typed by professional captioner. Most accessible for Deaf people. Expensive ($100-300+/hour).
- **ASL Interpreters:** Professional sign language interpreters. Essential for Deaf people who use ASL. Budget $50-100/hour, minimum 2 hours. Book early.
- **Auto-captions:** YouTube, Zoom, others offer auto-captions. Free but inaccurate. Better than nothing, not ideal.
- **Live captions:** Some platforms have built-in live captioning. Check accuracy.
- **Transcripts:** Written version of spoken content. Important as backup.

**How to arrange:**
- Book professional services 4-6 weeks in advance (hard to find)
- Provide speaker materials in advance (helps accuracy)
- Test technology beforehand
- Have backup plan (second captioner, iPad with backup captions)
- Display captions clearly (large screen, good contrast)
- Provide transcript after event

**Cost in budget:**
- CART: $100-300/hour
- ASL Interpreter: $50-100/hour (minimum 2 hours)
- Include in event budget from start (not surprise cost)

### Large Print & Digital Materials

**What disabled people need:**
- Large print (18pt minimum, better 20-24pt)
- High contrast (dark text on light background or light on dark)
- Simple fonts (sans-serif, not decorative)
- Space between lines (not cramped)
- Digital versions (can be enlarged by user)
- Screen-reader compatible
- Simple language (avoid jargon)

**How to provide:**
- Create materials in 20pt or larger
- Use sans-serif fonts (Arial, Verdana, not Comic Sans)
- Dark text on white or light background
- PDF with proper accessibility tags (searchable, selectable text)
- Word document that can be modified
- HTML version if possible
- Provide in advance (people need time to read)

### Plain Language

**What disabled people need:**
- Short sentences (under 20 words)
- Short paragraphs (3-5 sentences max)
- Common words (avoid jargon)
- Clear structure (headers, lists)
- Active voice ("We will..." not "It will be...")
- Clear purpose stated at start

**How to provide:**
- Write materials in plain language
- Have someone read for clarity (not just writer)
- Test with people unfamiliar with topic
- Revise as needed
- Provide glossary if jargon needed
- Use examples

## Dietary & Food Accessibility

### Common Dietary Restrictions & Disabilities

**Celiac Disease/Gluten sensitivity:** No gluten (wheat, barley, rye)

**Crohn's/IBS:** Often need low-fiber, low-fat, safe foods

**Diabetes:** Need low-sugar or sugar-free options

**Autism:** Often texture-based preferences (soft vs. crunchy, specific foods)

**Anxiety/OCD:** May need separate serving areas (contamination fears), specific preparation info

**Gastroparesis:** Need soft, easy-to-digest foods

**Allergies:** Common food allergies (peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, etc.)

**Vegetarian/vegan:** Often health-based (kidney disease, etc.) or ethical choice

**FODMAP:** Low fermentation diet for IBS

**Nut-free environments:** Critical for severe allergies

### Accessible Food Service

**How to ask:**
- Include dietary restrictions question in registration
- Ask about allergies (separate from preferences)
- Ask about specific needs
- Provide contact for questions about food

**How to provide:**
- Have vegetarian/vegan options (very common)
- Have gluten-free options (increasingly necessary)
- Label all food clearly (ingredients, allergens)
- Separate allergen foods (prevent cross-contamination)
- Provide individual snacks/packaged items (safe if ingredients unknown)
- Communicate about food before event
- Ask before event about dietary needs you're unsure of

**During event:**
- Keep food labels visible
- Monitor for cross-contamination
- Be flexible (people's needs change)
- Have backup snacks available
- Don't pressure people to eat

## Virtual & Hybrid Events: Accessibility

### Virtual Event Setup

**Accessibility features to use:**
- Live captions (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet all have options)
- Chat for questions/comments
- Breakout rooms for small group discussion
- Q&A feature
- Polls for engagement
- Screen share with clear slides
- Audio description option
- Recording for those who can't attend live

**Testing:**
- Test with screen reader (NVDA, etc.)
- Test with keyboard navigation only
- Test video quality
- Test audio quality
- Test captions work
- Test chat is accessible
- Have backup plan if tech fails

### Hybrid Best Practices

**The problem:** Hybrid events often fail virtual attendees (they're afterthought).

**Solutions:**
- Equal video quality for virtual and in-person
- Virtual attendees visible to in-person (not ignored)
- Captions benefit both
- Chat monitored for both groups
- Q&A allows virtual questions
- Recording available for those who can't attend live
- Virtual attendees not excluded from activities

**Staffing:**
- Tech person monitoring virtual platform
- Person reading chat questions for in-person
- Both groups feel included, not separated

## Accessibility Statement Template

```
Accessibility is Important to Us

This event is designed to be accessible to disabled people. 
We want everyone to participate fully. Here's what we offer:

PHYSICAL ACCESSIBILITY
- Wheelchair accessible entrance at [location]
- Accessible parking [# spaces] at [location]
- Accessible restrooms on [floor/location]
- Elevators available
- Scent-free building (we ask all attendees to respect this)
- Temperature controlled
- Service animals welcome

COMMUNICATION ACCESSIBILITY
- Live captions for all spoken content
- ASL interpreter [if applicable]
- Large print materials available
- Digital materials screen-reader compatible
- Plain language used throughout

DIETARY ACCESSIBILITY
- Vegetarian/vegan options
- Gluten-free options
- All food labeled with ingredients/allergens
- Please indicate dietary needs at registration

FLEXIBLE PARTICIPATION
- Virtual attendance option [if applicable]
- Quiet/rest space available
- Flexible break schedule
- No requirement to participate in all activities
- Recording available [if applicable]

REQUESTING ACCOMMODATIONS
If you need additional accommodations, please contact [name] at [email] 
by [date]. We can usually accommodate requests with advance notice.

Questions about accessibility? Contact [name] at [email] or [phone].

We're committed to continuous improvement. Please share feedback 
about what worked and what we can do better.
```

## Common Accessibility Requests & How to Meet Them

| Request | What They Need | How to Provide |
|---------|---|---|
| ASL Interpreter | Interpreter for event | Book 4-6 weeks early, provide speaker materials |
| CART/Captions | Real-time written captions | Use Zoom captions, hire CART service, or both |
| Large print materials | Materials in 18-24pt font | Create in larger font, provide before event |
| Audio description | Description of visual content | Have someone describe images/slides during event |
| Quiet/rest space | Low-stimulation area to take breaks | Designate quiet room, provide water, seating |
| Scent-free environment | No fragrances/strong smells | Enforce policy, alert building management |
| Flexible schedule | Breaks, no rushed pacing | Build in break time, don't over-schedule |
| Recording | Video/audio recording for later | Record event, share with attendees |
| Childcare | Care for children during event | Hire caregivers, provide accessible childcare space |
| Parking assistance | Help finding/accessing parking | Have staff available to help |
| Dietary accommodations | Special food for medical needs | Ask in advance, provide food labeled |
| Medication breaks | Time/space for medication management | Allow bathroom/break access anytime |
| Standing/moving | Ability to stand, move around | Avoid theater seating, allow flexible movement |

## Disability Type Accessibility

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**
- Captions essential (not optional)
- ASL interpreter or CART required
- Visual alerts (lights not just sounds)
- Chat open during virtual events
- Provide materials in written form

**Blind/Low Vision:**
- Large print materials
- Screen-reader compatible digital materials
- Audio description of visual content
- Clear verbal directions
- Adequate lighting
- High contrast (dark on light or light on dark)

**Neurodivergent (ADHD, Autism, Learning Disabilities):**
- Clear, simple language
- Advance materials for preparation
- Breaks built into schedule
- Flexible participation options
- Sensory management (low noise, low lights available)
- Clear structure and predictability
- Quiet space for regulation

**Mobility/Physical Disabilities:**
- Completely accessible venue (not just "mostly")
- Accessible parking close to entrance
- Flexible seating options
- Accessible bathrooms
- Climate controlled
- Elevators if multi-floor

**Chronic Pain/Fatigue:**
- Flexible participation (can attend part of event)
- Seating throughout (not standing-only)
- Adequate breaks
- Scent-free
- Climate controlled
- No extreme physical demands

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:**
- Clear schedule provided in advance
- Quiet/safe space available
- Crisis resources provided
- Trauma-informed moderation
- Trigger warnings for difficult topics
- Supportive community culture

**Multiply-Marginalized:**
- Representation in leadership/planning
- Language/translation services as needed
- Recognition of intersecting barriers
- Safety planning (especially for people facing harassment)
- Explicit inclusion statements

## Accessibility on a Budget

**Free/low-cost options:**
- Zoom auto-captions (free but basic)
- Large print materials (your printer)
- Accessible venues (libraries, community centers, free spaces)
- Volunteer ASL interpreters (hard to find but check disability orgs)
- Built-in accessibility (multiple ways to participate costs nothing)

**Worth the investment:**
- Professional captions ($100-300/hour) - enables Deaf participation
- ASL interpreter ($50-100/hour) - essential if Deaf attendees
- Accessible venue ($0-500) - foundation for everything

**Ways to fund:**
- Include accessibility in event budget from start
- Seek grants specifically for accessibility
- Ask for donations specifically for accessibility
- Partner with organizations for shared resources

**No-cost accessibility:**
- Clear communication
- Simple language
- Multiple formats (digital + print + verbal)
- Breaks built in
- Flexible participation
- Rest space (borrow a room)
- Advance materials (helps everyone prepare)

## After Event: Feedback & Improvement

**What to ask:**
- Was this event accessible to you?
- What worked well?
- What was difficult?
- What would make it more accessible?
- Would you attend again?
- How can we improve?

**How to ask:**
- Anonymous survey (people more honest)
- 1:1 conversations (personal follow-up)
- Open feedback channel (ongoing)
- Specific accessibility questions (not just general)

**How to use feedback:**
- Read all feedback
- Look for patterns (not just outliers)
- Discuss as planning team
- Make changes based on feedback
- Report back what changed ("You said X, we're now doing Y")
- Follow up with people who had problems

## Disability Type Accessibility

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**
All spoken content needs captions or interpreters. Not optional. Critical for Deaf participation.

**Blind/Low Vision:**
Materials in large print and digital formats. Audio description of visual content. High contrast. Accessible routes.

**Neurodivergent:**
Clear communication, advance materials, breaks, flexible structure, sensory management (quiet spaces, lower lights available).

**Mobility/Physical Disabilities:**
Completely accessible venue, parking, bathrooms, flexible seating, climate control.

**Chronic Pain/Fatigue:**
Flexible participation, adequate seating, breaks, accessible, no extreme demands.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:**
Safe space, trauma-informed, crisis resources, triggers warned in advance.

**Multiply-Marginalized:**
Leadership representation, language/translation, safety planning, explicit inclusion.

## Global Perspective

### Western Event Culture Issues

Events often designed for non-disabled people without consultation. Accessibility seen as add-on. Assumptions about participation (must attend in-person, all day, etc.). Limited funding for accessibility. Low awareness of disability needs.

### Global South & Informal Economy

Many disabled people in informal economy can't take time off. Events often require travel to capital/city. Virtual options enable broader participation. Accessibility often seen as Western luxury (it's not—it's basic access).

### Cultural Considerations

Different cultures have different approaches to disability, family involvement, time (punctuality vs. flexibility), communication styles. Accessible events accommodate diversity of cultural approaches.
