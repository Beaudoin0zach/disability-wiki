# LOCAL SUPPORT GROUPS

Local support groups connect disabled people in your geographic area—for peer support, resource sharing, and community building. This section helps you find, evaluate, and create in-person groups.

## WHY IN-PERSON GROUPS MATTER

Face-to-face connection provides something online can't:

**Physical presence:**
- Hugs and human contact (physical connection healing for many)
- Body language and non-verbal communication
- Shared meals or activities
- Real-time energy and connection
- Can bring snacks, comfort items
- Accessibility accommodations visible and specific

**Local resource sharing:**
- Share local doctors/providers recommendations
- Exchange information about local programs
- Organize transportation together
- Share practical tips for local barriers
- Connect to local services

**Crisis support with a face:**
- Crisis support from someone you see regularly
- Phone tree or personal check-ins
- Can bring food/practical help
- Local resources for immediate help
- Deeper accountability

**Community organizing potential:**
- Plan local advocacy together
- Attend appointments together
- Organize accessible events
- Build relationships that continue outside group
- Stronger local movement-building

**For people uncomfortable online:**
- No algorithm or posts to screenshot
- Can trust people you've met
- No social media anxiety
- Easier to understand confidentiality (you know who's in room)
- Preferred by many older disabled people

## FINDING IN-PERSON GROUPS

### Where to Search

**Organizations:**
1. **Independent Living Centers (ILCs)**
   - Federally funded disability organizations
   - Find them: ilru.org or your state's directory
   - Often host or know about local groups
   - Free information and referrals

2. **Disability-specific organizations**
   - Cerebral Palsy Association, Spina Bifida Association, etc.
   - Most have local chapters
   - Search organization website for "chapters" or "events"
   - Call local office directly (staff often know groups)

3. **Community mental health centers**
   - Often host support groups for various conditions
   - Call mental health department
   - Ask specifically about disability groups

4. **Hospitals and medical centers**
   - Patient education departments
   - Social work departments
   - Often coordinate support groups
   - Search hospital website or call

5. **Public libraries**
   - Often list community groups
   - May host groups in meeting rooms
   - Librarians can direct you

6. **Community centers and senior centers**
   - Recreation departments
   - Aging services
   - May have disability-specific or general groups

---

### Online Platforms

1. **Meetup.com**
   - Largest directory of local groups
   - Search by disability topic + location
   - Filter by in-person, online, or hybrid
   - See group size, next meeting date, RSVP
   - Free to use

2. **Facebook**
   - Search: "[your city] [condition] support group"
   - Search: "[your city] disability groups"
   - Join local Facebook groups and ask for recommendations

3. **Google Search**
   - "[Your city] [condition] support group" or "disability groups"
   - Check "Events" tab for upcoming meetings
   - Look at creation date (active groups updated regularly)

4. **211.org** (US)
   - Information and referral service
   - Search by condition, service type, location
   - Comprehensive database
   - Call 2-1-1 from most US phones

5. **SAMHSA National Helpline** (US)
   - 1-800-662-4357
   - Can refer you to local support services
   - Free, confidential, 24/7

---

### Direct Outreach

1. **Call local chapters** of national organizations
   - Ask about groups
   - Ask for referrals to similar communities
   - Staff often attend groups

2. **Ask healthcare providers**
   - Doctors, therapists, case managers often know groups
   - Ask for written referrals
   - May have printouts of local resources

3. **Ask other disabled people**
   - Online communities (Reddit, Facebook groups)
   - Other disabled people you know
   - Word-of-mouth most effective

4. **Check university/college disability services**
   - If near college, may have community resources
   - May facilitate groups
   - May have students interested in peer support

## TYPES OF IN-PERSON GROUPS

### By Leadership

**Peer-led groups:**
- Run by disabled people for disabled people
- Informal, often unpaid
- Focus on shared experience
- Variable structure (some very organized, some casual)
- Strengths: Authentic peer connection, low barrier
- Weaknesses: May lack resources, burnout risk

**Professionally facilitated:**
- Led by therapist, social worker, or healthcare provider
- Structured format
- May include educational components
- Often insurance-covered
- Strengths: Professional guidance, structured topics
- Weaknesses: Power imbalance, may feel clinical, cost

**Hybrid groups:**
- Peer-led with professional advisor available
- Growing model
- Best of both worlds when done well
- Strengths: Peer authenticity + professional guidance
- Weaknesses: Requires good coordination

---

### By Content/Focus

**General disability support:**
- All disabilities welcome
- General topics: coping, resources, community
- Larger groups
- Good entry point if you don't find condition-specific

**Condition-specific:**
- For one diagnosis or closely related conditions
- Can be very detailed about condition
- Easier to find community of people who "get it"
- Smaller groups
- Examples: ME/CFS group, Fibromyalgia group, Autism group

**Activity-based:**
- Walking group, art therapy, sports adaptation
- Built-in conversation starter
- Good for people anxious about traditional support group
- Combines activity with social connection

**Family & caregiver groups:**
- For non-disabled family members and caregivers
- Focus on caregiver stress, information
- Often most available type of group
- Often more formal structure

**Identity-specific:**
- LGBTQ+ disabled people
- Disabled people of color
- Deaf disabled people
- Parent with disability
- Combinations of identities
- Often meet online or in larger cities

**Advocacy & organizing groups:**
- Focus on disability rights and action
- Political discussion
- Planning campaigns
- Less about personal support, more about collective change
- Can find through disability rights organizations

## EVALUATING IN-PERSON GROUPS

### Questions to Ask Before Attending

**Accessibility:**
- [ ] Is the meeting location physically accessible?
  - Wheelchair ramp or no steps?
  - Accessible parking nearby (free or validated)?
  - Accessible restrooms on same floor?
  - Elevator if multi-floor?
  - Wide aisles and pathways (36" minimum for wheelchairs)?
  - Accessible seating throughout (varied heights)?
  - Quiet or rest space for sensory breaks?
  - Scent-free (or low-scent)?
  - Service animals welcome?

- [ ] Are there alternative meeting formats?
  - Multiple meeting times (some people can't make weekly)?
  - Online option available (for people unable to attend)?
  - Hybrid (in-person + video) option?

- [ ] What accessibility accommodations available?
  - ASL interpreter?
  - CART/live captioning?
  - Large print materials?
  - Childcare?
  - Transportation assistance info?

**Format & Structure:**
- [ ] What exactly happens at meetings?
- [ ] How long are meetings?
- [ ] Is there a set structure or flexible?
- [ ] Required to share or can you observe?
- [ ] How often do they meet?
- [ ] Do you need to register or just show up?

**Confidentiality & Safety:**
- [ ] Is there a confidentiality agreement or policy?
- [ ] Who attends? (peers only? family? mixed?)
- [ ] Is it open to public or private?
- [ ] How are medical/private details handled?
- [ ] What are group's policies on inclusivity?

**Moderation & Environment:**
- [ ] Who runs the group?
- [ ] What's the tone? (supportive? educational? casual? formal?)
- [ ] Are there ground rules or community agreements?
- [ ] How are conflicts handled?
- [ ] Is group actively moderated?

**Practical Info:**
- [ ] Cost? (free, small fee, dues?)
- [ ] When and where exactly?
- [ ] How do you join? (register, just show up, wait list?)
- [ ] Is there an online component?
- [ ] Who can you contact with questions?

---

### Red Flags 🚩

Avoid groups with:
- Aggressive recruitment ("You MUST come")
- Sales pressure or promoting specific products/diets
- Single viewpoint strongly enforced (all medical, all alternative, anti-medication, etc.)
- Unmoderated free-for-all with no structure
- No confidentiality policy
- Requiring extensive personal information upfront
- No accommodation for disabilities
- Inaccessible location with no alternatives offered
- Hostile to certain disabilities or identities
- No visible moderation or leader accountability

---

### Green Flags 🟢

Look for groups with:
- Clear accessibility statement and commitment
- Welcoming tone in materials and from members
- Structured but flexible format
- Active moderation visible
- Confidentiality clearly stated
- Diversity of members and perspectives visible
- Multiple ways to participate
- Responsive to feedback
- Accessible location or virtual alternative
- Clear leadership and contact person
- Specific statement about including multiply-marginalized people
- Crisis support protocols

## ATTENDING YOUR FIRST MEETING

### Preparation

**Before you go:**
- Arrive 5-10 minutes early to get acclimated
- Bring water, medications, or snacks needed
- Have an exit plan if you need to leave early (no judgment)
- Wear whatever makes you comfortable
- Bring a support person if helpful (ask group first if policy allows)

**Managing anxiety:**
- It's OK to just listen first (no need to share)
- Most groups understand first-time nervousness
- You don't have to be "sick enough" to be there
- Everyone is nervous at first

**What to bring:**
- Phone with contact info for emergency person
- Your medication if needed during meeting
- Snacks or water for yourself
- Notebook if note-taking helps
- Pen
- Comfort item (fidget, stuffed animal, etc.)

---

### During the Meeting

**Participation guidelines:**
- Listen more than you talk at first
- Share at your own pace (sharing optional)
- Use "I" statements ("I feel..." not "everyone feels...")
- Don't interrupt or dismiss others' experiences
- Respect people's privacy and stories
- OK to ask clarifying questions

**What to expect:**
- Introduction round (optional or required, varies)
- Group sharing on a topic (or open sharing)
- Q&A or discussion time
- Guest speaker or educational content (sometimes)
- Social time before or after meeting
- Sign-in sheet (privacy varies)

**If you're overwhelmed:**
- Step out to restroom
- Focus on breathing
- It's OK to leave early
- Return next meeting if interested
- Let facilitator know if something needs to change

---

### After the First Meeting

**Deciding to return:**
- You don't have to decide immediately
- Give it 2-3 visits before deciding it's not for you
- Different groups have different vibes—keep searching if needed
- Send feedback to organizers if you have suggestions

**Staying engaged:**
- Go regularly for best results
- Gradually increase your participation level
- Make connections outside the group (exchange contact info)
- Suggest topics or activities
- Eventually consider helping to facilitate if interested

## STARTING YOUR OWN IN-PERSON GROUP

### Planning Phase

**Define your purpose:**
- What specific need will your group fill?
- Who is your target audience?
- What makes it unique from existing groups?
- How much time can you realistically commit?

**Research:**
- Look for existing groups to understand landscape
- Identify gaps in services
- Learn what works in your community
- Talk to potential members about their needs

**Decide on format:**
- Peer-led or professionally facilitated?
- How often will you meet? (weekly, bi-weekly, monthly?)
- What will happen at meetings?
- Hybrid with online component?

**Plan first 3 meetings before launching:**
1. Meeting 1: Introduction and getting to know each other
2. Meeting 2: Deeper sharing or guest speaker
3. Meeting 3: Establishing patterns and comfort

---

### Finding a Meeting Space

**Location requirements:**
- Physically accessible
  - Wheelchair access (ramp, no steps)
  - Accessible parking nearby (free if possible)
  - Accessible restrooms on same floor
  - Adequate climate control
  - Quiet enough for conversation
  - Comfortable seating (varied options if possible)
  - Private but not isolated
  - Easy accessible by transit if possible

**Options:**
- Community center (often free or low cost)
- Library meeting rooms (usually free)
- Disability organization offices
- Coffee shop (private room if available)
- School or university (if affiliated)
- Nonprofit office space
- Faith community space
- Parks (weather permitting)
- Someone's home (if accessible and group size small)
- Online option (backup for weather, accessibility issues)

**Tips:**
- Visit in person before first meeting
- Check accessibility thoroughly (test wheelchair access, parking, restrooms)
- Arrive early first meeting to set up
- Have backup location in mind for weather or accessibility issues
- Test technology if hybrid model

---

### Promotion & Recruitment

**Getting the word out:**
- Post on Meetup.com
- Share on Facebook (local groups, community pages)
- Create flyers for libraries, community centers, medical offices, disability organizations
- Use word-of-mouth (most effective)
- Tell friends, healthcare providers, local organizations
- Reach out to online disability communities (if appropriate)
- Contact local media (community calendar, press release)

**Initial recruitment:**
- Start with people you know
- Aim for 5-10 committed core members
- Build slowly and intentionally
- Quality over quantity
- Be persistent but not aggressive

---

### Logistics & Structure

**Planning meetings:**
- Weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly? (monthly easier to sustain)
- Same time and place for consistency
- 60-90 minute meetings work well
- Plan each meeting's focus in advance
- Send agendas or topics ahead of time

**Group agreements:**
- Establish confidentiality rule
- Agree on communication style (respectful, no judgment)
- Decide on medical advice boundaries
- Establish participation expectations
- Create conflict resolution process

**Resources to have ready:**
- Crisis resources and hotlines
- List of local disability services
- Healthcare provider recommendations
- Accessibility information for your venue
- Community calendar of events
- Contact information for facilitators

## ACCESSIBILITY PLANNING FOR IN-PERSON MEETINGS

### Physical Accessibility Checklist

**Before the meeting:**

Entrance & Parking:
- [ ] Wheelchair accessible entrance (no steps or ramp available)
- [ ] Accessible parking nearby (free or validated if possible)
- [ ] Reserved accessible parking spots
- [ ] Clear curb cuts or ramps
- [ ] No obstacles in entryway

Building & Restrooms:
- [ ] Accessible restrooms on same floor
- [ ] Elevator access if multi-floor
- [ ] Wide aisles and pathways (36" minimum for wheelchair)
- [ ] Accessible seating throughout (not segregated in front/back)
- [ ] Variety of seating (different heights, with/without arms, standing space)
- [ ] Seating capacity for larger bodies
- [ ] Reserved seating near front for interpreter or captioning needs

Environment:
- [ ] Quiet or rest space for sensory breaks
- [ ] Scent-free (no fragrances, cigarette smoke, strong food smells)
- [ ] Service animals welcome
- [ ] Controlled lighting (not too bright, no flickering)
- [ ] Sound level manageable (not echoing or too loud)
- [ ] Temperature control (not too hot or cold)
- [ ] Seating available for people who need to rest while participating

---

### Communication Accessibility

**In promotion materials:**
- [ ] Clear accessibility statement
- [ ] Explain how to request accommodations
- [ ] State scent-free policy
- [ ] Mention virtual attendance option if available
- [ ] List what to expect at meeting

**During meetings:**
- [ ] Use microphone always (even small groups)
- [ ] Speak clearly and face audience (for lip readers)
- [ ] Describe visual materials verbally
- [ ] Allow processing time
- [ ] Announce breaks and activities
- [ ] Provide accessible format materials (large print, digital copies)
- [ ] Use high-contrast slides with alt text if presenting

---

### Accommodations to Offer

**Always ask:**
"What accommodations would help you participate?"

- [ ] Offer in registration form
- [ ] Ask in person at first meeting
- [ ] Ask 1-2 weeks before meeting

**Common accommodations:**
- ASL interpreter
- CART/live captioning
- Large print materials
- Digital materials
- Accessible seating (front row, separate seating, etc.)
- Quiet space for breaks
- Scent-free guarantee
- Permission to record or take pictures
- Childcare (if applicable)
- Transportation assistance information
- Dietary accommodation information (if snacks served)

---

### Hybrid Models (Virtual + In-Person)

**Why hybrid works:**
- Removes transportation barriers
- Enables participation during flare-ups
- Includes those unable to attend in-person
- Reaches broader geographic area
- Accommodates various disabilities

**Technical setup:**
- Video platform (Zoom with accessibility features)
- Screen share for materials
- Chat for questions
- Telephone dial-in for those without video
- Recording for those who can't attend live
- Clear tech support instructions

**Tips for success:**
- Announce which meeting is in-person, which virtual, or if hybrid
- Give virtual attendees equal visibility (not an afterthought)
- Ensure captioning available
- Provide materials in advance
- Have technical support person available
- Test technology before each meeting

## BUILDING SUSTAINABLE GROUPS

### Moderator Support & Training

- Offer basic group facilitation skills training
- Provide mental health first aid training
- Learn to recognize crisis situations
- Know local resources
- Practice self-care to prevent facilitator burnout

---

### Recruitment of Co-Facilitators

- Don't do it alone long-term
- Look for interested regular members
- Share responsibilities
- Rotate facilitation tasks
- Cross-train for backup coverage

---

### Communication Between Meetings

- Send email updates with upcoming topic
- Create online discussion forum or group chat
- Celebrate milestones
- Share resources
- Support members during difficult times

---

### Measurement of Success

**Regular attendees feeling connected:**
- Are people showing up consistently?
- Are people making friendships outside group?
- Do people say they feel less isolated?

**New people joining regularly:**
- Is there sustainable recruitment?
- Are word-of-mouth referrals happening?
- Are people staying long-term?

**Positive feedback:**
- Do members report the group is helping?
- Are there suggestions for improvement?
- Would members recommend to others?

**Real-world connections:**
- Are people exchanging contact info?
- Are friendships forming?
- Are subgroups organizing around interests?

## INTERSECTIONALITY IN LOCAL GROUPS

### Building Truly Inclusive Groups

**Multiply-marginalized people often need:**
- Explicit acknowledgment that you welcome them
- Leadership that reflects community diversity
- Discussions that connect disability to other identities
- Space to talk about racism, sexism, transphobia, etc. alongside disability
- Attention to access needs specific to their disabilities

**Specific considerations:**

**LGBTQ+ disabled people:**
- Use chosen names and pronouns consistently
- Address relationship status without assumptions
- Welcome partners of any gender
- Discuss disability + gender transition intersections

**Disabled people of color:**
- Facilitate conversation about racial discrimination + ableism
- Recruit leadership from communities of color
- Address racism in disability spaces
- Connect to racial justice organizing

**Undocumented disabled people:**
- Don't require ID
- Don't share info with authorities
- Discuss immigration + disability together
- Know local immigration resources

**Disabled parents:**
- Discuss parenting while disabled (not "despite disability")
- Acknowledge childcare barriers
- Welcome children if feasible
- Address custody issues

**Disabled poor/unhoused people:**
- Address economic barriers directly
- Provide food (financial insecurity + disability real)
- Don't assume everyone can afford membership
- Discuss benefits, work, and poverty alongside disability

## GLOBAL PERSPECTIVE: IN-PERSON GROUPS WORLDWIDE

**In-person groups look different globally:**

**Wealthy Western countries:**
- Lots of formal organizations
- Professional facilitators common
- Clear accessibility expectations
- Good resources and infrastructure

**Middle-income countries:**
- Mix of formal and informal groups
- Often condition-specific
- Growing awareness of disability rights
- May meet in homes or nonprofits

**Lower-resource countries:**
- Groups exist but harder to organize
- Often meet in homes or faith spaces
- May not use "disability" language (other cultural terms)
- Community-based care model
- Family-centered vs. individual-focused

**Starting groups in underserved areas:**
- Begin with informal gathering (coffee, park)
- Use what you have (no fancy space needed)
- Word-of-mouth recruitment
- Connect to existing organizations if available
- Start very small (3-5 people)
