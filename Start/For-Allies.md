# For Allies: Supporting Disabled People Well

You're here because you want to support disabled people. This page explains how to be genuinely helpful (and what NOT to do).

## What It Means to Be an Ally

**Allyship** is commitment to supporting disabled people's rights, access, and self-determination.

Key principles:
- **Listen to disabled people** (especially most-marginalized disabled people)
- **Follow disabled people's lead** (don't decide for us)
- **Prioritize access** (not your comfort)
- **Do the work quietly** (don't expect praise)
- **Keep learning** (allyship is not destination but process)
- **Admit mistakes** (apologize briefly, change behavior, move on)
- **Share resources and power** (not just token inclusion)

## What Allies Should Do

#### Listen to Disabled People

**Do:**
- Ask disabled people directly about their needs
- Listen without interrupting or planning your response
- Believe what disabled people tell you about their experience
- Ask before helping (don't assume what people need)
- Center voices of most-marginalized disabled people (disabled people of color, disabled poor people, disabled Global South people, etc.)

**Don't:**
- Assume you know what disabled people need
- Talk over disabled people
- Center your feelings or perspective
- Assume you know someone's disability or limitations
- Speak for disabled people

### Make Accessibility the Default

**Do:**
- Plan accessibility from the start (meetings, events, documents)
- Provide captions, transcripts, interpreters, large print, plain language versions
- Offer multiple ways to participate (in-person, online, async, etc.)
- Ask about access needs in advance
- Budget for accessibility costs
- Make accessibility ongoing practice, not afterthought

**Don't:**
- Treat accessibility as special accommodation (it's basic inclusion)
- Wait until last minute to provide access
- Complain about accessibility costs
- Act like accessibility is "too hard"
- Segregate accessibility (separate deaf meeting, separate mobility-accessible entrance)

### Use Respectful Language

**Do:**
- Use identity-first language ("disabled people")
- Ask how someone prefers to be described
- Use specific terminology (not "special needs")
- Avoid inspiration narratives
- Use active language (not "suffers from," "confined to")

**Don't:**
- Use outdated or offensive terms
- Describe disabled people as inspiring for existing
- Use inspiration porn narratives
- Assume someone's disability based on appearance
- Use "people with disabilities" when someone has asked for identity-first

#### Share Power and Resources

**Do:**
- Hire disabled people in decision-making roles (not just advisory)
- Pay disabled consultants and activists fairly
- Ensure disabled leadership (not token representation)
- Share resources (funding, platform, decision-making power)
- Step back when your non-disabled perspective isn't needed

**Don't:**
- Treat disabled people as free labor
- Hire disabled people only for "disability work"
- Make disabled people tokens
- Prioritize non-disabled comfort over disabled access
- Speak for disabled people without asking

### Admit Mistakes and Learn

**Do:**
- Recognize when you've made a mistake
- Apologize briefly and genuinely
- Change your behavior
- Continue learning
- Read disability authors and activists
- Follow disabled leaders on social media
- Support disability organizations led by disabled people

**Don't:**
- Expect disabled people to teach you
- Center your hurt feelings when corrected
- Make excuses
- Ask disabled people to forgive you
- Expect a medal for basic respect

## What Allies Should NOT Do

### Don't be savior-like

**Ableist framing:** "I'm helping the poor disabled people!"  
**Better approach:** "I'm supporting disabled people's self-determination."

Disabled people don't need saving. We need access, rights, and power.

### Don't center your comfort

**Ableist move:** Avoiding discussing disability because it makes you uncomfortable  
**Better approach:** Sit with discomfort. Disabled people live with discomfort all the time.

### Don't treat disability as tragedy

**Ableist framing:** "It must be so hard being disabled!" (centering your sadness, not disabled person's life)  
**Better approach:** Listen to disabled people's actual experience (which might include hard parts AND good parts AND regular parts)

### Don't use inspiration porn narratives

**Ableist:** "You're so brave for going to work despite being disabled!"  
**Better approach:** "Work is hard for everyone sometimes."

### Don't overstep boundaries

**Ableist move:** Offering unsolicited help ("Let me carry that!" without asking)  
**Better approach:** "Do you want help with that?" and respect the answer

**Ableist move:** Touching someone's mobility aid without permission  
**Better approach:** Ask before touching someone's wheelchair, cane, etc.

**Ableist move:** Asking about someone's disability or "what happened?"  
**Better approach:** Let disabled people share on their own terms

### Don't make it about you

**Ableist:** "I have a bad knee, so I understand disability!" (no you don't)  
**Better approach:** "That sounds difficult. What would help?"

### Don't demand gratitude

**Ableist:** "I made your event accessible, you're welcome!"  
**Better approach:** Provide accessibility without expectation of praise

## Specific Ally Actions

### If you're in healthcare:
- Listen to patient's expertise about their own body
- Believe people about their symptoms
- Provide accommodations without question
- Use plain language
- Respect patient autonomy in treatment decisions
- Address internalized ableism in healthcare system

### If you're an employer:
- Hire disabled people
- Provide real accommodations
- Pay disabled employees fairly (don't assume they'll work for less)
- Make workplace physically and cognitively accessible
- Support flexibility for disability management
- Protect from discrimination

### If you're an educator:
- Provide accommodations proactively
- Use accessible teaching methods (captions, transcripts, flexible deadlines, etc.)
- Hire disabled faculty and staff
- Include disability in curriculum
- Build community (disabled students shouldn't be isolated)
- Fight from within system for systemic change

### If you're a family member:
- Listen to your disabled relative about their needs
- Support their self-determination (even if you disagree with choices)
- Learn about disability (books, community, resources)
- Connect them to disability community if they want
- Don't isolate them
- Don't speak for them
- Examine your own ableist assumptions (you probably have some)

### If you're an organizer/activist:
- Make all events/actions accessible
- Center disabled people's leadership
- Pay disabled activists
- Build disabled leadership pipeline
- Address ableism in movement spaces
- Support disabled people's rest and sustainability

## Questions Allies Often Ask

**Q: What if I don't know if something is ableist?**  
A: Ask disabled people. "Is this ableist?" is a legitimate question.

**Q: What if I make a mistake?**  
A: Apologize sincerely, change behavior, move on. Don't center your guilt.

**Q: Can I ask about someone's disability?**  
A: Only if you have good reason and existing relationship. Usually not appropriate to ask strangers.

**Q: Should I stare/point out disability?**  
A: No. Treat disabled people normally. Don't make a big deal about disability.

**Q: What if disabled person tells me I'm being ableist?**  
A: Believe them. Adjust behavior. Thank them for feedback.

**Q: How do I learn more?**  
A: Read disabled authors, follow disabled activists, attend disability events, listen to disabled people.

## Remember

Being an ally is:
- **Ongoing process** (not destination)
- **Work you do quietly** (not performative)
- **Following disabled people's lead** (not your own agenda)
- **Centering most-marginalized** (not just visible disabled people)
- **Sharing power** (not maintaining control)

Your goal is to support disabled people's full participation, self-determination, and justice. That's it.
