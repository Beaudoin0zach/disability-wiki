# What Is Disability? Multiple Perspectives

Disability means different things to different people. This page explains various ways of thinking about disability.

## Quick Answer

**Disability** is a natural, permanent (or long-term) difference in how a body or mind works that affects how someone navigates a society not built for them.

**Longer answer:** Disability is complex. It's simultaneously:
- A medical reality (different body/brain function)
- A social experience (barriers created by inaccessible society)
- A political identity (basis for collective action)
- A cultural community (shared experience and values)
- A human diversity (different is not wrong)

This page explores all these dimensions.

## Medical Perspective: What's the Body/Brain Difference?

From a medical viewpoint, disability involves:

**Physical differences:** Different bone structure, muscle function, organ system, sensory capacity (vision, hearing, touch, etc.)

**Neurological differences:** Brain processes information differently (autism, ADHD, dyslexia, stroke recovery, brain injury, etc.)

**Systemic differences:** Internal body systems work differently (immune system, metabolic function, etc.)

**Examples:**
- Spinal cord injury → different nerve signaling
- Deafness → no auditory signal transmission (but often full brain/cognition)
- Autism → different neurological wiring affecting communication and sensory processing
- Diabetes → pancreas doesn't produce/regulate insulin
- Depression → different neurotransmitter function

**Important:** Medical differences vary WIDELY. No two disabled people have identical experiences, even with the same diagnosis.

## The Social Model: Disability is Social, Not Individual

The **social model** (developed by disabled activists) says:

**Impairment** = A physical, sensory, or cognitive difference (happens in the body)

**Disability** = Social disadvantage created when society fails to accommodate that difference

**Example:**
- **Impairment:** I don't hear sound
- **Disability:** The world is built for hearing—meetings have no captions, fire alarms have no visual indicators, no one knows how to communicate with me
- **The fix:** Not to "fix" my deafness, but to build a fully accessible world with interpreters, captions, flashing lights

**Another example:**
- **Impairment:** I use a wheelchair (my legs don't work)
- **Disability:** The world is built with stairs, no ramps, curbs everywhere, inaccessible transportation
- **The fix:** Not to make me walk, but to build ramps, elevators, accessible transit

**Why it matters:** The social model shifts the problem from "something is wrong with you" to "something is wrong with society." This is empowering because society can change. We don't need to.

## The Human Rights Model: Disability Rights Are Universal

The **human rights model** says disabled people have the same rights as everyone:
- Right to participate in society
- Right to make our own decisions
- Right to accessibility
- Right to freedom from abuse
- Right to work, education, relationships, family

This framework emphasizes:
- **Non-discrimination** — No one should be excluded based on disability
- **Accommodation** — Society must provide necessary supports
- **Accessibility** — Infrastructure must be usable by all
- **Inclusion** — Disabled people belong everywhere

This model is enshrined in the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD).

## Disability Justice Model: Intersectionality & Collective Power

**Disability justice** (developed by disabled activists of color, disabled queer/trans people, disabled immigrants) expands the social model:

Core principles:

**Intersectionality matters.** A disabled person of color experiences disability differently than a white disabled person. A disabled trans person's needs are different from a disabled cisgender person's. We can't understand disability separately from race, gender, class, immigration status, etc.

**Most-impacted lead.** The people affected by a problem should lead the solution. This means:
- Disabled people of color lead discussions about race and disability
- Disabled trans people lead discussions about gender and disability
- Disabled Global South people lead discussions about disability in their regions
- Disabled poor people lead discussions about poverty and disability

**Interdependence is power.** We reject the idea that independence = good and dependence = bad. Actually, humans are interdependent. We rely on each other. That's beautiful and creates collective power.

**Care and commitment to each other.** Disability justice emphasizes mutual aid, taking care of each other, and showing up for each other. Not charity (hierarchical, one-way), but care (mutual, respectful).

**Recognition of wholeness.** Disabled people are whole people—not broken, not incomplete. We have complex identities, multiple needs, and full humanity.

**Sustainability and transformation.** We can't burn ourselves out. Disability justice focuses on sustainable change and transforming systems—not just individual accommodation within broken systems.

## Lived Experience Perspective: What Disability Feels Like

Beyond models and frameworks, disability is a lived experience:

**Managing symptoms:** Many disabled people spend significant energy managing pain, fatigue, mental health symptoms, or unpredictable body function.

**Navigating inaccessibility:** Daily navigation of a world not built for you—finding accessible bathrooms, worrying about stairs, fighting for accommodations at work.

**Medical system interaction:** Many disabled people interact regularly with healthcare—appointments, procedures, medication management, fighting to be believed.

**Identity and community:** For many, disability is a core identity tied to community, culture, and shared values (especially true for Deaf and Blind communities).

**Grief and growth:** Some disabled people grieve losses of function or health, while simultaneously discovering strength, community, and new ways of living.

**Variable and unpredictable:** Many disabilities are unpredictable—good days and bad days, flares and remissions. This affects work, relationships, and planning.

## Disability vs. Difference

**Important distinction:**
- **Diversity** = Natural human variation (some people are left-handed, tall, introverted, etc.)
- **Disability** = Diversity that carries systematic disadvantage due to social barriers

Left-handedness is diversity. Using a left-handed scissors might require accommodation, but left-handed people can still fully participate in society.

Deafness is disability because the hearing-centered world creates systematic barriers. A deaf person can't access everyday communication without accommodation.

## Visible vs. Invisible Disabilities

**Visible disabilities** are apparent to strangers (wheelchair use, guide dog, visible facial difference, etc.)

Challenges:
- Assumptions and stereotyping
- Visible minority status
- Access to disabled parking, priority seating, etc. but also stares and comments
- People know to offer help (sometimes too much)

**Invisible disabilities** aren't obvious (chronic pain, mental health, neurodivergence, hearing loss, many autoimmune conditions, etc.)

Challenges:
- Disbelief and invalidation ("But you look fine!")
- People don't understand accommodations ("Why do you need to sit?" "Why can't you just try harder?")
- Can hide disability to avoid discrimination but then suffer alone
- Don't always get the support they need

Both visible and invisible disabilities have real challenges. Neither is "better" or "worse."

## Temporary vs. Permanent vs. Fluctuating

**Permanent disabilities** remain stable (blindness, spinal cord injury, amputations, deaf from birth, etc.)

**Progressive disabilities** worsen over time (multiple sclerosis, ALS, many types of arthritis, etc.)

**Fluctuating/episodic disabilities** have ups and downs (mental health conditions, chronic illness, migraine, etc.)

**Temporary disabilities** eventually resolve (broken leg, concussion recovery, postpartum disability, etc.)

Many people move between categories—a temporary injury becomes long-term, a progressive condition stabilizes, a stable condition flares up.

## Acquired vs. Congenital vs. Age-Related

**Congenital disabilities** present from birth or early development (cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, autism, deafness, many vision disabilities)

**Acquired disabilities** happen after birth (spinal cord injury, traumatic brain injury, stroke, amputation, etc.)

**Age-related disabilities** develop as we age (arthritis, vision loss, hearing loss, cognitive changes, etc.)

Different pathways but all create similar needs for accessibility and accommodation.

## Disability Communities

Some disabilities have strong communities and cultures:
- **Deaf community** — Shared language (ASL or other sign languages), culture, identity. Many Deaf people don't consider deafness a disability but a cultural/linguistic identity.
- **Blind community** — Shared strategies and technology, community organizations, culture
- **Autistic community** — Neurodivergent culture, rejection of "functioning labels," emphasis on neurodiversity (different, not disordered)
- **Chronic illness community** — Shared experiences of managing long-term health, fatigue, medical trauma

Other disabilities have less organized communities (depending on prevalence, visibility, country, etc.)

## Disability and Privilege

Disabled people can have privilege in some ways:

- A wealthy disabled person has more access to care than a poor disabled person
- A disabled person of a privileged race has advantages a disabled person of color doesn't
- A disabled person in a wealthy country has more resources than a disabled person in a low-income country
- A disabled person with family support has advantages over a disabled person alone

Disability justice means recognizing these intersections and centering most-marginalized disabled people.

## Medical vs. Social Model in Practice

These models aren't either/or. In real life:

**Medical stuff is real.** Pain, fatigue, cognitive differences are real physical realities.

**Social barriers are real too.** Inaccessible buildings, discrimination, lack of interpreters create disability.

**Both matter.** We need both medical care AND social change.

**Example:** An autistic person might benefit from:
- Medical support (therapy for co-occurring anxiety, strategies for sensory overload)
- Social support (workplace accommodations, understanding from family, community)
- Rights protection (fighting discrimination, access to education)
- Community (connecting with other autistic people, disability justice framework)

All are valuable. All are part of understanding disability holistically.

## Common Myths About Disability

**Myth:** Disability is always tragic/sad  
**Reality:** Disabled people have full lives with joy, meaning, relationships, and accomplishment. Tragedy narratives are about non-disabled people's discomfort, not disabled reality.

**Myth:** Disabled people want to be "fixed"  
**Reality:** Some want medical treatment, some don't. Many want social change, accessibility, and acceptance more than medical "cure."

**Myth:** Disability is about individual limitation  
**Reality:** Disability is about social barriers. The limitation comes from inaccessibility, not the person.

**Myth:** All disabled people want the same thing  
**Reality:** Disabled people have diverse needs, desires, and priorities. We're not a monolith.

**Myth:** Disability has to be permanent  
**Reality:** Temporary disabilities and fluctuating disabilities are real disabilities. Duration doesn't determine validity.
