# How to Use This Wiki: Navigation Guide

This wiki has a lot of information. This page helps you find what you need.

## Three Ways to Find Information

### Option 1: Search

Use the search box at the top of any page.

**Search for:**
- Topics: "housing," "employment," "mental health"
- Laws: "ADA," "Fair Housing Act"
- Programs: "SSI," "Medicaid," "Section 8"
- Disability types: "autism," "deaf," "chronic pain"
- Specific needs: "screen readers," "workplace accommodations"

**Search tips:**
- Be specific (search "ADA accommodations" not just "disabilities")
- Try different keywords if first search doesn't work
- Look at multiple results—there might be several relevant pages

### Option 2: Browse by Topic

Go to the home page and click on a topic category:
- Crisis & Emergency
- Money & Benefits
- Your Rights & Laws
- Housing
- Health & Care
- Education
- Work & Employment
- Assistive Technology
- Specific Disabilities
- Intersectionality
- Community & Activism
- Library

**Browse if:**
- You don't know exactly what you're looking for
- You want to explore a whole topic area
- You're researching broadly

### Option 3: Follow Related Pages

At the bottom of each page, you'll see "Related Pages" with links to connected content.

**Use related pages to:**
- Go deeper into a topic
- Find related information
- Discover pages you didn't know existed
- Jump to similar content in other regions/countries

## Understanding Page Structure

**Every page follows the same format:**

**1. Title + Opening Summary**  
Tells you what the page is about (usually 1-3 sentences)

**2. Historical Origins and Movement Leadership**  
Where this law/program/concept came from, who fought for it, why it matters historically

**3. Key Information**  
The main content—could be legal provisions, program details, terminology, steps, etc.

**4. Why It Matters**  
Why this is important for disabled people—practical impact

**5. Strengths and Breakthroughs**  
What works well about this law/program/concept

**6. Challenges, Nuances, and Critiques**  
Limitations, problems, what's missing

**7. Disability Types Applicability**  
How this applies to different disabilities (Deaf, Blind, Neurodivergent, Mental Health, etc.)

**8. How Organizers and Groups Can Apply It**  
Concrete strategies for advocates and disabled people

**9. Global Perspectives & Intersectionality**  
How it works in different regions, intersectional considerations

**10. Further Resources**  
Links, organizations, contact info, additional pages

**Pro tip:** If you just want basics, read sections 1-4. If you want deeper understanding, read all sections.

→ Read opening summary (1 min)

**If you need practical information:**
→ Read sections 1-4 (Key information + Why It Matters) (10-15 min)

**If you need complete understanding:**
→ Read all sections (20-30 min depending on complexity)

**If you need to advocate:**
→ Read sections 1-8 (focus on "How Organizers" section) (20 min)

**If you need global perspective:**
→ Focus on section 9 and look for multiple country pages (15-30 min)

## Tips for Different Disabilities

**If you're Deaf:**
- Check for caption and transcript links
- Look for interpreter access info
- Search for "sign language" or "ASL" if relevant

**If you're Blind or Low Vision:**
- Content is screen-reader compatible
- You can use NVDA or JAWS with any page
- Heading structure is semantic (press H to jump between sections)
- All links have descriptive text

**If you're Neurodivergent (ADHD, Autism, etc.):**
- Pages use short paragraphs and subheadings for easy scanning
- You can skip sections and jump around
- Don't feel obligated to read everything linearly
- Search for specific concepts rather than reading whole pages

**If you have mental health disability:**
- Pages include content notes where relevant
- Crisis resources linked throughout
- Pages centered on self-determination, not medical model
- You can take breaks—this is a lot of info

**If you have chronic illness/pain:**
- Pages designed for access without long reading sessions
- You can read in installments
- Search function helps you find what you need quickly
- Set your own pace

## Accessibility Features

This wiki works with:

**Screen readers** (NVDA, JAWS, VoiceOver, etc.)
- All pages have proper heading structure
- Links have descriptive text
- Images have alt text
- No color-only information

**Mobile devices** (phones, tablets)
- Single-column layout
- Large touch targets
- Responsive design
- Works in any orientation

**Browser text settings:**
- Zoom in/out (works at any size)
- Adjust text size
- High contrast mode
- Custom fonts (some browsers)

**Keyboard navigation**
- Tab through links and headings
- Arrow keys navigate after focusing on lists
- Enter/Space to click links
- No keyboard traps

## Printable Pages

Most pages can be printed or saved as PDF:
- Use browser's print function (Ctrl+P or Cmd+P)
- Content stays readable in print
- Links are preserved

## Suggested Reading Paths

**You need help RIGHT NOW:**
1. Search for specific crisis resource
2. Go to [[/crisis/hotlines|Crisis Hotlines]] page
3. Use contact info immediately

**You're newly disabled:**
1. Read [[/start/welcome|Welcome]] (orientation)
2. Read [[/start/what-is-disability|What Is Disability?]] (context)
3. Search for specific disability or topic you need
4. Explore [[/community|Community section]] to find peer support

**You need legal/rights information:**
1. Search for your country or specific law
2. Read page's "Key Information" section
3. Look at "How Organizers" section for advocacy strategies
4. Check "Further Resources" for legal aid organizations

**You're helping someone else:**
1. Read [[/start/allies|For Allies]] (this section)
2. Search for their specific needs
3. Use info to support their self-determination
4. Encourage them to read directly (not to receive info through you)

**You want to explore disability deeply:**
1. Read all [[/start|Start Here]] pages
2. Explore a topic section (Housing, Benefits, etc.) page by page
3. Follow "Related Pages" to go deeper
4. Read [[/library|Library section]] for books and media

## Common Confusions

**Q: Where's information about my country?**  
A: Use search function. If not found, it's on the roadmap. See [[/about/how-to-contribute|How to Contribute]] to help add it.

**Q: Can I print this page?**  
A: Yes, use your browser's print function. Formatting works well in print.

**Q: Can I download pages?**  
A: Yes, save as PDF using print function, or download markdown files (coming soon).

**Q: Is this legal advice?**  
A: No. This is informational. For legal advice, consult a lawyer.

**Q: Is this medical advice?**  
A: No. For medical advice, consult your healthcare provider.

**Q: Can I use this information elsewhere?**  
A: Yes, under CC-BY-SA 4.0 license (must credit us, must share under same license). See [[/about|About page]] for details.
