# Disability Models: How We Understand Disability

Disability can be understood through different "models" or frameworks. This page explains the main ones.

## Why Models Matter

A "model" is a framework for thinking about disability. Different models lead to different solutions:

**Medical model** says: "The problem is the person's body/brain. Fix the person."  
→ Solution: Medical treatment, surgery, therapy, cure

**Social model** says: "The problem is society's failure to accommodate. Fix society."  
→ Solution: Ramps, interpreters, accessible technology, accessibility laws

**Human rights model** says: "The problem is discrimination and lack of rights. Guarantee rights."  
→ Solution: Legal protections, non-discrimination laws, accessibility requirements

**Disability justice model** says: "The problem is intersecting oppression. Transform systems."  
→ Solution: Centering most-marginalized disabled people, collective action, radical transformation

All models have truth. Understanding them helps you navigate disability systems and advocacy.

## The Medical Model (Traditional)

**Core idea:** Disability is a medical condition in an individual body/brain that needs to be fixed, treated, or cured.

**How it works:**
- Doctor diagnoses the problem
- Treatment goal is to "fix" or manage the disability
- Success = symptoms reduced or person becomes more "normal"
- Disabled person is "patient" requiring expert help

**Example:** Person is blind. Medical model says: Get surgery to restore sight. If surgery fails, the person is "unfixable."

**Where it's useful:**
- Actual medical treatment (insulin for diabetes, pain management, etc.)
- Diagnosis and understanding of conditions
- Accessing healthcare

**Where it falls short:**
- Assumes all disability is medical problem needing fixing
- Ignores social barriers (ramps, interpreters, accessible technology matter more than surgery in many cases)
- Treats disability as tragic/wrong instead of natural variation
- Centers medical professionals as experts instead of disabled people
- Ignores that some disabilities can't/shouldn't be "fixed" but are valid identities

**In practice:** You'll encounter medical model thinking in healthcare, disability benefits systems, and insurance. Important to navigate but recognize its limitations.

## The Social Model (Liberation Framework)

**Core idea:** Disability is created by society's failure to accommodate human diversity. Fix the society, not the person.

**How it works:**
- Separate "impairment" (physical difference) from "disability" (social disadvantage)
- Recognize that society could accommodate everyone if designed well
- Solution is accessibility, accommodation, and inclusion
- Disabled people are experts (not patients requiring fixing)

**Example:** Person is blind. Social model says: The problem isn't blindness, it's that the world is designed for sighted people. Solution: Screen readers (technology), audio descriptions (design), accessible websites (infrastructure), braille (literacy).

**Core distinction:**
- **Impairment:** Doesn't see light
- **Disability:** Can't read printed books, can't navigate unmarked building, can't access websites

The impairment is in the person. The disability is in the society.

**Where it's useful:**
- Understanding why accessibility matters
- Recognizing systemic barriers
- Designing inclusive solutions
- Moving from individual "problem" to collective power
- Building disability rights movements

**Where it has limitations:**
- Medical care is still necessary for many (this model doesn't replace healthcare)
- Pain and symptoms are real (not just social construction)
- Doesn't address internalized ableism or community trauma
- Can sometimes ignore actual support needs

**In practice:** This is the framework for disability rights advocacy, accessibility law, and movement building. Most of this wiki is organized around social model principles.

## The Human Rights Model

**Core idea:** Disabled people have universal human rights that must be protected and guaranteed.

**How it works:**
- Disability rights are human rights (equal participation, freedom from discrimination, self-determination)
- Society has obligation to guarantee rights through law and policy
- Rights include: accessibility, accommodation, non-discrimination, freedom of choice
- Disability inequality is a human rights violation

**Key rights framework:**
- Right to participate in society (work, education, relationships, politics, culture, etc.)
- Right to accessibility (information, buildings, transportation, etc.)
- Right to accommodations (reasonable adjustments to participate)
- Right to self-determination (make own choices about treatment, support, life)
- Right to freedom from violence and abuse
- Right to inclusion (not segregation)

**Example:** Person is deaf. Human rights framework says: You have right to communication access, education, employment, community participation. Society must provide interpreters, captions, accessibility, non-discrimination.

**Where it's useful:**
- Legal frameworks and international law (UN CRPD)
- Disability rights advocacy and litigation
- Policy change and legal protection
- Establishing that disability access is not charity but right

**Where it has limitations:**
- Rights exist on paper but enforcement varies (especially Global South)
- Rich countries can afford more access than poor countries
- Individual rights focus misses collective/systemic power
- Doesn't center most-marginalized voices

**In practice:** This is the basis for disability rights law, the ADA, the CRPD, and accessible design requirements.

## The Disability Justice Model (Radical/Intersectional)

**Core idea:** Disability justice centers disabled people—especially disabled people of color, disabled poor people, disabled trans/queer people, disabled Global South people. It emphasizes collective power, intersectionality, and systemic transformation.

**Developed by:** Disabled activists of color, inspired by Black feminism and social justice movements. Leaders include Patty Berne, Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha, and many others.

**Core principles:**

1. **Leadership of most impacted.** The people affected by a policy should lead the solution. Disabled people of color should lead race + disability work. Disabled Global South people should lead Global South disability work.

2. **Intersectionality is central.** Disability cannot be understood separately from race, class, gender, sexuality, immigration status, etc. A disabled Black person's experience is different from a white disabled person's. Centering intersectionality means centering people with multiple marginalized identities.

3. **Interdependence over independence.** We reject the myth that independence = good and dependence = bad. Humans are interdependent. We rely on each other. That's healthy, beautiful, and creates power.

4. **Collective power.** Individual accommodation or charity is not enough. We need systemic change through collective action, mutual aid, and movement building.

5. **Recognition of wholeness.** Disabled people are whole. We're not broken, incomplete, or problems to fix. We have diverse talents, needs, relationships, and full humanity.

6. **Sustainability and care.** We cannot burn ourselves out. Justice work must be sustainable and include care for activists.

7. **Commitment to transformation.** We're not just asking for a seat at the table. We're asking to transform the table, the room, and the whole system.

**Example:** Person is disabled and poor and person of color. Disability justice says: We need to address disability, racism, and poverty together. Solutions must be led by people at this intersection. We need systemic change—not just ramps (important but insufficient) but also addressing police violence, poverty, and racism. We need mutual aid and community power, not charity.

**Where it's useful:**
- Understanding disability as connected to all oppressions
- Movement building with multiply-marginalized people
- Recognizing limits of individual accommodation
- Building collective power
- Centering Global South, Indigenous, and most-marginalized voices
- Long-term transformative change

**Where it's challenging:**
- Complex framework (takes time to understand)
- Requires examining your own privileges
- Asks for systemic change (harder than individual accommodation)
- Still emerging and evolving

**In practice:** This framework animates DisabilityWiki. We center disability justice values while using human rights and social model language.

## All Models in Practice

In reality, you need all of them:

**Medical care is necessary** (treatment, pain management, healthcare)

**Social model thinking is necessary** (recognizing barriers, fighting for accessibility)

**Human rights framework is necessary** (legal protection, ensuring rights)

**Disability justice principles are necessary** (centering multiply-marginalized people, building movement, transforming systems)

**Example:** Autistic person advocating for workplace accommodations needs:
- **Medical knowledge:** Understanding autism, neuroscience, maybe finding a doctor who supports accommodations
- **Social model:** Recognizing that workplace is designed for neurotypical people and needs to change, not that autistic person needs "fixing"
- **Human rights:** Knowing that ADA/local laws require accommodation, knowing their rights
- **Disability justice:** Recognizing that neurodivergent people of color face additional barriers, connecting to disability justice movement, building collective power

## Which Model Should You Use?

**When accessing healthcare:** Medical model is necessary and appropriate

**When advocating for accessibility:** Social model is your best tool

**When fighting discrimination:** Human rights framework gives you legal ground

**When building movement for systemic change:** Disability justice principles guide long-term work

**Best approach:** Understand all models. Use the right one for the situation. Hold them all lightly.

## Disability Models Globally

These models developed primarily in Western countries (especially US, UK). Other countries and cultures have different frameworks:

**Global South:** Many countries use different terminology, blend models, or have not adopted Western disability frameworks. Some disability scholarship is emerging from Global South, Indigenous, and other cultural frameworks.

**Indigenous communities:** Many Indigenous cultures don't separate "disability" as distinct category. Disability is part of natural diversity. Some have reciprocal care models that don't fit Western frameworks.

**Collectivist cultures:** Some cultures emphasize family/community responsibility more than individual rights. Frameworks may prioritize collective wellbeing over individual accommodation.

This wiki will increasingly include non-Western frameworks as we expand globally.

## Your Model Questions Answered

**Q: Which model is "right"?**  
A: They're all useful in different contexts. Disability justice is the most comprehensive but builds on foundations of all others.

**Q: Can I be disabled under social model but not medical model?**  
A: Possibly. If society were fully accessible, many people might not need medical treatment. But this doesn't mean medical care isn't real/valuable.

**Q: Does disability justice replace other models?**  
A: No. It builds on them and expands them. You still need medical care, human rights protections, and social model accessibility.

**Q: How do I use these models in advocacy?**  
A: Choose the one most effective for your audience. Lawyers respond to human rights framing. Accessibility designers respond to social model. Organizers respond to disability justice.
