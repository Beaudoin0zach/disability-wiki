# DISABILITY RIGHTS OVERVIEW

Disability rights are the fundamental protections and opportunities disabled people deserve. This page explains what disability rights mean, why they matter, and how they connect to your daily life.

---

## WHAT ARE DISABILITY RIGHTS?

**Disability rights are the idea that disabled people:**
- Deserve equal opportunities, not charity
- Have the right to make decisions about our own lives
- Can't be discriminated against because of disability
- Deserve access to employment, education, housing, healthcare, and community
- Deserve to live with dignity and respect
- Deserve a voice in decisions that affect us

**These aren't special favors.** These are basic human rights.

---

## QUICK START: YOUR BASIC RIGHTS

No matter where you live, disabled people generally deserve:

✓ **Equal opportunities** — Same chance at jobs, education, housing as non-disabled people  
✓ **Accessibility** — Information, buildings, and systems designed so you can use them  
✓ **Reasonable accommodations** — Changes to help you participate fully  
✓ **No discrimination** — Can't be treated worse because of disability  
✓ **Respect and dignity** — Treated as capable adult, not pitied or patronized  
✓ **Choice and control** — Make decisions about your own life  
✓ **Community participation** — Live in regular community, not segregated  
✓ **Privacy** — Your medical information is yours; confidentiality protected  

These rights are what disability rights laws try to protect.

---

## WHY DISABILITY RIGHTS MATTER

### For Individual Disabled People
Rights protect you from discrimination and open doors:
- Right to work means you can earn income and support yourself
- Right to education means you can learn and develop skills
- Right to accessible housing means you're not forced into institutions
- Right to accessible healthcare means you can stay healthy
- Right to accessible transportation means you can get around
- Right to vote means you have voice in democracy

**Without these rights:** Disabled people are more likely to be poor, isolated, unemployed, under-educated, and institutionalized.

### For Disabled Communities
When disabled people have rights, we can:
- Live in regular communities instead of institutions (Community integration)
- Participate in society fully
- Build disability culture and community
- Organize and advocate for each other
- Create a world that works better for everyone

### For Society
Disability rights benefit everyone:
- Accessible design helps non-disabled people too (curb cuts, captions, clear language)
- Diversity of thinking and perspectives improves everything
- Inclusive workplaces perform better
- Accessible communities are friendlier to everyone
- Respecting human rights is the foundation of democracy

## TWO MODELS: CHARITY VS. RIGHTS

Disability rights are based on a fundamentally different way of thinking about disability.

### Old Model: Charity Model
**How it sees disability:**
- Disability is tragedy; disabled people are victims
- Non-disabled people should "help" disabled people
- Disabled people are grateful for charity
- Focus on fixing/curing the disabled person

**Where you see it:**
- "Inspiration porn" (disabled people celebrated for existing)
- Telethons asking for pity and donations
- Institutions and group homes ("We take care of disabled people")
- Medical model (disability is problem with person's body)
- "You're so brave" (for just living)

**Problems with this model:**
- Disempowers disabled people (we're victims, not agents)
- Focuses on disability as problem, not society's failure to include us
- Doesn't actually improve our lives
- Makes us feel ashamed instead of valued

---

### New Model: Rights-Based Approach
**How it sees disability:**
- Disability is a natural part of human diversity
- Society excludes disabled people through lack of access, not disability itself
- Disabled people have expertise about our own lives
- Focus on removing barriers; we don't need to be "fixed"

**Where you see it:**
- Disabled people leading organizations and making decisions
- Accessibility built in from the start
- Disabled people talking about lives, not inspiration stories
- Social model (disability is society's failure to include, not body's failure)
- Recognition of disabled people as valuable

**How this model works:**
- Moves focus from "fixing the disabled person" to "removing barriers"
- Example: Person in wheelchair can't access building because no ramp
  - Old model: "Help the disabled person; find elevator"
  - New model: "Build accessible entrance for everyone"
- Recognizes disabled people as experts on our own needs
- Values disabled people's contributions

**Rights-based laws:**
- ADA (USA), Equality Act (UK), Accessibility Act (EU), DDA (Australia)
- CRPD (UN) is rights-based
- Most modern disability laws based on this model

## THREE LEGAL MODELS OF DISABILITY

Different countries approach disability law through different lenses:

### Medical Model (Older Approach)
**Sees disability as:** Medical problem in the person's body  
**Approach:** Find treatments or cure; if person can't be cured, they need care  
**Where it shows up:** Health insurance decisions, medical evidence requirements, focus on diagnosis  
**Problem:** Makes disability the person's fault; ignores environmental barriers  
**Countries:** Some Global South countries; some aspects in US/Western countries

---

### Social Model (Modern Approach)
**Sees disability as:** Society's failure to include people with disabilities  
**Approach:** Remove barriers in environment; adjust society, not person  
**Where it shows up:** ADA (USA), Equality Act (UK), accessibility requirements  
**Strength:** Shifts focus from fixing person to fixing barriers  
**Limit:** Sometimes doesn't address real needs (pain management, etc.)  
**Countries:** Most wealthy Western countries; increasingly Global South

---

### Disability Justice Model (Current & Evolving)
**Sees disability as:** Natural human diversity; interconnected with all oppressions (racism, sexism, etc.)  
**Approach:** Center multiply-marginalized disabled people; demand systemic change  
**Where it shows up:** Disability justice organizations; intersectional movements; some newer laws  
**Strength:** Names how racism, sexism, etc. make disability worse; demands real justice  
**Focus:** Not just access, but liberation; connecting disability to all justice work  
**Countries:** Growing in disabled communities globally, especially among disabled people of color and Indigenous disabled people

## HOW DISABILITY RIGHTS LAWS WORK

### Types of Disability Rights Laws

**Civil Rights Laws**
- Prohibit discrimination
- Require reasonable accommodations
- Apply to employment, housing, education, public services
- Examples: ADA (US), Equality Act (UK), DDA (Australia)
- Your remedy: Sue for discrimination; file complaint with agency

**Education Laws**
- Require free appropriate education for disabled children
- Require individualized education plans
- Examples: IDEA (US), Education Acts (most countries)
- Your remedy: Due process hearings, complaints to education department

**Benefits Laws**
- Provide income support for disabled people
- Require healthcare coverage
- Examples: SSDI/SSI (US), disability allowances (Europe)
- Your remedy: Appeal process for denied benefits

**Accessibility Laws**
- Require buildings, websites, transportation accessible
- Require communication accessible (captions, interpreters)
- Examples: Accessibility Acts (EU, many countries)
- Your remedy: Complaints to accessibility offices; legal action

---

### How Rights Actually Work

**On Paper (What the law says):**
- You're protected from discrimination
- You deserve reasonable accommodations
- You can't be excluded from programs

**In Practice (What actually happens):**
- Sometimes people follow the law
- Sometimes people don't know the law
- Sometimes people break it anyway
- You have to know your rights to enforce them
- You may need to fight (complain, sue, organize)
- Some people have resources to fight; others don't

**Key insight:** Rights are only real if people enforce them. That's why:
- Knowing your rights matters
- Having legal help matters
- Organizing with other disabled people matters
- Public pressure/advocacy matters

## YOUR RIGHTS ACROSS LIFE DOMAINS

### EMPLOYMENT
You have the right to:
- Fair hiring practices (no discrimination because of disability)
- Keep your job despite having disability
- Reasonable accommodations to do your job
- Privacy about medical information
- Protection if you disclose disability

**Protected by:** ADA Title I (US), Equality Act (UK), national employment laws worldwide

---

### EDUCATION
You have the right to:
- Free, appropriate public education (K-12)
- Accommodations and modifications to access curriculum
- Individualized education plan (if needed)
- Same class placement as non-disabled peers when possible
- Higher education accommodations

**Protected by:** IDEA (US), Education Acts (most countries), CRPD (international)

---

### HOUSING
You have the right to:
- Live where you choose without discrimination
- Accessible housing or modifications to make housing accessible
- Refuse to live in institutions (right to community)
- Fair rental practices
- Public housing accommodations

**Protected by:** Fair Housing Act (US), Equality Act (UK), national housing laws worldwide

---

### HEALTHCARE
You have the right to:
- Access to healthcare (not denied because of disability)
- Informed consent (right to understand and choose treatment)
- Accessible communication (interpreters, large print)
- Privacy of medical information
- Respectful treatment

**Protected by:** ADA (US), Equality Act (UK), healthcare laws, patient rights globally

---

### TRANSPORTATION
You have the right to:
- Accessible public transportation
- Accessible air travel
- Accessible paratransit services (if disabled, can't use regular transit)
- Service animal access
- Accessible information about transportation

**Protected by:** ADA (US), ACAA, Equality Act (UK), transportation laws worldwide

---

### VOTING & POLITICAL PARTICIPATION
You have the right to:
- Vote (same right as every citizen)
- Accessible voting methods
- Run for office
- Participate in civic life
- Accessible information

**Protected by:** Voting Rights Act (US), Equality Act (UK), election laws worldwide, CRPD

---

### PRIVACY & MEDICAL INFORMATION
You have the right to:
- Confidentiality of medical information
- Say who knows about your disability
- Privacy in medical care
- Not be forced to disclose disability
- Access to your own medical records

**Protected by:** HIPAA (US), GDPR (EU), privacy laws worldwide

---

## WHEN YOUR RIGHTS ARE VIOLATED

### Common Violations
- Employer refusing to hire you because of disability
- School not providing agreed-upon accommodations
- Landlord refusing accessible apartment
- Website so inaccessible deaf/blind people can't use it
- Police using excessive force against person with mental illness
- Doctor refusing service because of disability
- Getting fired after disclosing disability

### What You Can Do
1. **Document it** — Write down what happened, when, who was involved, witnesses
2. **Report it** — Complain to employer, school, landlord (internal process)
3. **Escalate it** — File formal complaint with government agency
4. **Get help** — Contact disability rights organization or lawyer
5. **Sue it** — File lawsuit if other methods don't work

## DISABILITY RIGHTS WORLDWIDE

### Most Developed Frameworks
- **United States:** ADA comprehensive; lawsuits frequent; enforcement uneven
- **European Union:** Strong accessibility and anti-discrimination laws
- **United Kingdom:** Equality Act 2010; Human Rights Act
- **Australia:** DDA; NDIS unique funding model; growing rights consciousness

### Growing Frameworks
- **Canada:** Federal + provincial system; growing protections
- **India:** Persons with Disabilities Act 2016; large disabled population
- **Brazil:** Law of Inclusion; Latin American leader
- **South Africa:** Strong constitution; disability still emerging in practice

### Limited Frameworks
- **Many Global South countries:** No disability rights laws or minimal enforcement
- **Conflict zones:** Disability rights often lowest priority
- **Very poor countries:** Focus on survival; disability rights not addressed

## INTERSECTIONALITY & DISABILITY RIGHTS

Disability rights laws sometimes miss intersecting oppressions:

**Disabled people of color:**
- Face racism AND ableism
- Disability laws don't address racism
- Criminal justice system more brutal to disabled POC
- Healthcare discrimination compounds

**LGBTQ+ disabled people:**
- Face homophobia/transphobia AND ableism
- Some disability spaces not LGBTQ+-friendly
- Healthcare discrimination includes LGBTQ+ discrimination

**Undocumented disabled people:**
- Many protections don't apply to undocumented people
- Fear of deportation prevents accessing rights
- Intersectional discrimination (immigration + disability)

**Disabled poor people:**
- Harder to fight discrimination without resources
- Benefits system often poverty trap
- Can't afford lawyers
- Benefits system treats disabled poor with suspicion

**Disabled women:**
- Face sexism AND ableism
- Sexual violence against disabled women not taken seriously
- Reproductive freedom complicated for disabled women
- Healthcare discrimination often gendered

## DISABILITY JUSTICE: EXPANDING RIGHTS FRAMEWORK

Disability justice goes beyond traditional disability rights. It asks:

**Not just:** "Can disabled people access buildings?"  
**But:** "Who designed this building? Who decided accessibility? Were disabled people involved?"

**Not just:** "Can disabled people work?"  
**But:** "What are working conditions? Is it exploitative? Are disabled people paid fairly? Do disabled people have power?"

**Not just:** "Do disabled people have accommodations?"  
**But:** "Why is society structured to need accommodations? Can we build different?"

**Disability justice connects:**
- Disability rights + racial justice + economic justice + gender justice + queer justice

**Disability justice centers:**
- Multiply-marginalized disabled people (disabled POC, LGBTQ+ disabled, disabled immigrants, disabled trans, etc.)
- Community/collective rather than individual rights
- Systemic change, not just individual accommodations
- Joy and culture alongside legal rights

---

## KEY PRINCIPLES ACROSS DISABILITY RIGHTS FRAMEWORKS

Regardless of specific laws in your country, disability rights are based on:

✓ **Equality** — Disabled people equal to non-disabled; no second-class citizenship  
✓ **Dignity** — Respect and worth as human beings  
✓ **Autonomy** — Right to make decisions about own life  
✓ **Inclusion** — Right to participate in community, not segregated  
✓ **Accessibility** — Right to access information, buildings, services  
✓ **Non-discrimination** — Can't be treated worse because of disability  
✓ **Accommodation** — Society adjusts to include us, not vice versa  
✓ **Community** — Right to live, work, learn, worship with non-disabled people  
✓ **Voice** — Disabled people's participation in decisions affecting us  
✓ **Justice** — Connection to racial justice, gender justice, economic justice

## THE DISABILITY RIGHTS MOVEMENT

Disability rights didn't happen by accident. Disabled people fought for them.

**Major milestone:**
- **1960s-1970s:** Independent Living Movement (disabled people demanding to live in community, not institutions)
- **1977:** Section 504 sit-in (disabled people camping in federal building until disability rights protections issued)
- **1990:** ADA passed (US; largest civil rights victory since Civil Rights Act)
- **1996:** CRPD negotiated and eventually (2006) came into force
- **2006+:** Global growth of disability rights laws

**Still fighting for:**
- Global South disability rights protections
- Better enforcement of existing laws
- Disability justice (beyond access to justice)
- Disability rights for undocumented disabled people
- Intersection with other justice movements
