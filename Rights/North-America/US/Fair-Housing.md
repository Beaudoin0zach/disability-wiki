# FAIR HOUSING ACT

The Fair Housing Act is the federal law protecting disabled people from discrimination in housing. It requires accessible housing, reasonable accommodations, and prohibits discrimination based on disability in all aspects of housing.

## WHAT IS THE FAIR HOUSING ACT?

The **Fair Housing Act** (FHA) is federal law that:
- Prohibits discrimination in housing based on disability (and other protected categories)
- Requires reasonable accommodations for disabled people
- Requires accessible design for new multifamily buildings
- Requires modifications for accessibility (landlord pays, with limits)
- Applies to landlords, property managers, real estate agents, housing programs
- Applies to rental housing, home sales, HOAs, condos, assisted living, etc.

**Core principle:** Disabled people have the right to live where they choose and have equal access to housing.

---

## KEY CONCEPTS

### DISABILITY DEFINITION
Same as ADA/Section 504:
- Physical or mental impairment substantially limiting major life activity
- Record of such impairment
- Regarded as having such impairment

**Covers:** Mobility disabilities, sensory disabilities, neurodivergence, mental health, chronic illness, hidden disabilities, etc.

---

### REASONABLE ACCOMMODATIONS
Modifications needed for disabled person to use housing equally:

**Common accommodations:**
- Accessible parking space
- Ramp instead of stairs
- Grab bars in bathroom
- Accessible entrance
- Accessible kitchen/bathroom modifications
- Allowing service animal
- Accessible alarm systems
- Modifications for hearing aid compatibility

**Landlord responsibility:** Pay for accommodations unless undue financial burden (high threshold)

---

### ACCESSIBILITY FEATURES
New multifamily buildings (4+ units) must have:
- Accessible entrances and routes
- Accessible common use circulation paths
- Accessible parking
- Accessible units (doors, hallways, bathrooms, kitchens)
- Communication systems accessible to Deaf/blind residents

**Note:** Applies only to NEW construction; existing buildings covered by reasonable accommodation requirement.

---

## WHAT FAIR HOUSING ACT PROHIBITS

### Direct Discrimination
- Refusing to rent/sell because of disability
- Different terms or conditions (higher price, stricter requirements)
- Refusing reasonable accommodations
- Refusing service animals
- Segregating disabled people

### Less Obvious Discrimination
- Making inquiries about disability not asked of non-disabled people
- Refusing to provide information in accessible format
- Denying credit because of disability
- Excessive deposits or requirements

---

## YOUR RIGHTS UNDER FHA

### Right to Reasonable Accommodations
- Request modifications needed for you to use housing equally
- Landlord must provide unless undue financial/administrative burden

### Right to Service Animals
- Landlord can't refuse service animal (even if "no pets" policy)
- Can ask if it's a service animal and what disability-related tasks
- Can't charge pet deposit/fee for service animal
- Can't require training certification
- Emotional support animals may be covered (different rules apply)

### Right to Accessible Information
- Requests for information in accessible format (large print, Braille, digital, ASL interpreter)
- Landlord can't charge extra

### Right to Privacy
- Landlord can't disclose disability information
- Can't require disclosure beyond what's necessary to accommodate

### Right to Non-Discrimination in Financing
- Can't deny mortgage based on disability
- Can't charge different rates
- Can't require additional documentation

## COMMON FAIR HOUSING SITUATIONS

### REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION REQUEST PROCESS

**Step 1: Request in Writing**
- Put request in writing (creates record)
- Explain what accommodation needed
- Explain how it helps with disability

**Step 2: Provide Documentation**
- May be asked to provide documentation of disability
- Landlord can ask:
  - Verification that you have disability
  - Functional limitation related to housing
  - How accommodation helps
- Landlord typically can't ask diagnosis or full medical history

**Step 3: Landlord Response**
- Landlord must respond in reasonable timeframe
- Can approve, request more info, or deny with reason
- If undue burden claimed, must explain why

**Step 4: If Denied**
- Ask for written explanation
- Contact Fair Housing organization
- File complaint if appropriate

---

### SERVICE ANIMAL vs. PET

**Service Animal (covered by FHA):**
- Trained to perform disability-related tasks
- Legally considered assistance animal, not pet
- No deposit/fee required
- Landlord must allow even with "no pets" policy

**Emotional Support Animal (coverage varies):**
- Provides comfort by presence (not trained tasks)
- May be covered under FHA as reasonable accommodation
- May require documentation
- Landlord can charge pet deposit (varies by interpretation)

**Pet (NOT covered):**
- Owned for companionship only
- Not trained for tasks
- Landlord can refuse/charge deposit

---

### ACCESSIBILITY MODIFICATIONS

**Common modifications:**
- Ramp for wheelchair access
- Grab bars in bathroom
- Accessible parking space
- Lowered light switches
- Accessible counter height
- Accessible bathroom fixtures
- Door widening
- Exterior modifications

**Who pays:** Usually landlord pays (it's reasonable accommodation)

**Exceptions:** Undue financial burden if modification costs exceed percentage of annual rent or landlord's costs (high threshold)

**Tenant responsibility:** May need to restore to original condition upon moving (varies)

## PROTECTED CATEGORIES (Beyond Disability)

Fair Housing Act also prohibits discrimination based on:
- Race/color
- National origin
- Religion
- Sex/gender
- Sexual orientation
- Gender identity
- Familial status (families with children)
- **Disability** ← Our focus here

**Intersectionality consideration:** Disabled people of color, LGBTQ+ disabled people, etc. protected under multiple categories.

---

## STRENGTHS OF FAIR HOUSING ACT

- **Broad coverage** — Applies to most housing
- **Requires accessibility** — New construction must have accessible features
- **Requires accommodations** — Landlord must pay for modifications
- **Clear enforcement** — HUD investigates; can sue
- **Service animal protections** — Can't prohibit
- **Communication access** — Can't charge for accessible format
- **Individual complaint option** — Can file complaint or sue

---

## CHALLENGES WITH FAIR HOUSING ACT

- **Private properties sometimes exempt** — Owner-occupied buildings with 4 or fewer units
- **Proof burden on tenant** — Must document discrimination
- **Enforcement slow** — HUD investigations take time
- **Accessibility requirements only for new construction** — Existing buildings exempt from accessibility standards (but not reasonable accommodations)
- **"Undue burden" interpreted inconsistently** — Some landlords claim burden without justification
- **Doesn't cover all aspects** — HOA common areas sometimes covered differently
- **Overlapping laws can be confusing** — FHA, ADA, Section 504 all apply; sometimes unclear which

## FINDING ACCESSIBLE HOUSING

### Search Strategies
- Use accessible housing databases
- Contact Independent Living Centers (have lists)
- Ask in disability communities (peer recommendations)
- Work with real estate agent familiar with accessibility
- Contact HUD for public housing with accessibility

### Evaluating Accessibility
- [[Accessibility Features Checklist]]
- Test wheelchair access (if relevant)
- Check common areas
- Test noise levels (for hearing aid users)
- Check lighting (for low vision)
- Verify accessible parking nearby

## IF YOU EXPERIENCE FAIR HOUSING DISCRIMINATION

### Documentation
- Write down what happened, when, who, what was said
- Keep all communications
- Take photos of barriers
- Document any health impacts

### Reporting
- Complain to property management in writing
- Contact local fair housing organization
- File HUD complaint (within 1 year)
- Consider lawsuit (within 2 years)

## DISABILITY TYPES & FAIR HOUSING

### MOBILITY DISABILITIES
**Common accommodations:** Accessible parking, ramp, grab bars, accessible layout  
**Strength:** Strong protections; clearly recognized

### DEAF/HARD OF HEARING
**Common accommodations:** Visual alarm systems, accessible phone systems  
**Strength:** Clear protections for communication access

### BLIND/LOW VISION
**Common accommodations:** Guide dog access, accessible information, tactile features  
**Strength:** Good protections; well-established accommodations

### NEURODIVERGENT
**Common accommodations:** Sensory modifications (lighting, sound), flexible lease terms  
**Strength:** Growing recognition; some resistance

### MENTAL HEALTH DISABILITIES
**Common accommodations:** Service animal, accessibility to public transit  
**Strength:** Improving; still faces stigma

### CHRONIC ILLNESS
**Common accommodations:** Accessible parking, flexible policies, near transit  
**Strength:** Growing recognition
