# ADA: AMERICANS WITH DISABILITIES ACT

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) is the most comprehensive disability rights law in the United States. Signed in 1990, it protects disabled people from discrimination in employment, public services, transportation, and more.

## WHAT IS THE ADA?

The **Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)** is a federal civil rights law that:
- Prohibits discrimination against disabled people
- Requires "reasonable accommodations" in employment, education, housing, transportation
- Requires accessibility in buildings and digital spaces
- Applies to private businesses, governments, and organizations (with some exceptions)
- Was passed in 1990 and amended in 2008

**Basic principle:** Disabled people have the same rights as non-disabled people. Society must remove barriers to make this possible.

**Quote from the law:** "Disability is a natural part of the human experience and in no way diminishes the right of individuals to live independently, enjoy self-determination, make choices, contribute to their communities, and enjoy full participation in society."

---

## HISTORICAL CONTEXT: WHY ADA WAS NEEDED

### Before ADA (Before 1990)
- No federal law protected disabled people from discrimination
- Disabled people regularly denied jobs solely because of disability
- No requirement for accessible buildings
- No requirement for accommodations in schools or workplaces
- Disabled people could be denied service in restaurants, hotels, etc.
- Deaf people had no captioning; blind people no accessible technology
- Institutionalization seen as normal

### The Fight for ADA
**1960s-1980s:** Disability rights movement grew
- Independent Living Centers demanded community living (not institutions)
- Disabled people occupied buildings, protested, organized
- Disabled activists demonstrated that accessibility is possible

**1977: Section 504 Sit-in**
- Disabled people occupied federal building for 28 days
- Demanded federal office implement Section 504 (earlier civil rights law)
- First major disability rights protest; galvanized movement

**1988: Deaf President Now**
- Deaf students at Gallaudet University protested when school hired hearing president
- Demanded Deaf president for Deaf university
- Victory showed Deaf community power; inspired ADA push

**1990: ADA Signed**
- President George H.W. Bush signed ADA into law
- Disabled activists and allies celebrated outside
- Changed landscape for disabled Americans

### Why ADA Was Revolutionary
- First time federal law explicitly protected disabled people from discrimination
- Moved from "charity" model to "rights" model
- Legally required accommodations (not optional)
- Applied broadly (employment, education, public services, businesses)
- Included "undue hardship" exception to prevent bankrupting businesses
- Meant disabled people could work, go to school, participate in community

## FIVE TITLES OF THE ADA

ADA has five main sections, each covering different areas:

### TITLE I: EMPLOYMENT (1-15 employees)
**Who it applies to:** Private employers with 15+ employees; federal government; labor unions; employment agencies

**What it requires:**
- Non-discrimination in hiring, firing, pay, promotion
- Reasonable accommodations so qualified disabled people can work
- Confidentiality of medical information
- Accessible job applications and interviews

**Example:** Disabled person uses wheelchair; employer must provide accessible parking, ramp, accessible bathroom. If wheelchair-accessible office undue hardship, employer explores alternatives.

**Your remedy:** File charge with EEOC within 180-300 days (varies by state)

**Does it apply to:** Most private jobs; federal jobs; labor unions

**Doesn't apply to:** Churches; very small businesses (fewer than 15 employees)

---

### TITLE II: PUBLIC SERVICES & PUBLIC TRANSPORTATION
**Who it applies to:** State and local government agencies; public transit systems

**What it requires:**
- Non-discrimination in all government services
- Accessible public transportation (buses, trains, etc.)
- Accessible government buildings
- Accessible meetings, hearings, services
- Auxiliary aids (interpreters, captions, readers)

**Example:** Blind person applying for driver's license; DMV must provide accessible application, reader, or assistant. City transit must make buses accessible.

**Your remedy:** File complaint with relevant federal agency or file lawsuit

**Does it apply to:** Schools, public universities, government offices, public transportation

**Doesn't apply to:** Private transportation (Uber, airlines—covered under Title III)

---

### TITLE III: PUBLIC ACCOMMODATIONS & PRIVATE BUSINESSES
**Who it applies to:** Private businesses, nonprofits, other organizations serving public (1-15 employees exempted)

**What it requires:**
- Non-discrimination in public accommodations (restaurants, hotels, stores, etc.)
- Accessible buildings (ramps, elevators, accessible bathrooms)
- Accessible information (large print, Braille, digital)
- Auxiliary aids (interpreters, captions)
- Service animal access

**Example:** Restaurant doesn't deny service because person is deaf. Must provide interpreter or CART (real-time captioning). Store must be accessible to wheelchair users.

**Your remedy:** File complaint with DOJ or file private lawsuit

**Does it apply to:** Restaurants, hotels, stores, healthcare offices, theaters, museums, etc.

**Doesn't apply to:** Private clubs; buildings 3+ floors without common use elevator (some exemptions)

---

### TITLE IV: TELECOMMUNICATIONS
**Who it applies to:** Telephone companies

**What it requires:**
- Relay services for Deaf/hard of hearing and speech disabled people
- Captioning for video relay services
- 711 relay number accessible to all

**Example:** Deaf person calls relay service (711), types message, relay operator speaks to hearing person, hears their response back in text.

**Your remedy:** File complaint with FCC

**Applies to:** All telephone services

---

### TITLE V: MISCELLANEOUS
**Who it applies to:** Various

**What it includes:**
- Confidentiality protections
- Insurance rules
- Architectural/transportation standards
- Technical assistance
- Limitations on damages

## KEY CONCEPTS IN ADA

### DISABILITY DEFINITION
ADA protects people with:
- Physical or mental impairments substantially limiting major life activities
- Record of such impairment
- Regarded as having such impairment

**Major life activities include:**
- Walking, hearing, seeing, breathing, working, learning, thinking, concentrating
- Eating, sleeping, communicating, performing manual tasks
- Caring for oneself, interacting with others, recreational activities

**Important:** You don't have to have a diagnosed condition. If you have symptoms substantially limiting daily activities, you may be protected.

**Note:** 2008 ADA Amendments expanded definition to include more people. It's broader now than it was in 1990s.

---

### DISABILITY TYPES COVERED
ADA covers:

✓ **Physical disabilities** (mobility, dexterity, pain)  
✓ **Sensory disabilities** (Deaf, blind, deafblind)  
✓ **Neurological disabilities** (brain injury, cerebral palsy, MS, Parkinson's)  
✓ **Neurodivergence** (autism, ADHD, learning disabilities—after 2008 expansion)  
✓ **Mental health disabilities** (depression, anxiety, bipolar, schizophrenia—increasingly recognized)  
✓ **Chronic illness** (ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, lupus, diabetes—if symptoms substantial)  
✓ **Hidden/invisible disabilities** (increasingly protected post-2008)  
✓ **HIV/AIDS** (explicitly covered)  
✓ **Cancer** (explicitly covered)  
✓ **Substance abuse disorder** (if in recovery or perceived as having it)  

**NOT covered:**
- Currently using illegal drugs (excluding marijuana in some states)
- Sexual behavior disorders
- Gender identity disorders
- Pedophilia

---

### QUALIFIED INDIVIDUAL
You're protected if you're a "qualified individual with a disability":
1. **You have disability** (as defined above)
2. **You can do the job/access the program** with reasonable accommodations

**Important:** You don't have to be able to do the job without accommodations. You have to be able to do it WITH accommodations.

**Example:** Blind person can do data entry job with screen reader software (reasonable accommodation). They're a qualified individual even though they need accommodation.

---

### REASONABLE ACCOMMODATIONS
An accommodation is "reasonable" if it:
- Enables qualified person to do the job/access program
- Doesn't cause undue hardship to employer/organization
- Is appropriate to the circumstances

**Common accommodations:**
- Flexible schedule
- Modified job duties
- Telework/work from home
- Accessible parking
- Ramp/elevator access
- Interpreter or CART captioning
- Large print documents
- Screen reader software
- Extended test time
- Written rather than verbal instructions
- Allowing service animal or emotional support animal

**Undue hardship exceptions:**
Employer doesn't have to provide accommodation if:
- Cost is significant relative to business size
- Would fundamentally alter program/job
- Would be extremely disruptive

**Important:** Many accommodations cost little. Cost alone doesn't prove undue hardship.

## STRENGTHS OF ADA

### What ADA Does Really Well
- **Makes accommodations legal right, not request** — Disabled people have leverage
- **Covers many areas** — Employment, education, transportation, public services, buildings
- **Broad disability definition** (after 2008) — Includes invisible disabilities, neurodivergence, mental health
- **Has enforcement mechanisms** — EEOC, DOJ, private lawsuits available
- **Case law keeps expanding it** — Courts often interpret broadly to benefit disabled people
- **Changed culture** — Even where unenforced, creates expectation of accessibility
- **Covers employment, which is critical** — Allows disabled people economic independence

### Practical Wins
- Employers legally required to hire qualified disabled people
- Schools legally required to provide accommodations
- Businesses legally required to be accessible
- Public transit legally required to be accessible
- Deaf people able to participate via interpreters/CART
- Blind people able to access technology
- Millions of disabled Americans have jobs because of ADA

## CHALLENGES & GAPS IN ADA

### What ADA Doesn't Do Well

**1. "Substantially Limits" is vague**
- Before 2008, narrow interpretation meant few people protected
- Even after 2008 expansion, courts sometimes interpret restrictively
- If symptoms only sometimes limit activities, might not qualify

**2. Undue hardship exception is weak protection**
- Employers often claim hardship without proving it
- Courts don't always scrutinize claims carefully
- Large companies rarely have legitimate hardship claims

**3. Enforcement depends on disabled person taking action**
- EEOC complaint requires burden of proof
- Private lawsuit is expensive; many can't afford
- Some employers retaliate (though illegal)
- Disabled people often have limited time/energy for legal battles

**4. Doesn't cover small businesses**
- Businesses with fewer than 15 employees exempt from Title I
- Means many disabled people unprotected at work

**5. Medical model bias remains**
- Focus on "can you do the job" requires medical documentation
- Some disabilities (especially neurodivergence, mental health) hard to prove
- Requires disability diagnosis; undocumented immigrants often excluded

**6. Invisible disabilities harder to protect**
- Even post-2008, requires proving "substantial limitation"
- Harder to prove than visible disabilities
- Still discrimination and dismissal, but harder to prove

**7. Doesn't address root causes**
- Accommodations model assumes something's "wrong" with person, not society
- True accessibility would build it in, not require accommodations

**8. Intersectionality gaps**
- Doesn't address racism + ableism simultaneously
- Doesn't address gender discrimination alongside disability discrimination
- Doesn't protect undocumented disabled people

**9. Limited scope**
- Doesn't cover all employers (federal contractors have different requirements)
- Doesn't cover housing (Fair Housing Act covers that, but different law)
- Doesn't cover insurance (separate rules)

## HOW TO USE ADA IF YOUR RIGHTS ARE VIOLATED

### Step 1: Document Everything
- Date, time, what happened
- Who was involved
- What was said
- Witnesses
- Any written communications

### Step 2: Report It
- Tell employer/organization what happened
- Request accommodation in writing
- Keep copies of all communications

### Step 3: File Formal Complaint
**If employment (Title I):**
- File charge with EEOC within 180-300 days (varies by state)
- EEOC investigates
- Can request "right to sue" letter
- Then file private lawsuit if desired

**If public services (Title II):**
- Complain to relevant agency first (usually required)
- Can file lawsuit against government

**If public accommodation (Title III):**
- DOJ can investigate and sue
- You can file private lawsuit
- No administrative process required

### Step 4: Get Legal Help
- Legal aid organizations (free)
- Disability rights organizations (sometimes free)
- Lawyers (expensive but sometimes take on contingency—paid from settlement)

## DISABILITY TYPES & HOW ADA APPLIES

### MOBILITY DISABILITIES
**Covered?** Yes, extensively.  
**Examples:** Wheelchair users, people using canes, people with chronic pain affecting walking  
**Protections:** Ramps, elevators, accessible parking, modified job duties  
**Strength of protection:** Strong; mobility disabilities most recognized under ADA

---

### DEAF/HARD OF HEARING
**Covered?** Yes, explicitly.  
**Examples:** Deaf people, CODA (children of deaf adults), people with hearing loss  
**Protections:** Interpreters, CART captioning, video relay services, TTY devices  
**Strength of protection:** Strong for communication access; weaker for other issues

---

### BLIND/LOW VISION
**Covered?** Yes, explicitly.  
**Examples:** Blind people, people with legal blindness, low vision, tunnel vision  
**Protections:** Screen readers, Braille, accessible electronic documents, guides, magnification  
**Strength of protection:** Strong; significant technology advances help

---

### NEURODIVERGENT (After 2008 Expansion)
**Covered?** Yes, but sometimes requires fight.  
**Examples:** Autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia  
**Protections:** Extended test time, written instructions, sensory accommodations, remote work  
**Strength of protection:** Growing; still more challenging than mobility disabilities

---

### PSYCHOSOCIAL/MENTAL HEALTH
**Covered?** Yes, but requires documentation.  
**Examples:** Depression, anxiety, bipolar disorder, PTSD, schizophrenia  
**Protections:** Schedule flexibility, break access, reduced hours, mental health days  
**Strength of protection:** Improving but still faces stigma; requires diagnosis

---

### HIDDEN/INVISIBLE DISABILITIES
**Covered?** Yes, increasingly; but burden of proof higher.  
**Examples:** Chronic pain, chronic fatigue, episodic conditions  
**Protections:** Flexible schedule, remote work, rest breaks, modified duties  
**Strength of protection:** Depends on specific condition; still fighting for recognition

---

### MULTIPLY-MARGINALIZED DISABLED PEOPLE
**Coverage considerations:**
- Disabled people of color: ADA doesn't address racism; racial discrimination + ableism happens
- LGBTQ+ disabled: ADA doesn't address anti-LGBTQ+ discrimination specifically
- Disabled immigrants: Limited protections if undocumented
- Disabled poor people: Can't afford to fight violations

**Intersectionality gap:** ADA provides legal frameworks but doesn't address how multiple oppressions combine.

---

## GLOBAL PERSPECTIVE: ADA & INTERNATIONAL CONTEXT

### How ADA Compares
**Stronger than:**
- Most Global South disability laws (limited/no enforcement)
- No law at all (many countries)
- Medical model-only approaches

**Weaker than:**
- Some EU accessibility requirements
- Some cultural/community-based approaches in Indigenous frameworks
- Some countries with more aggressive enforcement

### International Influence
- ADA inspired similar laws in other countries
- CRPD (UN convention) drawn from ADA model
- 180+ countries adopted CRPD (though implementation varies)

### Limitations of ADA Model
- Individual complaint-based system; slow
- Doesn't address poverty/economic justice
- Assumes Western legal system; doesn't work in countries without strong courts
- Focused on individual accommodations, not systemic change
- Doesn't work for undocumented disabled people
