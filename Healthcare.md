# Health & Care: Overview

Healthcare is essential for disabled people, but systems are often inaccessible, discriminatory, and confusing. This section explains how to navigate medical care, find accessible providers, and advocate for your health rights.

## Historical Origins and Movement Leadership

Disabled people historically excluded from healthcare systems. Doctors assumed disability meant "not worth treating." Eugenics movement explicitly worked to deny medical care to disabled people.

Modern disability healthcare rights emerged from civil rights movements. Section 504 protests (1977) included demand for accessible healthcare. Activists fought for recognition that disabled people deserve medical treatment, accommodations, and respectful care.

Deaf culture resisted medical "cure" narrative—organized to be culturally recognized, not medically fixed. Gained right to interpreters, culturally Deaf providers. Model for other disabilities.

Disability justice and peer support movements emphasize disabled people knowing our bodies and needs better than doctors. Pushback against medical paternalism—doctors deciding what's "best" without our input.

Today: Healthcare remains inadequate. Long waits for appointments, inaccessible buildings, providers unfamiliar with disabilities, discrimination, and cost barriers continue. Organizing demands accessible, respectful, affordable care.

## What Healthcare Means for Disabled People

Healthcare is different for disabled people than non-disabled people:

**Medical Management:** Ongoing care for disability conditions (doctor visits, medications, therapies, monitoring).

**Preventive Care:** Screenings, vaccinations, check-ups to catch problems early.

**Acute Care:** Emergency treatment for immediate medical needs.

**Chronic Condition Management:** Long-term support for conditions that won't "go away."

**Mental Health Care:** Therapy, psychiatry, peer support for mental health disabilities.

**Rehabilitation:** Therapy to maintain or improve function (physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy).

**Assistive Technology:** Equipment and devices enabling participation (wheelchairs, hearing aids, speech-to-text, etc.).

**Accessibility:** Physical access to care (building accessibility, interpreter services, accessible communication, flexible scheduling).

## Healthcare System Components

**Primary Care Providers (PCPs):** Your main doctor—usually general practice. First contact for most health issues. Coordinates specialist referrals.

**Specialists:** Doctors focused on specific areas (cardiology, neurology, rheumatology, psychiatry, etc.).

**Hospitals:** Inpatient care for serious illness/injury, surgery, emergency.

**Clinics:** Outpatient care for specific needs (family planning, mental health, diabetes, etc.).

**Therapies:** Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, mental health therapy.

**Pharmacies:** Medication access.

**Home Health:** Care providers visiting your home (nursing, personal care, therapy).

**Emergency Care:** 911, emergency rooms for immediate life-threatening situations.

**Telehealth:** Phone and video appointments (increasingly common, accessibility varies).

## Insurance: The Barrier

Healthcare in US is insurance-based. Without insurance, care expensive or unavailable.

**Insurance types:**

- **Medicare:** Federal health insurance (disabled, 65+, ESRD, ALS)
- **Medicaid:** State health insurance (low-income people)
- **Private Insurance:** Employer-based or individual purchase
- **Veterans Healthcare:** VA system for veterans
- **Indian Health Services:** For federally recognized tribes
- **Charity Care:** Hospitals required to provide some uninsured care

**Underinsurance:** Having insurance but insufficient coverage. High deductibles, copays, limited provider networks, don't-cover services all leave disabled people struggling.

**Cost Barriers Despite Insurance:**

Copays, deductibles, coinsurance add up. Many disabled people can't afford prescribed treatments despite insurance.

Medications cost $100–500+/month (even with insurance).

Therapies often hit visit limits (30–60 visits/year) before deductible met.

Disabled people often must choose: rent or medication? Food or therapy?

## Core Healthcare Challenges for Disabled People

### Inaccessible Healthcare Settings

Physical access problems:

- Narrow hallways, doorways not wheelchair-accessible
- No accessible bathrooms
- Long waits, no places to sit (hard for people with pain, fatigue)
- Loud environments (overwhelming for deaf, neurodivergent people)
- No interpreter services (Deaf people)
- No accessible communication (Blind people, deaf-blind people)
- No fragrance-free environments (chemically sensitive disabled people)

### Provider Discrimination and Ignorance

Many providers:

- Don't believe disability is real (especially invisible disabilities)
- Blame disability on mental health ("it's all in your head")
- Refuse to treat disabled people
- Assume disabled people want "cure"
- Don't listen to disabled people about their bodies
- Have never learned about specific disabilities
- Ask inappropriate questions (inspiration porn, "how do you have sex," "aren't you depressed")
- Dismiss disabled people's medical concerns
- Refuse to provide accommodations

### Communication Barriers

Disabled people struggle to communicate with healthcare providers:

- Deaf people: No interpreters provided or poor quality
- Blind people: No Braille, large print, electronic materials
- Neurodivergent people: Inability to describe symptoms in ways providers understand
- People with speech disabilities: Providers don't give time, dismiss concerns
- Non-English speakers: No interpreters
- Cognitively disabled people: Complex explanations, rushed appointments

### Cost Barriers

Even with insurance:

- Medications unaffordable
- Therapy visits limited before max reached
- Deductibles too high
- Insurance denies coverage for necessary equipment
- Disabled people can't work full-time, less access to employer insurance
- Pre-existing condition discrimination (now illegal but still happens informally)

### Medical Trauma and Institutional Abuse

Healthcare history for many disabled people includes:

- Non-consensual treatment or procedures
- Abuse in institutions (group homes, hospitals, nursing homes)
- Forced institutionalization
- Forced sterilization (historically and still occurring)
- Conversion therapy (LGBTQ+ disabled people)
- Medical experimentation without consent
- Trauma from disrespectful, dismissive providers

Many disabled people have medical PTSD affecting healthcare access today.

## Why Healthcare Matters

**Survival:** Without healthcare, disabled people die from treatable conditions.

**Function:** With healthcare (medication, therapy, equipment), disabled people can participate in work, family, community.

**Dignity:** Respectful medical care that listens and accommodates affirms disabled people's right to exist.

**Prevention:** Regular care catches problems early, prevents complications.

**Mental Health:** Medical care for mental health disabilities as essential as physical.

## Strengths and Breakthroughs

**Telemedicine Expansion:** COVID-19 forced healthcare providers online. Accessibility varied but increased options for disabled people unable to travel.

**Peer Support Models:** Growing recognition that disabled people helping other disabled people (peer support specialists, peer health navigators) effective and important.

**Disability-Aware Providers Growing:** Some healthcare providers specialize in disability care, understand accommodations, employ disabled staff.

**Patient Rights Recognition:** Legal framework now (ADA, Section 504) requires healthcare accessibility (though enforcement weak).

**Mental Health Destigmatization:** Growing recognition that mental health disabilities as serious as physical (though still undertreated).

**Assistive Technology Advances:** Technology improving faster than ever (screen readers, mobility aids, communication devices, accessible apps).

**Telehealth Infrastructure:** Even though not universally accessible, infrastructure now exists for remote appointments.

## Challenges, Nuances, and Critiques

**Medical System Rooted in Paternalism:** Doctor decides what's "best." Disabled people's expertise about their own bodies not respected.

**Cure Focus Instead of Acceptance:** Medicine tries to "fix" disabilities. Many disabilities not fixable. Need shifts to acceptance, accommodation, function.

**Ableism Embedded:** Healthcare providers trained in ableist frameworks. Assume disability = problem needing solving.

**Financial Barriers Systemic:** Healthcare profit-driven. Disabled people poor. System designed for wealthy.

**Inequitable Access:** Healthcare disparities by race, immigration, geography, class, disability type all exist and compound.

**Insurance Tied to Employment:** US system ties health insurance to jobs. Disabled people can't work full-time, lose insurance.

**Global South Healthcare Crisis:** Most disabled people globally have zero healthcare access. Organizing focuses on basic existence.

**Informal Economy Excluded:** Gig workers, self-employed, cash workers have no employer insurance. High-risk population.

**Mental Health Underfunded:** Mental health care often not covered or severely limited by insurance.

**Long-Term Care Crisis:** Aging disabled population needs care. Nursing homes often abuse. Community alternatives underfunded.

## Disability Type Accessibility Considerations

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**

Healthcare right: Video Remote Interpreters (VRI) or in-person interpreters at all medical appointments (legally required under ADA).

Challenge: Many providers don't provide interpreters or provide low-quality. Must request explicitly and advocate.

Communication: Can use ASL, written communication, lip reading (state preference).

**Blind/Low Vision:**

Healthcare right: Accessible information materials (Braille, large print, electronic).

Challenge: Many medical forms, instructions not accessible.

Communication: Screen reader compatible forms, verbal explanations, guides during appointments.

**Neurodivergent:**

Healthcare challenge: Describing symptoms in ways providers understand difficult. Executive function barriers (making appointments, remembering to take meds, attending appointments).

Strategy: Written symptom logs, appointment reminders, extra time, plain language explanations.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health Disability:**

Healthcare challenge: Stigma. Providers doubt mental health issues. Medical trauma common.

Strategy: Trusted providers, peer support, trauma-informed care.

**Hidden/Invisible Disabilities:**

Healthcare challenge: Providers don't believe disability exists. Proving disability medically necessary but exhausting.

Strategy: Seek informed providers, get documentation, bring records.

**Mobility Disabilities:**

Healthcare challenge: Physical accessibility barriers (stairs, narrow doorways, no accessible bathrooms, parking too far).

Strategy: Request accessible exam rooms, bring mobility aids, schedule longer appointments.

**Multiply-Marginalized:**

Healthcare challenge: Intersecting discrimination (racism, sexism, homophobia, transphobia + ableism).

Strategy: Seek culturally competent providers, bring advocate, document discrimination.

## How Organizers and Groups Can Apply It

**Advocate for Accessible Healthcare:**

- Push provider offices for accessibility (interpreters, accessible bathrooms, fragrance-free environments)
- Document healthcare discrimination (photos, written accounts)
- File ADA complaints when providers refuse accommodations
- Organize campaigns demanding accessible healthcare

**Build Peer Support:**

- Train disabled peer health navigators
- Create peer support groups for specific disabilities
- Develop resources disabled people create for disabled people
- Recognize disabled people as experts about their conditions

**Fight Medical Paternalism:**

- Affirm disabled people's right to make own healthcare decisions
- Oppose forced treatment or procedures
- Organize against institutionalization
- Support informed consent and shared decision-making

**Address Mental Health Crisis:**

- Advocate for mental health care funding
- Destigmatize mental health disabilities
- Create peer support mental health models
- Fight psychiatric coercion

**Center Global South Healthcare:**

- Organize internationally for healthcare access
- Share strategies across countries
- Recognize informal economy contexts
- Support traditional/community healthcare models

## Global Perspective & Intersectionality

**Western Context:** United States, Canada, European countries have insurance systems (though inaccessible, expensive, discriminatory). Healthcare exists but often unaffordable. Access varies by insurance type.

**Global South Context:** 80% of disabled people live in countries with minimal healthcare access. Disabled people often cannot afford any healthcare. Organize for basic care existence. Traditional healers and community care more important than formal medical systems.

**Informal Economy Impact:** Gig workers, self-employed, cash economy workers have no employer healthcare. Without employer insurance, must buy private (expensive) or go uninsured (risky).

**Intersectionality Considerations:**

**Gender:** Women with disabilities face reproductive discrimination, maternal mortality barriers, gender-based violence intersection with disability.

**Race:** Disabled people of color face medical racism (providers dismiss concerns, assume drug-seeking, don't take pain seriously). Historical medical racism (Tuskegee, forced sterilization of disabled women of color) creates justified medical mistrust.

**Immigration:** Undocumented disabled immigrants often cannot access healthcare (fear of deportation, cost). Chronic stress of legal precarity affects health.

**LGBTQ+:** Disabled LGBTQ+ people face homophobia, transphobia + ableism. Healthcare providers discriminate. Conversion therapy targeting LGBTQ+ disabled youth.

**Class:** Poor disabled people cannot afford healthcare even with insurance. Choose food or medications. Work while sick due to poverty.

**Age:** Disabled youth denied recognition of disabilities. Disabled elders offered institutionalization instead of community care.
