# Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

The Americans with Disabilities Act is the most comprehensive civil rights law protecting disabled people in the United States. Signed into law in 1990, it was won through decades of organizing by disabled people—including protests, sit-ins, and the famous Capitol Crawl.

This page centers disabled people's expertise on using the ADA to protect your rights.

---

## What the ADA Does

### Overview

The ADA prohibits discrimination against disabled people in:
- Employment (Title I)
- State and local government services (Title II)
- Public accommodations—private businesses open to the public (Title III)
- Telecommunications (Title IV)

### Who's Protected

You're protected if you:
- Have a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities
- Have a record of such an impairment (past disability)
- Are regarded as having such an impairment (perceived disability)

The ADA Amendments Act of 2008 made clear that "disability" should be interpreted broadly.

### Major Life Activities

Examples include:
- Walking, seeing, hearing, speaking
- Breathing, eating, sleeping
- Learning, reading, concentrating, thinking
- Communicating, working
- Major bodily functions (immune system, digestion, neurological, etc.)

## Title I: Employment

### What It Covers

Title I prohibits employment discrimination by:
- Private employers with 15+ employees
- State and local governments
- Employment agencies
- Labor organizations

### Your Rights

**Hiring**: Employers cannot ask about disability before making a job offer. They can ask if you can perform job functions.

**On the job**: You're entitled to reasonable accommodations unless they cause undue hardship.

**Termination**: Can't be fired because of disability if you can do the job with or without accommodation.

### Reasonable Accommodations

Changes that enable you to do your job:
- Modified schedules
- Assistive technology
- Physical workspace changes
- Job restructuring
- Reassignment to vacant position
- Leave for treatment

See the Workplace Accommodations page for more detail.

### Requesting Accommodations

1. Tell your employer you need an adjustment due to a disability
2. Engage in "interactive process" to find solutions
3. Employer can request limited documentation
4. Accommodation should be implemented

### Enforcement

File complaints with the **Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)**:
- Within 180 days (or 300 days in some states)
- EEOC investigates and may mediate
- May receive "right to sue" letter for court action

## Title II: State and Local Government

### What It Covers

All services, programs, and activities of state and local governments:
- Public transportation
- Courts
- Public schools
- Voting
- Public meetings
- Parks and recreation
- Social services
- Public buildings and facilities
- Government websites

### Your Rights

- Programs must be accessible
- Effective communication must be provided
- Reasonable modifications to policies must be made
- Physical barriers must be removed when readily achievable

### Program Accessibility

Government programs must be accessible, though not every building needs to be accessible if the program can be provided in accessible locations.

### Effective Communication

Governments must provide:
- Sign language interpreters
- Written materials in alternative formats
- Assistive listening devices
- Other communication aids

### Enforcement

File complaints with:
- The relevant federal agency (DOJ for most issues)
- Department of Justice: ada.gov
- Or file a private lawsuit

## Title III: Public Accommodations

### What It Covers

Private businesses open to the public:
- Restaurants, bars, hotels
- Retail stores
- Theaters, stadiums
- Medical offices, hospitals
- Private schools
- Gyms, recreational facilities
- Banks
- And many more

### Your Rights

**Physical access**: Businesses must remove barriers where "readily achievable" (easy and inexpensive).

**Policies**: Must make reasonable modifications to policies.

**Effective communication**: Must provide accessible communication (interpreters, accessible formats).

**New construction**: New buildings must be accessible.

**Service animals**: Must allow service animals.

### "Readily Achievable"

Barrier removal is required when "readily achievable"—meaning easy and inexpensive considering:
- Cost of the action
- Business resources
- Type of operation

What's readily achievable varies by business size and resources.

### Website Accessibility

Courts have increasingly held that websites are public accommodations that must be accessible. This is an evolving area of law.

### Enforcement

- File complaint with Department of Justice
- File a private lawsuit
- No damages in federal court (injunctive relief only)
- Some state laws allow damages

---

## Title IV: Telecommunications

### What It Covers

Title IV requires:
- Telephone relay services for deaf and hard of hearing people
- Captioning on federally funded public service announcements

### Relay Services

Telecommunications Relay Service (TRS) allows deaf, hard of hearing, and speech-disabled people to make phone calls:
- TTY relay
- Video relay service (VRS)
- IP relay
- Captioned telephone

## Filing ADA Complaints

### Employment (Title I)

**Where**: EEOC
**Deadline**: 180 days (300 in some states) from discriminatory act
**How**: File online at eeoc.gov, by phone, or in person at EEOC office

### State/Local Government (Title II)

**Where**: Department of Justice or relevant federal agency
**Deadline**: 180 days generally, but varies
**How**: Online at ada.gov or by mail

### Public Accommodations (Title III)

**Where**: Department of Justice
**Deadline**: No specific deadline, but file promptly
**How**: Online at ada.gov or by mail
**Note**: Can also file private lawsuit (no deadline, but statutes of limitations apply)

### What Happens Next

After filing:
1. Agency reviews complaint
2. May investigate
3. May attempt mediation
4. May find violation or dismiss
5. May refer to DOJ for action
6. You may be able to sue privately

## ADA Amendments Act (ADAAA)

### Why It Matters

In 2008, Congress passed the ADA Amendments Act to overturn narrow Supreme Court interpretations that had limited who was "disabled" under the ADA.

### Key Changes

- "Disability" should be interpreted broadly
- Mitigating measures (medication, devices) not considered when determining disability
- Episodic conditions and conditions in remission can be disabilities
- "Regarded as" discrimination doesn't require showing actual impairment

### Practical Effect

More people are protected. Focus shifted to whether discrimination occurred, not whether someone "counts" as disabled.

## Limitations and Critiques

### What the ADA Doesn't Do

- Doesn't guarantee success, just prohibits discrimination
- "Undue hardship" and "readily achievable" exceptions create loopholes
- Enforcement is largely complaint-driven
- Damages are limited in Title III cases
- Doesn't cover small employers (under 15)

### Ongoing Barriers

Despite the ADA:
- Unemployment of disabled people remains high
- Many buildings and businesses remain inaccessible
- Website accessibility is inconsistent
- Enforcement is slow
- Attitudes and discrimination persist

### The Work Continues

The ADA was a major victory, but disabled people continue organizing for:
- Stronger enforcement
- Website accessibility standards
- Employment improvements
- Accessible housing
- Healthcare access
- And more

## History of the ADA

### Disability Rights Movement

The ADA was won through decades of activism:

**Section 504 sit-ins (1977)**: Disabled activists occupied federal buildings for 26 days to win regulations implementing Section 504.

**ADAPT bus actions (1980s)**: Protesters blocked buses and demanded accessible public transit.

**Capitol Crawl (1990)**: Activists abandoned wheelchairs and crawled up the Capitol steps to demand ADA passage.

**Ongoing advocacy**: Disabled people continue fighting for implementation and expansion.

### Key Figures

- Ed Roberts (independent living pioneer)
- Judy Heumann (activist, official)
- Justin Dart (ADA champion)
- Patrisha Wright ("General" of the ADA campaign)
- Wade Blank (ADAPT founder)
- Many more

## Scenarios: Finding What You Need

### "My employer won't give me accommodations"

Request in writing. Engage in interactive process. If denied, ask for explanation. File with EEOC within 180-300 days. Consider consulting an employment attorney.

### "A business isn't accessible"

Ask to speak with management. Request specific accommodations. If refused, file complaint with DOJ or consider private lawsuit. Document the barriers.

### "I was denied service because of my disability"

Document what happened. File complaint with DOJ. Consider state law claims which may offer damages. Contact disability rights organizations.

### "My city's services aren't accessible"

File complaint with DOJ or relevant federal agency. Attend public meetings and advocate. Connect with disability organizations pushing for accessibility.
