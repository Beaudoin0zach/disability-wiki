# Higher Education Accommodations

How to access college or university with disabilities. This page covers disability services offices, accommodations, legal protections, and strategies for success in higher education.

**Geographic scope:** Primarily U.S. colleges/universities, with some Canadian/UK/Australian context  
**Legal basis:** Section 504 (U.S.), Equality Act 2010 (UK), CRPD (international)  
**What they cover:** Academic accommodations, accessibility, disability services, student support

---

## Historical Origins and Movement Leadership

Before the 1970s, disabled students were rarely admitted to colleges or universities. Disability was seen as a barrier to academic ability (it's not—disability and intelligence are separate). Disabled students had to hide disabilities or were excluded entirely.

The Section 504 movement of the 1970s–80s opened college doors. Advocacy by blind students, deaf students, mobility-disabled students, and others pushed universities to provide accommodations. The American Association of University Administrators for Disabilities (ADUA) and student disability organizations fought for accessibility.

Despite this history, many colleges remain unwelcoming to disabled students. Disabled students of color face compounded barriers. Systemic ableism persists in academia.

---

## Key Provisions

**Section 504 compliance (U.S.):**

All colleges and universities receiving federal funding must comply with Section 504. This means:

- **No discrimination:** Cannot deny admission based on disability
- **Accommodations:** Must provide reasonable accommodations allowing equal educational access
- **Accessible campus:** Physical accessibility (buildings, sidewalks, housing, dining, transportation)
- **Accessible materials:** Textbooks in accessible formats (Braille, large print, audio, digital)
- **Qualified staff:** Disability services office staff with disability expertise
- **Confidentiality:** Disability information is private; student decides who knows

**What accommodations typically include:**

- Extra time on tests (common: time-and-a-half to double time)
- Reduced distractions (separate testing location)
- Note-taking services (paid note-taker or access to professor notes)
- Audio materials (textbooks as audio, lectures recorded)
- Accessible formats (large print, Braille, digital)
- Sign language interpreters or real-time captioning for deaf/hard of hearing students
- Assistive technology (screen readers, speech-to-text software)
- Priority registration for classes
- Accessible housing (ground floor, accessible bathrooms, etc.)
- Service animals allowed in all areas (including housing, dining)
- Medical leave (temporary withdrawal without penalty)
- Modified academic requirements (alternative assignments, flexible deadlines in some cases)
- Counseling and psychological services
- Career counseling and job coaching

**Limitations on accommodations:**

- Universities can refuse accommodations that "fundamentally alter" the course. However, many colleges use this excuse too broadly. Legitimate example: cannot require physics professor to teach without labs (fundamental). Illegitimate example: cannot require extra time on test (not fundamental; tests measure knowledge, not speed).

- Cannot require students to disclose disabilities to professors (though many professors need this information to provide accommodations)

- Cannot require students to use only particular assistive technology (example: if Braille is your accommodation, college cannot require you use digital instead)

**Scope:** Section 504 applies to U.S. institutions receiving federal funds. Private schools may have different obligations depending on local law. Community colleges through elite universities must comply. Distance learning programs must provide accessibility.

## Why It Matters

**For disabled students:** College often feels inaccessible—both physically and academically. Accommodations level the playing field. Without them, disabled students face discrimination and may be unable to succeed despite capable minds.

**For organizing:** College advocacy is powerful—disabled students organizing together can demand institutional change. College is often where disabled people first encounter other disabled peers and identify systemic barriers.

**For disabled people long-term:** College affects career, income, social networks, and independence. Accessible higher education translates to better post-college opportunities.

---

## Strengths and Breakthroughs

Some universities are leaders in accessibility. Disability services offices at these institutions genuinely support students. Some colleges have accessible housing and campus design. Some professors are disability-literate and proactively provide access.

Many disabled students find community and power in college—connecting with other disabled students, finding disability studies courses, joining student disability advocacy organizations.

Remote/online learning (expanded during COVID-19) has opened opportunities for some disabled students who struggled with physical/commute access.

---

## Challenges, Nuances, and Critiques

**Disability services is gatekeeping:** To get accommodations, students must disclose disabilities to disability services office. Some students fear this disclosure leads to stigma, low expectations, or discrimination. Many students of color fear that disability disclosure will interact with racist stereotyping.

**Shame and reluctance:** Some students don't disclose disabilities despite needing accommodations—due to stigma, fear, or internalized ableism. Many disabled students struggle through college without support.

**Inadequate services:** Many disability services offices are understaffed and under-resourced. Wait times for accommodations can be long. Some disability services staff are untrained or discriminatory.

**Physical accessibility gaps:** Many historic college buildings aren't accessible. Newer buildings sometimes are. Housing accessibility is inconsistent. Some campuses have inadequate accessible parking or transportation.

**Materials accessibility lags:** Textbooks aren't always available in accessible formats. Creating Braille takes time. Professors sometimes resist providing materials early for digitization.

**Ableism from peers and faculty:** Disabled students face microaggressions, skepticism about "real" disabilities, and social exclusion. Some professors deny accommodations or make accommodations humiliating (example: pulling disabled student out of class for test instead of providing quiet room).

**Bias in admissions:** Some programs claim to exclude disabled students (example: some medical schools have discouraged deaf applicants, though this violates law). Graduate programs may assume disabled students will be unable to handle workload.

**Mental health crisis:** Many disabled students struggle with mental health in college. Some schools have inadequate counseling services. Suicide rates among college disabled students are high.

**College not designed for disabled access:** Timed lectures, inability to miss class, rigid schedules, and culture of constant availability ("always-on") conflict with many disabled people's needs.

**Global gaps:** Outside the U.S., higher education accessibility varies widely. Canada has some provincial requirements but less developed than U.S. Many Global South countries have limited higher education accessibility.

## Disability Type Accessibility

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:** Interpreters, CART (real-time captioning), and video captions are essential. Some Deaf students prefer working with Deaf interpreters. Access to Deaf community on campus matters. Some colleges have strong Deaf services; others minimal.

**Blind/Low Vision:** Accessible textbooks (Braille, audio, digital), JAWS or NVDA software support, accessible lab equipment, and O&M (orientation and mobility) support on campus are needed. Library accessibility is critical. Career services need to support blind students in achieving non-stereotyped jobs.

**Mobility Disabilities:** Campus accessibility (ramps, elevators, accessible parking), physical accessibility of classrooms and housing, and accessible transportation are essential. Some students may need personal care attendant support during college.

**Neurodivergent (ADHD, Autism, Learning Disabilities):** Accommodations include extra time on tests, executive function support, sensory accommodations (quiet spaces, headphones), flexible attendance policies, and assignment modifications. Some neurodivergent students need support navigating social/unwritten rules of academia.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:** Accommodations include flexible attendance for mental health reasons, deadline flexibility during crisis, counseling access, and sometimes course load reduction. Many colleges lack adequate mental health services.

**Chronic Illness/Invisible Disability:** Accommodations include flexible attendance for health reasons, ability to miss class for medical appointments, and sometimes modified course workload. Professors often skeptical of invisible illness accommodations.

**Multiply-Marginalized:** Disabled students of color face racial bias that compounds disability discrimination. LGBTQ+ disabled students may face both disability and gender/sexuality discrimination. Disabled students from low-income backgrounds may struggle affording college and navigating without family knowledge of higher education. Disabled first-generation college students often lack mentorship.

## Step-by-Step: Accessing Accommodations

**Step 1: Find the disability services office**

Most colleges have an office of disability services (name varies: "Office of Accessible Education," "Disability Resource Center," etc.). This is usually in student services area.

**Step 2: Register and disclose**

Contact disability services. Provide documentation of your disability (this varies—some colleges accept self-diagnosis; others require medical documentation). You decide what to disclose and to whom.

**Step 3: Meet with disability services advisor**

Discuss your disabilities and needs. Create an accommodation plan. Be specific about what you need to participate equally in classes.

**Step 4: Get accommodation letter**

Receive formal letter listing accommodations. You decide which professors to give it to (you don't have to disclose to all).

**Step 5: Share with professors**

Decide how to contact professors. Some students hand them the letter; others email. Some professors are approachable; others are hostile. You're allowed to ask disability services for help if professors refuse.

**Step 6: Implement accommodations**

Work with disability services to arrange specific accommodations (extra time location, interpreter scheduling, material accessibility). Build relationships with helpful professors and staff.

**Step 7: Adjust as needed**

Accommodations can change. If something isn't working, ask disability services to modify your plan.

## Tips for Success in College with Disabilities

**Before college:**

- Visit campus; assess accessibility
- Contact disability services to ask about their services quality
- Connect with current disabled students if possible (ask disability services to put you in touch)
- Ask about mental health support availability
- Know Section 504 rights before arriving (you have rights before registering for services)

**First semester:**

- Register with disability services early
- Don't wait until struggling—get accommodations in place from start
- Connect with other disabled students (join disability student organizations)
- Identify 1-2 supportive professors; build relationships
- Use campus mental health services if needed
- Be patient with yourself during transition

**Throughout college:**

- Advocacy pays off—many accommodations students assume they can't have, they can negotiate for
- Document everything (save emails about accommodation requests/denials)
- Build disability community—you're not alone, others understand
- Consider disclosing strategically to professors; you have the right to accommodations whether or not professor is supportive
- Self-care and access needs are not laziness; they're survival
- Don't hide disability to "fit in"—accommodation-seeking is legitimate

**Mental health:**

- College mental health services are often inadequate but sometimes helpful
- Build support networks beyond campus (disability community, online communities)
- Know suicide prevention resources; share with peers
- Disability-affirming therapy (from therapist who doesn't view disability as problem) is valuable if available

## Navigating Discrimination & Disputes

**If professor refuses accommodations:**

1. **Document everything.** Save emails, note dates/times of conversations.
2. **Contact disability services.** They can often mediate or provide formal accommodation letter.
3. **File complaint** with college's Section 504 coordinator if problem persists.
4. **External complaint:** File with Department of Education Office for Civil Rights (OCR).
5. **Legal action:** If systemic discrimination, consult disability rights attorney.

**If college denies accommodation:**

Ask specifically why. Many colleges deny accommodations incorrectly. Challenge the decision. Ask for written explanation. Consult disability rights attorney if serious.

**If you face harassment or discrimination:**

Report to Title IX office (office handling discrimination complaints). You have legal protections.
