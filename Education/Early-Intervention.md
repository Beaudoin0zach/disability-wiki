# Early Intervention (Ages 0–3)

Services and legal protections for babies and toddlers with disabilities or developmental delays. Early intervention provides therapy, family support, and coordination to help young children develop and prepare for school.

**Geographic scope:** Primarily United States and Canada; frameworks vary globally  
**Key age range:** Birth to 36 months  
**What it covers:** Speech, physical, occupational therapy; family services; care coordination

---

## Historical Origins and Movement Leadership

Early intervention emerged from the disability rights movement's insistence that disabled children deserve support from infancy, not just school age. In the United States, advocacy by parents of disabled children and disability rights organizations led to Part C of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), passed in 1986. This was a major shift: before IDEA Part C, young children with disabilities had almost no guaranteed services.

The program reflects decades of research showing that early childhood support dramatically improves outcomes. Disability advocates pushed for family-centered models rather than child-only services—recognizing that supporting parents matters as much as direct therapy. However, implementation varies wildly by state and region, with some areas providing comprehensive services and others offering minimal support.

Globally, early intervention systems are uneven. Canada has provincial programs, the UK has early years services, and many Global South countries lack formalized systems. In low-resource contexts, family support and community-based approaches are often more accessible than clinical therapy models.

---

## Key Provisions

**Eligibility criteria:** Children with documented disabilities (cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, autism, hearing loss, etc.) or developmental delays (significant gap between age and functioning) qualify. Each state/region defines "delay" differently—typically 25–33% delay in one or more areas.

**Services provided:** Speech-language pathology, occupational therapy, physical therapy, audiology, psychological services, family training and counseling, assistive technology, and care coordination. Services are individualized based on the child's and family's needs.

**Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP):** Similar to IEPs (see below), but centered on family needs and outcomes. Must include natural environments (home, daycare, community settings) rather than clinical settings when possible.

**Parental involvement:** Required. Parents are partners in planning, not afterthoughts. Services include family training, coaching, and support.

**Cost:** Free or low-cost in most U.S. states. Funded through federal/state partnership. In other countries, costs vary; private therapy often expensive in Global South.

**Transition to school:** Services coordinate with school-entry IEP/504 plan around age 3. This transition period is critical—gaps in services can occur.

**Scope:** In the U.S., Part C of IDEA mandates services to age 3. After age 3, Part B (school-age services) begins. In Canada and UK, coverage extends through preschool. Many Global South countries have limited formal early intervention; family and community support often primary.

## Why It Matters

**For families:** Early intervention can prevent or reduce disability-related delays. Therapy in early years is often more effective than later intervention. Equally important: family support reduces parental isolation and burnout. Many parents of newly-diagnosed disabled children experience grief, confusion, and stress—early intervention services provide education, community connection, and practical help.

**For disabled children:** Access to services now affects long-term outcomes in school, employment, independence, and wellbeing. Early support for communication, mobility, learning, or sensory needs builds confidence and skills. Children who receive early intervention often transition to school with better self-advocacy understanding and existing accommodations.

**For systems:** Early identification and support is cost-effective—preventing secondary disabilities and school failure saves money long-term. Supporting families early reduces crisis interventions later.

---

## Strengths and Breakthroughs

Early intervention represents a philosophical shift: recognizing disabled infants and toddlers as people deserving support from birth. The emphasis on natural environments (home and community) rather than clinical settings is disability-justice aligned—it's less medicalized and more integrated.

Family-centered models (when well-implemented) empower parents rather than positioning them as passive. Some programs genuinely partner with families. In some regions, early intervention has strong connections to community organizations, making it more culturally responsive.

For deaf children, early identification of hearing loss and access to ASL (American Sign Language) or other sign language from infancy leads to better language development than waiting until school age. Similarly, early mobility support for children with cerebral palsy can prevent secondary complications.

---

## Challenges, Nuances, and Critiques

**Variation by location:** Quality, availability, and comprehensiveness differ dramatically. Some U.S. states have excellent early intervention; others are bare-bones. Global South countries often lack formal systems entirely—wealthier families access private therapy while poor families get nothing.

**Over-reliance on medical model:** Despite language about "family support," many early intervention programs are still therapy-heavy and clinical. Therapy occupies appointment slots but doesn't address barriers like poverty, housing, transportation, or discrimination. A blind toddler doesn't need occupational therapy; they need a community that supports blind navigation and independence.

**Diagnosis pressure:** Families often feel pressure to seek diagnosis to access services. In Global South contexts, diagnosis may be unavailable or unreliable. Additionally, early diagnosis can lead to intensive therapy-focused childhoods rather than play-based learning and typical childhood experiences.

**Accessibility of early intervention itself:** Many early intervention offices and therapy spaces are not physically accessible. Therapist shortage means long waits. Getting to appointments is a barrier for families without transportation or living in remote areas.

**Language barriers:** In Global South and for immigrant families, services may not be available in home languages. Interpreters are often unavailable or untrained in disability contexts.

**Eurocentric development norms:** Early intervention is based on Western developmental milestones. Some disabled children will not meet these—not because intervention failed, but because development is different. Blind children, for example, may reach milestones later but on their own trajectory.

**Transition gaps:** The shift from early intervention (age 0–3) to preschool/school (age 3+) is often abrupt. Many children experience loss of services during transition.

## Disability Type Accessibility

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:** Early identification of hearing loss (newborn screening) should lead to immediate access to sign language and Deaf community connection, not just hearing aids/cochlear implants. Some programs offer bilingual ASL-and-spoken-language approaches; others focus only on oral rehabilitation. Quality varies significantly.

**Blind/Low Vision:** Early mobility training, orientation and mobility services, and connection to blind mentors are critical. Many programs lack O&M specialists. Braille introduction (even if child will use some print) in early years is important but often missing.

**Cerebral Palsy/Mobility Disabilities:** Physical therapy can be valuable, but should focus on functional movement and participation in family/community activities, not normalization of walking or typical movement. Assistive technology (mobility devices, communication devices) should be introduced early.

**Autism/Neurodivergent:** Early intervention may focus on reducing "autistic" behaviors rather than supporting the child's neurology. Some programs offer sensory supports and communication help; others emphasize behavioral compliance. Quality depends on program philosophy—disability-justice-aligned programs are rare but better.

**Down Syndrome/Intellectual Disability:** Early intervention is available and valuable. Speech and physical therapy can help. Family support is critical since parents often face negative attitudes and low expectations.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:** Young children with mental health conditions (anxiety, depression, attachment disorders) may qualify for early intervention in some regions. Access is inconsistent and often only available through child welfare systems.

**Multiply-Marginalized:** Families with immigration status concerns may avoid early intervention due to deportation fears. Indigenous families may experience cultural mismatch with mainstream programs. Low-income families may face transportation and childcare barriers to attending appointments.

## How Organizers and Groups Can Apply It

**Advocacy strategy 1: Expand access**
Push for early intervention funding and availability in underserved regions (rural areas, Global South contexts). Document waitlists and service gaps. Demand translation services and community-based models alongside clinical models.

**Advocacy strategy 2: Shift focus from therapy to family support**
Advocate for funding allocation toward family coaching, parent peer groups, and systemic barrier removal (transportation, childcare, flexible scheduling) alongside direct therapy. Push programs to address poverty and housing as factors in child development.

**Advocacy strategy 3: Ensure sign language access for deaf children**
Work with Deaf organizations to ensure all deaf children have access to native sign language from infancy, regardless of parental hearing status. Push for Deaf mentors and Deaf-led early intervention components.

**Advocacy strategy 4: Community-based models**
For Global South contexts and under-resourced regions, develop community health worker and peer-led early intervention models. This is more sustainable and culturally appropriate than importing Western clinical models.

**Advocacy strategy 5: Transition support**
Organize to ensure continuity when children age out of early intervention (age 3). Create transition teams, warm handoffs, and bridge programs. Document service gaps during transition.

## Global Perspective & Intersectionality

**In Western contexts (US, Canada, UK, Australia):** Early intervention is formalized with legal requirements, though quality and access vary significantly by region and socioeconomic status.

**In Global South contexts:** Formal early intervention is often unavailable. Families rely on community support, informal childcare, and traditional approaches. Where services exist, they're often accessible only to wealthier urban families. Disability-friendly approaches may not match Western therapy models—community inclusion and family care are primary.

**Intersectionality note:** Low-income families may struggle to attend appointments due to transportation and work barriers. Indigenous families often experience cultural mismatch in mainstream services. Families with immigration status concerns may avoid services. Deaf families (often with deaf parents) may have culturally different attitudes toward early intervention—Deaf parents may not view deafness as requiring "fixing" through therapy. Multiply-marginalized children face compounded barriers to access.
