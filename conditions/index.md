# Specific Disabilities & Conditions

Disability is not one thing. Different disabilities create different needs, access requirements, and community cultures. This section provides resources organized by disability type, recognizing that lived experience guides what information matters most.

## About This Section

This section avoids a medical model "disorder of the week" approach. Instead, each page centers the perspectives, strategies, and community knowledge of people with that disability. We include both medical information (when relevant) and disability justice perspectives.

Pages are organized by how people think about their disability, not by medical classification. This recognizes that disability identity matters, that some people identify with their diagnosis and others don't, and that categorization itself can be limiting.

## Physical Disabilities

Physical disabilities affect mobility and physical function. Include:

**Mobility impairments**: Difficulty moving limbs or whole body
- Cerebral palsy, stroke, spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis

**Amputations**: Loss of limb or part of limb

**Muscle-related**: Muscular dystrophy, myasthenia gravis, cerebral palsy

**Joint/connective tissue**: EDS, arthritis, lupus, fibromyalgia

**Other**: Tremor, paralysis, weakness

Strategies for physical disabilities include:
- Mobility aids (wheelchair, scooter, walker)
- Home/workplace accessibility modifications
- Pain management
- Medication
- Therapy (PT, OT)
- Assistive technology
- Caregiver support

## Sensory Disabilities

Sensory disabilities affect perception:

**Vision-related**:
- Blindness
- Low vision
- Color blindness
- Legal blindness definitions

**Hearing-related**:
- Deafness (may be identity as well as disability)
- Hard of hearing
- Late-deafened (became deaf later in life)
- Tinnitus
- Auditory processing disorders

**Other sensory**:
- Olfactory/gustatory disabilities (can't smell/taste)
- Touch-related (numbness, hypersensitivity)

**DeafBlindness**: Combined deafness and blindness
- Unique communication methods (tactile sign language, braille)
- Community and culture

Sensory disabled communities are strong and culturally vibrant. Deaf culture is particularly rich with its own language, values, and communities.

## Chronic Illness

Chronic illness is ongoing, often fluctuating health conditions:

**Autoimmune conditions**: When immune system attacks body
- Lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, Hashimoto's, Celiac disease, etc.

**ME/CFS (Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome)**:
- Post-exertional malaise (activity causes disproportionate worsening)
- Severe fatigue
- Cognitive issues ("brain fog")
- Often severe and homebound

**POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome)**:
- Heart rate jumps when changing position
- Dizziness, fainting, fatigue
- Affects young people, especially women

**Chronic pain conditions**: 
- Widespread or localized pain
- Often no visible cause
- Central sensitization, neuropathic pain, etc.

**Long COVID**:
- Ongoing symptoms after COVID infection
- Overlaps with ME/CFS
- Post-exertional malaise common
- Relatively new, gaining recognition

**EDS (Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome)**:
- Connective tissue disorder
- Joint hypermobility, subluxations, dislocations
- Skin, digestive, and other involvement
- Genetic, multiple types

Chronic illness is unpredictable. Strategies include pacing, rest, symptom management, and accepting fluctuation.

## Neurodivergence

Neurodivergent people think differently. Not better or worse, just different. Includes:

**Autism**:
- Sensory processing differences
- Social communication differences
- Special interests/intense focus
- May be nonspeaking, pre-speaking, or speaking
- Supports needed vary widely
- Autistic culture and pride

**ADHD**:
- Executive function challenges
- Attention/focus differences
- Impulsivity and hyperactivity (presentation varies)
- Time blindness
- Often co-occurs with other neurodivergence

**Dyslexia**:
- Reading and spelling challenges
- Neurological, not laziness or lack of intelligence
- Often co-occurs with other neurodivergence
- Different ways of processing language

**Dyspraxia**:
- Coordination and motor planning difficulties
- Affects movement, speech, perception
- Often co-occurs with other neurodivergence

**Tourette Syndrome**:
- Motor and vocal tics
- Involuntary, though somewhat manageable
- Not just swearing (myth)
- Often co-occurs with ADHD, OCD

**Learning disabilities**:
- Challenges processing specific information
- Dyscalculia (math), dysgraphia (writing), etc.
- Not about intelligence
- Can be addressed with support

Neurodiversity is normal human variation. Neurodivergent people bring unique perspectives and strengths.

## Intellectual & Developmental Disability

Intellectual disability involves challenges with cognitive development and functioning. Developmental disability refers to disabilities emerging during development.

**Self-advocacy**:
- People with intellectual disability leading and speaking for themselves
- Organizations led by people with intellectual disabilities
- "Nothing about us without us"

**Supported decision-making**:
- Alternative to guardianship
- Person makes decisions with trusted advisors
- Maintains legal rights while getting support

**Inclusion and community integration**:
- Right to work, learn, live in community
- Not segregation in special programs
- Community participation and relationships

**Self-determination**:
- Making choices about your own life
- Taking risks
- Learning from experience

## Psychiatric & Psychosocial Disability

Psychiatric disabilities include:

**Major depression**:
- Persistent sadness, hopelessness
- Lack of energy, motivation
- Changes in sleep, appetite
- Can be severe and disabling

**Bipolar disorder**:
- Mood episodes: depression, mania, hypomania
- Unpredictable cycling
- Medication often essential

**Schizophrenia and psychosis**:
- Experiences of reality different from others
- Hallucinations, delusions
- Cognitive changes
- Often severe and early-onset

**PTSD and trauma**:
- Response to traumatic experiences
- Intrusive thoughts/memories
- Avoidance, hypervigilance
- Complex PTSD from repeated trauma

**OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder)**:
- Intrusive thoughts causing anxiety
- Compulsions to manage anxiety
- Severe cases very disabling

**Anxiety disorders**:
- Persistent worry and fear
- Panic attacks possible
- Affects functioning

**Substance use disorders**:
- Disability in its own right (not moral failure)
- Often co-occurs with other mental health disabilities
- Recovery is possible

Psychiatric disabilities are real disabilities. Mental health is health. Recovery doesn't mean becoming "normal"—it means managing symptoms and building a good life.

## Invisible Disabilities

Invisible disabilities aren't visible to others. Include:

- Chronic pain and fatigue
- Sensory processing disabilities (can be hidden)
- Cognitive disabilities
- Psychiatric disabilities
- Some neurological conditions
- Many others

Challenges of invisible disability:
- Not believed by others
- Pressure to "look sick"
- Assumptions about capacity
- No visible accommodation needs
- Disability only visible during flare
- "But you don't look disabled"

Invisible doesn't mean less real or less disabling. Visibility has nothing to do with validity.

## Multiple Disabilities

Many people have more than one disability. Combinations create unique challenges:

- Deaf-blind people need specific communication methods
- Chronic illness + psychiatric disability doubles fatigue
- Mobility + cognitive disability needs different accommodations
- Multiple disabilities don't equal multiple sympathy

Multiply-disabled people often have unique insights and resilience.

## Rare Diseases and Disabilities

Rare disabilities present unique challenges:

- Limited information available
- Few specialists in the condition
- Difficulty finding others with same condition
- High medical costs
- Difficulty getting diagnosis
- Difficulty getting accommodations (people unfamiliar)

Strategies:
- Online communities
- Rare disease organizations
- Self-education
- Research participation
- Connecting globally with others

## Finding Your Community

Condition-specific communities exist for almost every disability. Finding your people can be powerful:

- Understand your disability better
- Learn strategies others use
- Feel less alone
- Advocate together
- Find joy in shared experience

Not all communities are healthy. Look for communities that:
- Center lived experience
- Welcome diverse perspectives
- Don't push specific treatments/cures
- Support autonomy and choice
- Address intersectionality
