# Spanish Glossary & Style Rules

Apply these consistently. Most were caught in a native-speaker review of the
pilot — they are the difference between "technically Spanish" and "a Spanish
speaker would trust this."

## Voice & register

- **Neutral Latin-American Spanish.** Avoid regionalisms; this serves a broad
  US/Latin-American audience.
- **Person-first**: "personas con discapacidad", never "los discapacitados" /
  "los minusválidos" (offensive).
- Address the reader with **tú** (not usted) to match the warm, direct English
  voice. Use inclusive "/a" sparingly where the English is explicitly welcoming
  (e.g. "¡Bienvenido/a!").

## Headings: Spanish sentence case

English title case ("How To Use This Wiki") feels translated. Spanish capitalizes
only the **first word and proper nouns**:

- `## How to Use This Wiki` → `## Cómo usar este wiki`
- `### Common Issues` → `### Problemas comunes`
- Proper nouns stay capitalized: `## Relación con la Sección 504`,
  headings naming `Título I`, `Medicare`, `Olmstead`, organization names.

## Distinct legal terms — do NOT merge them

These are separate legal standards in US disability law; collapsing them loses
meaning. Keep the English in parentheses on first use.

| English | Spanish |
|---|---|
| undue hardship (Title I employment) | **dificultad excesiva** (undue hardship) |
| undue burden (Title III communication) | **carga onerosa** (undue burden) |
| readily achievable | fácilmente realizable |
| reasonable accommodation | adaptación razonable |

Never translate either standard as the vague "carga excesiva."

## Coordinated noun lists — repeat the head noun

English can stack adjectives after one noun; Spanish gets ambiguous about whether
they all describe the same people. Repeat the noun to disambiguate:

- "for disabled people, allies, and advocates" →
  "para personas con discapacidad, **personas** aliadas y defensoras"
  (not "...con discapacidad, aliadas y defensoras", which reads as if all three
  describe the disabled people).

## Proper nouns: keep, don't translate

- **Acronyms stay**: ADA, SSDI, SSI, SNAP, TANF, IDEA, FHA, EEOC, DOJ, FCC, CART,
  AAC, WCAG.
- **Official law names**: keep recognizable, gloss on first use —
  "Ley de Estadounidenses con Discapacidades (Americans with Disabilities Act,
  ADA)". After first use, the acronym alone is fine.
- **Organization names stay in English** (National Federation of the Blind,
  ADAPT, DREDF, NCIL, Job Accommodation Network…). A Spanish speaker searches for
  and contacts them under their real names. You may add a short Spanish gloss for
  a government body on first use (e.g. "Comisión para la Igualdad de
  Oportunidades en el Empleo (EEOC)").
- **Phone numbers, URLs, email addresses**: never alter.

## Dates & numbers

- Months lowercase: "enero de 2026".
- Keep US number/date formats as they appear (e.g. population "50,000",
  "April 24, 2026" → "24 de abril de 2026"). Don't convert currencies or invent
  locale-specific figures.

## Common footer blocks (translate consistently)

- `## Contribute to This Page` → `## Contribuye a esta página`
- The "Have lived experience or expertise…" paragraph and the
  "[Suggest an edit or addition →]" link have fixed Spanish forms used across all
  pilot pages — reuse the wording in the existing `es/` pages for consistency.
- `*Last updated: January 2026*` → `*Última actualización: enero de 2026*`
