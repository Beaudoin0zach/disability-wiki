# How to Use This Wiki

All people deserve access to information in formats they can navigate. This page helps you find what you need, even if you're tired, overloaded, or on a small screen.

---

## Why This Matters

Disability often comes with barriers to finding information: brain fog, fatigue, sensory processing differences, limited time between appointments and paperwork. This wiki is designed with those realities in mind. Understanding how it's organized will help you find what you need faster.

---

### Use Keyword Search

Try searching for a topic (wheelchair repair, SNAP, IEP, paratransit), a country or region (Canada, UK, Philippines), or a phrase in quotes ("chronic pain," "service animals").

If you don't find something you expected, that page may need to be created. You can suggest it through our [contribution form](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSfVmedRxQZ1tmu63I0OZSZXuinbzNQ7WYyBFHMp6NbsHb0F0g/viewform?usp=sharing&ouid=110839947117265692657).

### Use "Where Are You?" Navigation

Many pages include a "Where Are You?" section near the top with quick links to country-specific information. This helps you jump directly to information relevant to your location without scrolling through content that doesn't apply.

## How Pages Are Organized

Most pages follow a similar pattern:

**Opening** — What this topic is and why it matters, plus a note about centering disabled people's expertise.

**Key content** — The main information, organized by subtopic or by country depending on the page type.

**Where Are You?** — Country-specific sections clearly labeled (for pages where information varies by location).

**Resources** — Links to organizations, official sites, community guides, and further reading.

**Related Pages** — Links to other wiki pages on connected topics.

**Contribute section** — How to suggest additions or corrections.

**Footer** — Last updated date and attribution.

Some pages are high-level overviews (like "What Is Disability?"), while others are practical how-to guides (like "Requesting Workplace Accommodations"). The structure adapts to what the content needs.

## Symbols and Notes You Might See

Throughout the wiki, you may encounter:

**Note**: Practical tips or clarifications that might help.

**Important**: Information that significantly affects how you approach something.

**Caution**: Something that could affect your benefits, immigration status, legal rights, or safety.

**Region-specific**: Information that only applies in a specific country or area.

## Accessibility of This Wiki

### What We Aim For

This wiki is designed with accessibility in mind:

**Plain language** where possible, with definitions for jargon when technical terms are necessary.

**Screen-reader-friendly structure** using proper headings, descriptive link text, and semantic markup.

**Consistent organization** so you can predict where to find information once you learn the pattern.

**Image descriptions** for key images.

**No auto-playing media** or content that flashes.

**High contrast support** where the platform allows.

### For Screen Reader Users

Pages use semantic headings (H1 for title, H2 for main sections, H3 for subsections) to enable easy navigation. Link text describes where links go rather than using "click here." Lists are properly formatted for screen reader announcement.

### For Neurodivergent Users

Information is broken into manageable chunks. Bullet points are used for scannable content. Each page starts with an overview so you know what you're getting. Related pages are linked so you can follow your interests without losing your place.

### If Something Isn't Accessible

If something on this site is not accessible to you, that is a bug, not a personal failing. Please report it so it can be fixed. You can use the [contribution form](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSfVmedRxQZ1tmu63I0OZSZXuinbzNQ7WYyBFHMp6NbsHb0F0g/viewform?usp=sharing&ouid=110839947117265692657) to report accessibility issues.

## Contributing or Suggesting Edits

This wiki is community-built. You can contribute by suggesting small corrections, adding resources you've found useful, proposing new pages, sharing information from your country or community, or translating content.

You don't need a degree or perfect writing to help. Lived experience is expertise.

See [How to Contribute](/glossary/how-to-contribute) and [Editorial Guidelines](/glossary/editorial-guidelines) for details on the contribution process and the tone and framing we aim for (non-ableist, justice-focused, no cure evangelism).
