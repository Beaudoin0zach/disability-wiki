# Medicaid and Medicare

Medicaid and Medicare are different federal health insurance programs. Confusion is common because names similar. Understanding which one you have is critical—they cover different things.

**Medicare:** Federal health insurance for disabled people (any age) and seniors (65+). You qualify through work history or disability, not income.

**Medicaid:** State-run health insurance for low-income people of any age. You qualify by income level, not work history.

## Historical Origins and Movement Leadership

**Medicare (1965):** Created as part of Great Society. Originally for elderly (65+). Disability portion added 1972 after disabled workers and advocates pushed for healthcare security.

Concept: Disabled people, like elderly, deserve healthcare access regardless of employment. Disability means medical costs. Insurance is earned through work history (similar to SSDI).

**Medicaid (1965):** Created simultaneously as healthcare for poor people. State-administered, federal-funded. Each state sets rules within federal guidelines.

Advocacy for Medicaid: War on poverty rhetoric. Recognition that poor people couldn't afford healthcare. Medicaid embedded in state systems, varies significantly state to state.

Both programs improved through decades of activism. Coverage expanded repeatedly. Advocacy continues around adequacy—both programs cover basics but not everything, and costs remain barriers.

## Medicare Explained

Medicare is federal insurance for disabled people (any age) and seniors (65+).

### Who Qualifies for Medicare

**Disabled:** Automatic after 24 months on Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI).

**Seniors:** Automatic at age 65 regardless of disability status.

**End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD):** Anyone needing dialysis or kidney transplant.

**ALS (Lou Gehrig's Disease):** Automatic, no waiting period.

**Citizenship:** US citizens and certain legal residents.

### What Medicare Covers

**Part A: Hospital Insurance**
- Hospital inpatient care
- Skilled nursing facility (if medically necessary after hospital)
- Home health care (if medically necessary)
- Hospice (end-of-life care)

**Part B: Medical Insurance**
- Doctor office visits
- Outpatient care
- Diagnostic tests
- Lab work
- Preventive care (screening, vaccines)
- Durable medical equipment (wheelchairs, walkers, oxygen)
- Therapies (occupational, speech, physical—some limits)

**Part D: Prescription Drug Coverage**
- Medications from approved formulary
- Varies by plan (you choose plan annually)

**Original Medicare** combines Part A + Part B. You choose your doctors (nationwide coverage).

**Medicare Advantage** (Part C) replaces Part A + B with private insurance. Network-based (doctors must be in network).

### What Medicare Doesn't Cover

- Routine dental (some plans may offer small benefit)
- Routine vision/eye exams (Part B covers diabetic retinopathy screening)
- Hearing aids (not covered, though some plans offer allowances)
- Long-term care or custodial care
- Personal care assistance (not skilled nursing)
- In-home support services
- Food or shelter
- Some therapies beyond limits (occupational, speech, physical have visit limits)
- Experimental treatments (usually)

### Medicare Costs for Disabled People

**Premiums (what you pay monthly):**
- Part A: Usually free (you or spouse paid through payroll taxes)
- Part B: $176–$230/month (2024, varies by income)
- Part D: Varies by plan ($0–500+/month)

**Deductibles:** $1,600 hospital deductible per stay (2024). Varies yearly.

**Copays:** $400 per day for hospital days 4–60 (2024).

**Coinsurance:** 20% of approved amount for doctor visits, tests, equipment after deductible.

**Cost-Sharing for Low-Income:** Medicare Savings Programs (MSP) and Extra Help programs reduce costs for low-income disabled people. SSA or Medicaid office can enroll you.

### How to Enroll in Medicare

Most disabled people automatically enrolled when SSDI begins (month 25).

If not automatically enrolled: Contact SSA or Medicare.

Medicare.gov has enrollment calculator and support.

## Medicaid Explained

Medicaid is state-administered health insurance for low-income people. Rules vary by state significantly.

### Who Qualifies for Medicaid

Varies by state. Generally:

**Income:** 100–300% of federal poverty level depending on state and family size.

**Category:** Disabled, elderly, parent of minor, child, pregnant person.

**Assets:** Varies, but generally low limits ($2,000–3,000).

**Citizenship:** US citizens and certain legal residents.

**Work Status:** No work requirement (though some states experimenting).

### What Medicaid Covers

**Mandatory Coverage (all states must provide):**
- Doctor office visits
- Hospital inpatient and outpatient care
- Laboratory and X-ray
- Skilled nursing facility
- Home health care (if ordered by doctor)
- Early and periodic screening, diagnostic, and treatment (EPSDT) for children
- Family planning services
- Pregnancy and delivery
- Nursing facility services for elderly

**Optional Coverage (states choose):**
- Prescription drugs
- Dental care
- Vision care
- Hearing aids
- Therapy services (occupational, speech, physical)
- Mental health and substance use treatment
- Assistive technology devices
- Personal care services
- Home and community-based services (alternatives to institutionalization)
- Waiver services (services not usually covered)

### What Medicaid Doesn't Cover (varies by state)

- Services not covered in your state
- Non-emergency transportation (some states cover)
- Adult dental (some states cover)
- Vision care (some states cover)
- Hearing aids (some states cover)
- Services deemed not medically necessary
- Experimental treatments
- Cosmetic procedures

### Medicaid Costs for Disabled People

**Premiums:** Usually free or very low for disabled people.

**Copays:** Typically $1–4 per service. Some services (emergencies, preventive) have no copay.

**Deductibles:** Usually none for disabled people or very low ($0–200 annually).

**Cost-Sharing:** Generally low. Some states waive all cost-sharing for disabled people.

### How to Enroll in Medicaid

Apply at state Medicaid office or online (varies by state).

SSI applicants often automatically enrolled if approved.

SSDI beneficiaries after Medicaid Extended period (36 months after losing SSI) may apply separately.

Healthcare.gov can connect you to state Medicaid.

## How These Programs Work Together

### If You Receive SSDI

At month 24: Medicare automatically begins.

You have Medicare (Part A, B, D) as primary insurance.

You don't automatically get Medicaid (but you can apply separately in most states).

Many states have **Medicaid for Disabled Adults (MDAG)** program—you might qualify.

Having both Medicare and Medicaid is called "dual coverage" and is beneficial (reduces costs, expands coverage).

### If You Receive SSI

At approval: Medicaid automatically begins (in most states; some states different).

You have Medicaid as primary insurance.

You don't have Medicare unless also on SSDI (or age 65+).

If income increases above SSI limit: You lose SSI but keep Medicaid Extended 36 more months.

### If You Have Both Medicare and Medicaid (Dual Eligible)

Medicare pays first for covered services.

Medicaid pays Medicare copays, deductibles, coinsurance.

Medicaid covers services Medicare doesn't.

This is beneficial—your out-of-pocket costs minimal.

### Coordination Example

You're on SSDI (month 25+) with Medicare.

You apply for Medicaid and qualify (low income).

You see doctor:
- Medicare covers doctor visit (pays 80% after deductible)
- Medicaid covers your 20% coinsurance and any copay
- Your cost: $0 or minimal

You need physical therapy:
- Medicare covers up to 60 visits/year
- After 60 visits: Medicaid may continue therapy if medically necessary
- Expanded coverage due to both programs

## Medicaid Varies by State

**Generous States (broad coverage):**
- California: Covers adults up to 138% of federal poverty level
- New York: Dental, vision, hearing aids included
- Massachusetts: Excellent coverage, high eligibility

**Restrictive States (narrow coverage):**
- Texas: Covers parents up to 18% of federal poverty level (very low)
- Florida: Limited coverage for disabled adults
- Georgia: Basic coverage only, many services optional

**Work Incentive Program Medicaid:** All states must provide Medicaid to people using work incentive programs (working while on SSI/SSDI).

Research your state at medicaid.gov or state Medicaid agency.

## Disability Type Coverage Considerations

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**

Medicare Part B covers hearing tests (diagnostic) but not hearing aids. Some Medicaid programs do cover hearing aids.

Question for your insurance: Are hearing aids covered? Cochlear implant surgery? Implant maintenance?

TTY/video relay services often free (separate from insurance).

**Blind/Low Vision:**

Medicare Part B covers low vision exam. Does not typically cover glasses/contacts.

Medicaid varies by state on vision coverage. Some states cover glasses.

Screen readers and Braille devices covered if deemed medical equipment.

Rehabilitation services (teaching screen reader, Braille) may be covered under therapy benefits.

**Neurodivergent:**

Mental health coverage varies. Medicare Part B covers psychiatrist/psychologist. Copays apply.

Medicaid usually covers more mental health visits with lower copays.

Medication management: Both cover psychiatric medications under prescription drug benefits.

Therapy (ADHD, autism-specific): Occupational therapy covered if ordered by doctor. Usually 30–60 visit limit/year.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health Disability:**

Both programs cover psychiatric care, medications, therapy.

Medicaid often more generous on mental health (fewer visit limits, lower copays).

Substance use treatment: Both cover inpatient and outpatient.

Residential treatment: Medicaid often covers if medically necessary; Medicare doesn't.

**Hidden/Invisible Disabilities:**

Coverage depends on visibility to insurance company, not to general public.

Diagnosis required for coverage. "You have invisible disability" doesn't mean insurance covers it if not documented.

Rare conditions: Harder to get coverage. Need documented medical necessity.

**Mobility Disabilities:**

Both cover wheelchairs, walkers, scooters, accessible equipment.

Home modification coverage varies. Not usually covered but sometimes considered medical equipment.

Personal care assistance: Medicare doesn't cover. Medicaid varies (some states generous, others don't cover).

**Multiply-Marginalized:**

Immigration status: Legal residents usually covered. Undocumented vary by state (some states provide Medicaid to undocumented). Medicare requires citizenship/legal residency.

Trans healthcare: Coverage varies by state and program. Some states deny transition-related care; others cover fully.
