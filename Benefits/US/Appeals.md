# Appealing Denied or Reduced Disability Benefits

If benefits denied or reduced, you can appeal. Many people are denied initially but win on appeal. Appealing is essential.

**Key Point:** Initial denial doesn't mean final answer. Appeal process exists specifically because many initial denials wrong.

## General Appeal Process (Most Federal Programs)

Most federal benefits programs follow similar appeal structure:

**Step 1: Reconsideration/Recalculation**
- Request within 60 days
- Different staff reviews case
- You provide additional evidence
- Decision: 30–90 days typical

**Step 2: Hearing Before Judge**
- Request within 60 days of Step 1 denial
- Administrative law judge reviews case
- You present evidence (can bring lawyer/advocate)
- Decision: 3–12 months typical

**Step 3: Appeals Council**
- Request within 60 days of judge denial
- Council reviews decision
- Decision: 6–12 months typical

**Step 4: Federal Court**
- Last resort appeal
- Expensive (lawyer essential)
- Rare, but possible

## Specific Appeal Processes by Program

### SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance) Appeals

**Initial Denial Process:**

Receive "Notice of Determination" in mail explaining denial.

Reason stated (insufficient evidence, work capacity found, etc.).

### Step 1: Reconsideration

**Request Within:** 60 days of denial notice

**How to Request:**

SSA office in person, by phone, online at SSA.gov, by mail.

Form SSA-561 (Request for Reconsideration)

**Process:**

Different SSA staff reviews.

Request additional medical records.

You can provide new evidence (medical records, doctors' statements, employment history).

**Decision Time:** 3–6 months

### Step 2: Hearing Before Administrative Law Judge (ALJ)

If reconsideration denied:

**Request Within:** 60 days of denial notice

**Form:** SSA-501 (Request for Hearing Before an Administrative Law Judge)

**Process:**

Hearing conducted by judge (phone or in person).

You present evidence: medical records, doctors' testimony, work history, witnesses.

Judge asks questions.

Expect 1–2 hour hearing.

You can have representative (lawyer, advocate, VSO).

Representatives often work contingency (take percentage of back pay owed).

**Decision Time:** 3–12 months (varies by location)

**Outcome:** Many people win at hearing stage. Approximately 60% of hearings result in approval.

### Step 3: Appeals Council

If judge denies:

**Request Within:** 60 days of judge decision

**Process:**

Council reviews case files (not new hearing).

Submit written brief explaining why judge was wrong.

Council decides based on written record.

**Decision Time:** 6–12 months

**Outcome:** Fewer decisions favorable at council stage than hearing stage. But appeals continue forward.

### Step 4: Federal Court

If council denies:

**Time Frame:** Specific deadline applies (consult lawyer)

**Process:**

File lawsuit against SSA in federal court.

Lawyer essential at this stage.

Very expensive.

Few cases reach this stage.

**Decision Time:** 1–3 years

### SSI (Supplemental Security Income) Appeals

Same appeal process as SSDI.

Timelines identical.

Process identical.

## Strategies for Winning Appeals

### Get Strong Medical Evidence

**Why It Matters:**

SSA requires medical documentation of disability.

Initial denials often due to insufficient medical evidence.

Appeals won by providing stronger evidence.

**Types of Evidence:**

- Medical records (doctor notes, test results, diagnoses)
- Doctor's statement directly addressing functional limitations
- Multiple providers confirming diagnosis/limitations
- Hospital records (if applicable)
- Therapy records (if in therapy)
- Work history showing job loss due to disability
- Buddy statements (people who know your limitations)

**Getting Evidence:**

Request medical records from all providers.

Ask doctor to write detailed letter explaining:
- Diagnosis
- Expected duration
- Functional limitations
- Work capacity assessment
- Treatment ongoing

Explains why you cannot work—this is critical.

### Request Hearing (Not Just Reconsideration)

**Why It Matters:**

Hearings have much higher approval rate than reconsiderations.

Judges more likely to approve than initial SSA staff.

In-person presentation and representation improve outcomes significantly.

**How to Skip Reconsideration:**

Can request hearing directly (skipping reconsideration step).

Faster path to judge.

Better outcome rate.

### Get a Representative

**Lawyer Representation:**

Social Security Disability lawyers specialize in SSDI/SSI appeals.

Most work on contingency: 25% of back pay owed (capped at $6,000).

You only pay if you win.

Many lawyers provide free initial consultation.

Worth the cost—lawyers win approximately 70% of cases, vs. 60% without representation.

**How to Find Lawyer:**

- National Organization of Social Security Claimants' Representatives (NOSSCR): Lawyer referral
- Local legal aid: Free/low-cost representation
- Google "SSDI lawyer [your state]"

**Other Representation:**

Certified advocate or VSO (Veterans Service Officer) can also represent.

May be free or lower cost than lawyer.

Not as legally trained but helpful.

### Collect Documentation

**Create Evidence File:**

Medical records from all providers

Doctors' statements about functional limitations

Work history documentation (job loss dates, performance reviews)

Education records

Medications and treatments

Therapy records

Witness statements from family/employers about limitations

Buddy statements from people who see you regularly

### Create Strong Narrative

**Tell Your Story:**

Don't just present medical facts.

Explain how disability affects daily life:
- Can't work more than X hours before exhaustion
- Pain prevents concentration
- Cognitive limitations prevent complex tasks
- Transportation limitations prevent reliable work
- Medications cause side effects affecting function

Functional story more persuasive than diagnosis alone.

**Written Statement:**

Submit written statement with appeal explaining:
- How disability developed
- Medical treatment
- How disability affects ability to work
- What you've tried and why didn't work
- Why you believe you should be approved

## Common Reasons for Denial

### Insufficient Medical Evidence

**Problem:** You say disabled but no doctors confirm.

**Solution:** Get doctor to document. Be explicit about functional limitations.

### Work Capacity Found

**Problem:** Vocational expert testified you could do other work.

**Solution:** Present evidence showing you cannot do that work. Medical evidence. Work history showing inability.

### Failure to Follow Treatment

**Problem:** You not taking medications or attending therapy.

**Solution:** Resume treatment. Document compliance. SSA wants to see you trying treatment.

### Income Above Limit (SSI Only)

**Problem:** Your income exceeds SSI limit.

**Solution:** Use work incentives. PASS plan. IRWE. Reduces countable income.

### Not Following Prescribed Treatment

**Problem:** Doctor prescribed treatment but you refused.

**Solution:** Comply with treatment or explain why can't (side effects, contraindications, etc.).

### Disagreement About Severity

**Problem:** SSA believes you not as disabled as claim.

**Solution:** Stronger medical evidence. Functional assessment. Work history.

## Appeals for Benefits Reduction

If receiving benefits but amount reduced:

**Same Appeal Process Applies**

Request reconsideration within 60 days.

Can request hearing if reconsideration denied.

Present evidence showing continued disability and limitations.

## Continuing Disability Reviews (CDRs)

SSA periodically reviews whether still disabled.

**Timeline:**

- Medical Improvement Possible: 6–18 months after approval
- Medical Improvement Not Likely: Every 3 years
- Work Activity Suggests Improvement: Every 2–3 years
- Medical Evidence Suggests Permanent: Every 5–7 years

**When CDR Notice Arrives:**

You receive notice asking for updated medical records.

Submit medical records.

SSA reviews and decides if still disabled.

**If Benefit Stopped After CDR:**

Most common reason for benefit termination (not fraud or income—medical review finding you improved).

**Can Appeal CDR Decision:**

Same appeal process as initial denial.

Request reconsideration.

Request hearing.

Can win appeal even if initially found not disabled in CDR.

## Medical Evidence Needed for Appeals

### Doctor's Statement (Most Important)

**What to Ask Doctor:**

"I'm appealing disability denial. Can you write letter addressing:

1. My diagnosis and history
2. Expected duration (permanent or how long?)
3. Functional limitations (What can't I do?)
4. Work capacity (Can I work? How many hours?)
5. Specific impact on ability to work
6. Ongoing treatment needed
7. Any opinion on whether I can perform past work
8. Any opinion on whether I can do other work"

**Stronger if:**

Doctor specific about limitations (not just diagnosis).

Doctor aware of work demands.

Doctor experienced with disability assessment.

Letter detailed and comprehensive.

### Functional Assessment

**Request from Doctor:**

Assessment of day-to-day functional abilities:
- Can you stand more than 30 minutes?
- Can you sit more than 8 hours?
- Can you concentrate on complex tasks?
- Can you remember instructions?
- Can you manage medications/treatments?
- Can you care for yourself?

### Work History

**Gather:**

Employment history (dates, jobs, reasons for job loss).

Performance reviews.

Explanation of why left each job (disability reason if applicable).

Wages earned (showing consistency or pattern).

Demonstrates work capacity or lack thereof.

### Witness Statements

**Buddy Statements:**

Written statements from people who see you regularly.

Family member, friend, coworker, church member.

Describe your functional limitations as they observe them.

More credible coming from multiple people.

## Veterans Appeals (VA Disability)

VA appeals different process than Social Security.

**VA Appeal Steps:**

1. File Notice of Disagreement (within 1 year)
2. Request Substantive Appeal to Review
3. Administrative hearing (optional, can skip to rating decision)
4. Appeal to Appeals Court for Veterans Claims (if still denied)

**VA Appeals Faster than SSA:**

Decision in 6–12 months typical (compared to 1–3 years for Social Security hearing).

Many veterans use VA service officers (free representation).

## State Benefits Appeals

Each state has different appeal process for state programs.

**Check with State:**

Contact state agency administering benefit.

Ask about appeal process.

Follow their specific timeline.

## Timeline and Patience

**Understanding Timelines:**

SSA appeals slow. Expect 1–2+ years from initial denial to final hearing decision.

This is intentional—can take time to gather evidence, schedule hearing.

Meanwhile: You don't receive benefits (unless retroactive payment if win).

**During Waiting:**

Find other income sources: Work if able, family support, emergency assistance.

Use food banks, medical providers offering sliding scale.

Community resources while waiting for appeal.

## Working While Appealing

**If Already on Benefits:**

Work incentive program allows continued work while appealing if already receiving benefits.

Trial work period, IRWE, PASS available.

If denied but appealing: Don't qualify for work incentive benefits yet.

**If Denied and Appealing:**

No restrictions on work while appealing.

Can earn income (helps survival while waiting).

If appeal approved: Retroactive back pay covers period denied (minus amount earned if rules apply).
