# Supplemental Security Income (SSI)

SSI provides federal income to disabled, blind, or elderly people with minimal resources. Unlike SSDI, you don't need work history. You need to be poor. It's a needs-based program.

**Program Type:** Federal assistance (means-tested)  
**Administering Agency:** Social Security Administration (SSA)  
**Geographic Coverage:** Entire United States (states may add supplements)  
**Approximate Monthly Benefit:** $943 federal; states may add more

## Historical Origins and Movement Leadership

SSI replaced earlier state-by-state relief programs (1972). Before SSI, elderly and disabled poor people depended on state welfare—benefit amounts varied wildly. Some states gave almost nothing.

Federal SSI created minimum standard. Disabled poor people had baseline income floor, theoretically. Activists pushed for this recognizing that disability meant both inability to work and immediate poverty.

Blind and elderly included from start. Recognition that disability, old age, and poverty intersect and require safety net.

However, benefit amounts and asset limits set from beginning barely above poverty. Decades later: poverty line has risen, SSI hasn't kept pace. Ongoing organizing pushes for adequate benefits.

## Who Can Access This

**Disability Requirement:** You have severe impairment expected to last 12+ months or result in death. Medical documentation required.

**Income Limit:** Federal maximum SSI (2024) is $943/month for individual, $1,415 for married couple. Income above this reduces benefit dollar-for-dollar.

**Asset Limit:** Maximum $2,000 in assets for individual, $3,000 for married couple. Assets above limit disqualify you.

**Citizenship:** US citizens and legal residents may qualify. Undocumented people cannot.

**Age:** No minimum age (children can qualify). No maximum age (elderly poor qualify).

**Work Status:** You cannot be earning above "substantial gainful activity" threshold (~$1,500/month in 2024).

**Living Arrangement:** Where you live affects benefit amount. Living in someone else's home may reduce payment.

## What SSI Provides

**Monthly Cash Payment** from federal government. Varies: $943 federal base; states add supplements (can be $100–300 more depending on state).

**Medicaid Health Insurance** automatically included. This is critical difference from SSDI. Medicaid covers doctors, hospitals, prescriptions, therapies.

**Food Stamps (SNAP)** often automatically approved when you get SSI.

**Work Incentives** similar to SSDI. Plans to achieve self-support, trial work periods exist for SSI too.

**Continued Coverage** as long as you remain disabled and resource-limited.

## Income and Asset Limits Explained

**Income Limit (how much you can earn):**

You lose $1 of benefit for every $1 of income over $65/month. After $65, only half of remaining income counts.

Example: You earn $200/month.
- First $65 doesn't count
- Remaining $135: Half counts = $67.50
- Your countable income is $67.50
- SSI benefit reduced by $67.50

**What Counts as Income:**

- Wages from employment
- Self-employment earnings
- Unemployment benefits
- Rental income
- Gifts from others (sometimes)
- Interest or dividends
- Royalties
- Food or shelter given to you

**What Doesn't Count as Income:**

- Food and shelter from family/friends (with limits)
- Scholarships for education
- Some disability-related benefits
- SNAP (food stamps)
- Tax refunds
- In-kind support and maintenance exclusions

**Asset Limit (how much you can own):**

Maximum $2,000 in countable resources for individual, $3,000 for married couple.

You lose eligibility if assets exceed limit.

**What Counts as Assets:**

- Bank accounts (savings, checking)
- Vehicles (more than one may not be counted)
- Real property besides home
- Jewelry and collectibles
- Stocks and bonds
- Life insurance (with cash value)

**What Doesn't Count as Assets:**

- Your primary home
- One vehicle
- Work equipment
- Household goods/personal effects
- Burial account ($1,500 set-aside)
- Accessible modifications to home/vehicle
- Training/education equipment

**Special Allowances:**

Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS) lets you set aside money toward work goal.

Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWE) don't count toward income limits.

Student earned income: Part of work earnings don't count if you're in school.

## How to Apply

**Step 1: Gather Documents**
- Birth certificate or passport
- Social Security card
- Bank statements (show assets and income)
- Tax returns (if applicable)
- Medical records documenting disability
- List of medications and treatments
- List of doctors and providers
- Proof of citizenship or legal residency

**Step 2: Complete Application**

Apply online at www.ssa.gov/benefits/ssi or in person at Social Security office.

Form SSA-8 (Application for Supplemental Security Income).

Takes 30–45 minutes. You'll provide:
- Personal information
- Disability information and medical history
- Income information (jobs, support, gifts)
- Asset list (bank accounts, property, vehicles)
- Living arrangement
- Contact information
- Direct deposit banking

**Step 3: Submit**

Online processing faster. In-person at local SSA office.

No application fee.

Request interpreter if needed (SSA must provide at no cost).

**Step 4: Waiting Period**

Initial decision: 3–6 months. SSA requests medical records.

If denied: Request reconsideration within 60 days.

If denied again: Request administrative law judge hearing.

Many people denied initially. Appeals often successful.

**Step 5: Approval and First Payment**

Approval letter arrives by mail. First payment within one month typically.

Medicaid card arrives separately (can take additional weeks).

## Understanding Your SSI Benefits

**Benefit Amounts by State (2024):**

Federal SSI: $943/month for individual

State supplements:
- California: $43 supplement = $986 total
- New York: $74 supplement = $1,017 total
- Texas: No state supplement = $943
- Many states have small supplements ($10–50)

Check your state at www.ssa.gov/benefits/ssi/

**Cost of Living Adjustments (COLA):**

Federal SSI increases annually with inflation. Not immediate—occurs in January.

State supplements vary by state.

**Married Couple Benefit:**

Federal SSI for couple: $1,415/month (49% each, not split evenly).

Each person's benefit calculated separately based on their income and resources.

**Family Support:**

Parents may be able to use SSI while disabled/blind children live with them (slightly higher limits).

Adult children cannot usually help parents on SSI without reducing benefit.

**What's Covered:**

Monthly income for living expenses: rent, food, utilities, transportation, clothing.

Medicaid healthcare (doctors, hospitals, prescriptions).

Some states add small amounts for utilities or essential expenses.

**What's NOT Covered:**

Ongoing personal care assistance (not funded; you can hire with benefits).

Housing assistance (SSI alone below rent in most places; combine with [[Housing Vouchers]]).

Education or training (you can attend school, some income doesn't count).

Medical devices (Medicaid covers most).

Work-related equipment (can use IRWE or PASS).

## How Much You Can Earn While on SSI

**Trial Work Period:** Nine months earning over $1,000/month. You keep full SSI benefit (and Medicaid). Test working without financial risk.

**Extended Eligibility:** Next 36 months. Medicaid continues even if earnings high enough to stop SSI benefit (Medicaid Extended).

**Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWE):** Costs directly related to working with disability don't count toward income.

Examples:
- Medications needed only for work
- Accessible transportation to work
- Personal care assistant at work
- Work-related therapy
- Wheelchairs or mobility aids needed for work
- Assistive technology for work

**Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS):** Most powerful option. Allows you to save money toward work goal (education, equipment, business) without affecting benefits. Must write plan with SSA.

Different calculation than SSDI. Consult work incentive planner for specifics.

## Application Problems & Solutions

**Benefit Too Low:**

Federal benefit below poverty line. Combine with:
- Housing vouchers ([[Housing Vouchers]])
- SNAP ([[SNAP / Food Assistance]])
- State programs ([[State Disability Programs]])
- Work incentives to earn additional income

**Denied Due to Assets:**

If you have $2,001, you're denied. Options:
- Spend down assets on disability-related needs (home modification, vehicle accessibility)
- Use Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS) to save for work goal
- Wait until assets deplete naturally

**Denied Due to Income:**

If you earn too much: Use work incentive exclusions.

Many earned income portions don't count. Ask SSA for work incentive analysis.

**Medicaid Lost:**

If income increases above SSI limit: Medicaid Extended continues 36 more months. Free coverage.

After Medicaid Extended ends: Apply for regular Medicaid (income limits differ). You may still qualify.

**Re-evaluations:**

SSA performs Continuing Disability Reviews (CDRs). They check if still disabled.

After initial approval: CDR might be 1–3 years out or longer.

Keep medical records updated. New treatment or improvement doesn't mean you lose benefits if still have functional limitations.

## Disability Type Specific Tips

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**

Video Remote Interpreters available at SSA offices. Request when scheduling.

Forms: Available in accessible formats. Request large print or electronic.

Medicaid: Covers hearing aids and cochlear implants after initial approval.

**Blind/Low Vision:**

Accessible application materials: Request Braille, large print, or electronic.

Medicaid: Covers vision care, devices, rehabilitation.

SSA office: Bring guide person if needed. Accessible accommodations provided upon request.

**Neurodivergent:**

Complex paperwork challenging. Ask SSA for plain-language explanation.

Medical documentation: Ensure providers document functional impact on daily life and work capacity, not just diagnosis.

In-person interview options: Can do phone or video if office environment is overwhelming.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health Disability:**

Medical documentation critical. SSA requires functional limitations documentation from provider, not just psychiatric diagnosis.

Stigma barrier: You may fear disclosing mental health condition. Disclosure necessary for approval.

Medicaid includes mental health treatment—essential for stability.

**Hidden/Invisible Disabilities:**

Medical documentation essential. You need proof of disability.

Functional limitations key—document how disability affects daily life and capacity for work.

Consistency documentation helpful—show ongoing treatment and functional limitations over time.

**Mobility Disabilities:**

SSA office accessibility: Request accommodations in advance if needed.

Medicaid at approval includes mobility aids, home modifications, personal care.

Vehicle considerations: Can own one vehicle without affecting SSI (resource not counted).

**Multiply-Marginalized:**

Immigration status: Undocumented people cannot access SSI.

Gender and family: Married couples (same-sex and opposite-sex) can have both receive SSI based on combined resources.

Race: SSA discrimination complaint process exists. Document discrimination.

## For Multiple Marginalized People

**Women:** Benefits available equally. Pregnant women: may get emergency assistance before formal approval.

**LGBTQ+:** All benefits available to legally married couples (same-sex and opposite-sex). Unmarried partners don't receive benefits.

**Immigrants:** Legal residents may qualify (Supplemental Security Income for Non-Immigrants—ITIN requirements—complex, consult advocate).

**Undocumented:** Generally cannot access SSI. Some states offer benefits to mixed-status families.

**Victims of domestic violence:** May qualify as dependent even if separated. Spousal resources may not count against limit if separated.

**Incarcerated:** Benefits suspend during incarceration (30+ days). Resume upon release.
