# SNAP: Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program

SNAP (formerly food stamps) provides monthly money to buy food. It reduces hunger for low-income people, including many disabled people.

**Program Type:** Federal assistance (means-tested)  
**Administering Agency:** State Department of Social Services / Food and Agriculture  
**Geographic Coverage:** Entire United States (rules vary by state)  
**Approximate Monthly Benefit:** $200–$350 for individual (varies by state and income)

## Historical Origins and Movement Leadership

Food stamp program began 1939 as agricultural subsidy—government bought surplus food to stabilize farm prices and distribute to poor people.

Modern SNAP established 1964. Civil rights activists, welfare rights organizations, and poor people's movements pushed for recognition that hunger is poverty issue requiring systematic response.

Key organizing: National Welfare Rights Organization (1960s) fought for benefit increases and accessibility.

Expanded repeatedly through 1970s and 1980s as advocates pushed.

Post-welfare reform (1996): Benefits reduced. Organizing continues demanding adequate nutrition support.

## Who Can Access This

**Income Limit:** Maximum income 130% of federal poverty line for most people; some categories (elderly, disabled) 200%.

**Example (2024):** 
- Individual: Maximum income ~$1,435/month
- Family of 3: Maximum income ~$3,045/month
- Elderly/disabled: Slightly higher limits

**Asset Limit:** $2,500 in countable resources for most people; $3,250 for elderly or disabled.

**Citizenship:** US citizens and certain legal residents. Undocumented people cannot receive.

**Work Requirement:** Generally required to work or look for work if able-bodied adult. Disabled and elderly exempt.

**Living Arrangement:** Can live with others, still eligible (benefits for individuals added together).

**Age:** No minimum age. Families, elderly, disabled people all eligible.

## What SNAP Provides

**Monthly Benefits** deposited on EBT card (Electronic Benefits Transfer). Like debit card for groceries.

**Amount Based On:**
- Household size
- Household income
- Household expenses (rent, utilities, childcare)

**Formula (simplified):**
Net monthly income × 30% = Approximate monthly benefit.

Maximum benefit per month (2024): $1,018 for individual; $4,779 for family of 8.

**Benefit Increase:** Standard Utility Allowance (SUA) counts certain utility expenses, increasing benefits.

**What You Can Buy:**
- Fruits and vegetables
- Meat, poultry, fish
- Dairy products
- Breads and cereals
- Snacks and nonalcoholic beverages
- Seeds and plants producing food

**What You Cannot Buy:**
- Hot/prepared foods
- Alcohol or tobacco
- Vitamins/medicines
- Pet food
- Household items (soap, paper products)
- Cosmetics

## How to Apply

**Step 1: Gather Documents**
- Social Security number
- Birth certificate or ID
- Proof of citizenship
- Recent pay stubs (if working) or SSA statement
- Rent/mortgage statements
- Utility bills
- List of household members
- Contact information

**Step 2: Complete Application**

Online at state website (easiest). Each state differs—search "[Your State] SNAP application."

Phone: Call state SNAP office. They mail application.

In person: Visit state office (DSHS, Social Services, TANF office).

Application takes 15–30 minutes.

Provide:
- Household members and income
- Expenses (rent, utilities, childcare)
- Assets and resources
- Immigration status
- Work status

**Step 3: Interview**

Phone or in-person interview (state choice, request preference).

Verify information you provided.

Ask about disability, income sources, expenses.

**Step 4: Decision**

Expedited SNAP: Decision within 7 days if eligible.

Standard SNAP: Decision within 30 days.

Approval letter arrives. Benefits loaded on EBT card.

First benefit available within 3–5 days.

**Step 5: Card Use**

EBT card works like debit card at grocery stores, farmers markets, some online retailers.

PIN required (you choose number).

Balance shows eligible spending, not cash balance.

## Understanding Your SNAP Benefit

**Monthly Amount Examples (individual, 2024):**

Income $500/month, no expenses: ~$285/month SNAP  
Income $800/month, $400 rent: ~$280/month SNAP  
Income $1,000/month, $600 rent, $150 utilities: ~$150/month SNAP  
Income $1,400/month (maximum): $0/month (exceeds limit)

**How Benefit Calculated:**

1. Subtract 20% of gross income: Earned Income Deduction
2. Subtract allowable expenses: Standard Deduction, Dependent Care, Disability-Related, Child Support paid
3. Multiply remaining by 30%: That's approximate benefit
4. Subtract any cash assistance received
5. Remaining is SNAP benefit (capped at state maximum)

Complex formula—state workers calculate.

**Important:** Increase in any income, decrease in expenses, or change in household affects benefit.

## How SNAP Works with Other Benefits

**If You Receive SSI:**

SSI automatically approved for SNAP in most states. Simplified application.

Can receive both SSI ($943) and SNAP ($280+) for total ~$1,200+ monthly.

**If You Receive SSDI:**

Must apply for SNAP separately. Income over SNAP limit may disqualify you.

Depends on income amount.

**If You Work:**

Earned Income Deduction: 20% of work income doesn't count against SNAP limit.

Work incentive program benefits: Can set aside money through PASS or IRWE, doesn't reduce SNAP.

**If You Receive Other Benefits:**

Benefits count toward income limit.

Unemployment benefits, child support, disability, all count.

## Common Issues & Solutions

**Denied for Asset Limit:**

If you have $2,501 in resources, technically ineligible.

Options: Spend down on disability-related needs, pay down debt, save for essential purchase.

Shelter/Utility Allowance can increase benefit enough to reach limit without reducing resources.

**Income Slightly Over Limit:**

Shelter and Utility Allowance (SUA) often lowers countable income enough to qualify.

Dependent Care Deduction if paying for childcare.

Disability-Related Expense Deduction if disabled paying for disability support.

**Card Declined at Store:**

PIN incorrect (reenter PIN, maximum 3 attempts, card locks temporarily).

Insufficient funds (check balance).

Merchant doesn't accept EBT (not all stores do, some items excluded).

Card lost: Call 1-800-997-1111 to replace (varies by state).

**Benefit Reduced Without Notice:**

State conducts review. Income or household change identified.

Can request reconsideration if think mistake.

Work requirement: If able-bodied adult not meeting work requirement, benefits reduced or stopped.

**Underpayment:**

Many people underpaid—not getting full benefit entitled to.

Request recalculation if believe error.

Advocate or legal aid can help.

## Disability Type Specific Tips

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**

Video relay service (VRS) for phone interview: Request when scheduling.

Interpreter at office: Request when applying in person.

Written information: Ensure materials are accessible.

**Blind/Low Vision:**

Large print application materials: Request.

Phone interview instead of in-person: Request if office visit difficult.

Accessible online application: Check state website accessibility.

**Neurodivergent:**

Complex application challenging. Bring advocate or support person.

Ask for plain-language explanation: State workers can simplify.

Phone instead of in-person: Request if sensory environment overwhelming.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health Disability:**

Benefit can help reduce stress around food security.

Vulnerability: Work requirement waiver if disability prevents work.

Advocate helpful if struggling with interviews, paperwork.

**Hidden/Invisible Disabilities:**

Medical documentation may be needed for:
- Standard Utility Allowance (if heating/cooling essential for disability)
- Shelter and Utility Allowance (if disabled, higher allowance sometimes available)
- Disability-Related Expense Deduction

Get medical letter documenting disability-related expenses.

**Mobility Disabilities:**

Accessibility at office: Bring support person if needed.

Phone interview option: Request if office visit difficult.

Driver assistance: Spend card at stores with delivery options.

**Multiply-Marginalized:**

Immigration status: Undocumented cannot access.

Gender-based violence: Waiver available for work requirement if experiencing abuse.

Incarceration: Benefits suspend if imprisoned 30+ days, resume upon release.

## For Multiple Marginalized People

**Women:** All eligible equally. Pregnant women: Expedited eligibility in some states.

**LGBTQ+:** No discrimination based on sexual orientation or gender identity.

**Immigrants:** Only citizens and certain legal residents. Mixed-status families: Only eligible members receive benefits.

**Incarcerated:** Benefits pause during imprisonment. Resume upon release.

**Formerly incarcerated:** Eligible same as anyone else (some states have restrictions, check yours).

## Work and SNAP

**Work Requirement:**

Able-bodied adults without dependents must work 20 hours/week or participate in work program.

Disabled people, elderly, parents of minor children exempt.

Minimum wage job typically sufficient.

**Earned Income Deduction:**

First 20% of earned income doesn't count.

Example: Earn $500/month, only $400 counts against income limit.

Encourages work—earning more doesn't immediately reduce benefits.

**Work Incentive Benefits:**

Plan to Achieve Self-Support (PASS): Can set aside income for work goal.

Impairment-Related Work Expenses: Disability-related work costs don't count.

Benefits remain available through trial work period.

## Recertification & Continued Eligibility

**Recertification Period:**

Every 12 months (sometimes every 24 months depending on state and situation).

Letter arrives reminding you to recertify.

Submit updated income, expense information.

Simple form or phone interview.

**Reporting Changes:**

New job, income change, moved, household change must be reported within 10 days (varies by state).

Failure to report can cause overpayment (owing back benefits) or disqualification.

Easy to report online, by phone, or in person.

**Overpayment:**

If received benefits you weren't entitled to: State seeks repayment.

Can request waiver if overpayment due to state error.

Can set up payment plan if overpayment your fault.
