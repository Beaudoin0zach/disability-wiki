# Section 8 Housing Vouchers and Public Housing

Housing vouchers (Section 8) help low-income people afford rent. Program pays portion of rent; you pay remainder.

**Program Type:** Federal housing assistance  
**Administering Agency:** Department of Housing & Urban Development (HUD), local public housing authorities  
**Geographic Coverage:** Entire United States (varies by locality)  
**Approximate Subsidy:** $300–$1,500/month depending on market, family size, income

## Historical Origins and Movement Leadership

Public housing created 1937 as safe housing for low-income families. Concept: Families shouldn't be homeless.

Section 8 created 1974 as alternative to public housing. Uses voucher system—tenants choose apartments; program pays portion of rent to landlord.

Civil rights organizing pushed for housing desegregation and accessibility. Disability advocates fought for Section 8 accessibility requirements.

Independent living movement (1970s+) pushed for housing freedom—away from group homes toward individual apartment living. Section 8 essential to this.

Today: Waiting lists long (often years). Advocacy continues demanding increased funding and accessibility.

## Who Can Access This

**Income Limit:** Generally 50% of Area Median Income (AMI) for initial qualification. Varies by locality.

**Example:** 
- Metropolitan area: 50% AMI ~$30,000/year for individual, $43,000 for family of 3
- Rural area: 50% AMI ~$22,000/year for individual

Check your local public housing authority (PHA) for exact limits.

**Asset Limit:** Generally $5,000–$8,000 in assets (higher than SSI/SSDI). Check local rules.

**Citizenship:** US citizens and legal residents. Undocumented cannot access.

**Disability:** Not required but given preference in many localities.

**Family Composition:** Any family size, single adults, elderly, disabled all eligible.

**Work Status:** No work requirement to access housing (though income must be below limit).

## How Section 8 Works

**Waiting List Phase:**

Apply at local public housing authority. Join waiting list.

Wait time: Can be months to years depending on locality.

Some cities: 1–2 year wait  
Some cities: 5–10+ year wait  
Some areas: No wait (program well-funded)

Check PHA website for estimated wait time.

**Voucher Phase:**

Receive voucher (permission to search). Typically 60–120 days to find apartment.

Search for apartment in your price range within rent limits set by HUD (Fair Market Rent for your area).

Landlord must accept voucher (but can refuse for other reasons like rental history).

**Lease Phase:**

Find apartment you can afford within FMR limit.

Work out lease with landlord (sets amount you'll pay, PHA pays rest).

PHA inspects apartment (must meet HUD standards).

PHA pays portion directly to landlord; you pay remainder.

**Ongoing Phase:**

Pay your share of rent monthly.

PHA adjusts your contribution based on income changes annually.

Recertification required yearly.

**Portability (Moving):**

Can move while on voucher (to different apartment, different PHA jurisdiction).

PHA transfers voucher. New PHA becomes administrator.

Helpful for disabled people moving closer to family, better services, or different climate.

## What PHA Pays vs. What You Pay

**PHA Payment Calculation:**

PHA pays difference between 30% of your income and rent amount (up to Fair Market Rent).

Formula: PHA payment = FMR minus (30% of your income)

Example calculation:

Your income: $1,000/month  
30% of income: $300  
Fair Market Rent (FMR) for apartment: $900  
Rent you want to pay: $850  

PHA payment: $850 − $300 = $550  
Your rent contribution: $300/month

If rent higher than FMR, you pay difference (PHA won't pay beyond FMR).

**Your Payment (Tenant Portion):**

Minimum: Usually 30% of income or $0 (whichever is greater).

Maximum: Amount set in lease (cannot exceed FMR).

Recalculated annually based on income.

Income increase: Your portion increases  
Income decrease: Your portion decreases  

**Utilities:**

Landlord pays some utilities (water, sewer usually).

Tenant pays others (electricity, gas, cable typically).

Standard Utility Allowance deducts allowable utility costs from 30% calculation.

If you pay utilities: You pay less rent because utilities already accounted for.

## Finding an Apartment with Section 8

**Challenges:**

Some landlords refuse Section 8 tenants (discrimination, but illegal).

Landlords sometimes quote higher rent to Section 8 tenants than to others (also illegal).

Landlords may require higher credit scores, income requirements.

Apartments in desirable neighborhoods often at or above FMR, making them unaffordable.

**Strategies:**

Research FMR limit for your area—determine maximum rent you can access.

Contact disability-friendly landlords (organizations have lists).

Request disability accommodations (landlord must allow reasonable modifications).

Use online search for Section 8 accepting apartments.

Ask PHA for landlord list (some have pre-qualified landlords).

Network with other voucher holders (they know which landlords accept).

## Accessibility and Reasonable Accommodations

**Fair Housing Act Requirement:**

Landlords must make reasonable accommodations to policies, practices, procedures.

Cannot charge extra for accessibility modifications.

Cannot refuse lease because of disability.

Examples of reasonable accommodations:

**Physical Modifications:**
- Wheelchair ramp installation
- Accessible bathroom modifications (grab bars, roll-in shower, raised toilet)
- Lowered cabinets, counters
- Widened doorways
- Accessible parking space

**Policy Accommodations:**
- Service animal (not classified as pet; no pet fee)
- Emotional support animal (reasonable accommodation)
- Flexible lease terms due to disability
- Accessible payment methods (phone, online if have disability)

**Procedure Accommodations:**
- Application in accessible format (large print, Braille, electronic)
- Extra time to apply due to disability
- Interview via phone/video if office inaccessible
- Interpreter at lease signing

**Request Process:**

Make request in writing (email, letter, formal form).

Explain why accommodation needed.

Landlord cannot charge for reasonable accommodations.

If landlord refuses: File complaint with HUD.

## How Section 8 Works with Other Benefits

**SSI/SSDI Income Calculation:**

Rent portion of SSI/SSDI (30%) flows directly to PHA.

Allows very low-income SSI recipients to live independently.

Example: SSI $943 → Pay $283 rent → PHA subsidy covers rest.

**Working While on Section 8:**

Section 8 not tied to work requirement.

Can work and earn, still on Section 8.

Income increase reduces PHA subsidy proportionally.

**SNAP/Food Assistance:**

Section 8 housing assistance doesn't affect SNAP eligibility.

Receive both simultaneously.

**Housing Assistance and Asset Limits:**

Asset limits for Section 8 (~$5,000–$8,000) higher than SSI ($2,000).

Can own some assets while on Section 8 (car, home furnishings, savings).

## Common Challenges & Solutions

**Long Waiting Lists:**

Problem: Years-long wait in many cities.

Solutions:
- Request preference (disabled people given priority in some PHAs)
- Ask about emergency preference (homelessness, disability crisis)
- Consider moving to area with shorter wait
- Advocate locally for increased funding
- Emergency shelter while waiting

**Landlord Discrimination:**

Problem: Landlord refuses Section 8 tenant or demands extra deposit/requirements.

Solution: File HUD complaint. Protected by Fair Housing Act.

Document discrimination (keep emails, write down conversations, dates).

Contact fair housing organization (PHA can provide contact info).

**Finding Accessible Apartment:**

Problem: Few apartments within FMR accessible.

Solutions:
- Request reasonable accommodation (modification of existing apartment)
- Request FMR exception (pay higher rent if no accessible options)
- Use voucher portability to move to different PHA
- Contact disability organization—many maintain lists of accessible landlords

**Disability Accommodation Denied:**

Problem: Landlord refuses reasonable accommodation request.

Solution:
- Write formal request in letter/email
- Explain why accommodation necessary (disability connection)
- Offer to pay for modification if reasonable
- Document refusal (keep all communication)
- File HUD Fair Housing complaint

**Rent Increase:**

Problem: Landlord raises rent above new FMR limit or above what PHA will pay.

Solution:
- Work out different lease terms
- Move to different apartment
- Negotiate with landlord (sometimes possible)
- Request PHA explore other options

## Disability Type Specific Issues

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**

Request interpreter at lease signing, inspections, landlord meetings (free, landlord required).

Video relay service: Can use for phone calls to PHA/landlord.

Accessible building design: Request bright lights for doorbells, alarm system.

**Blind/Low Vision:**

Accessible building design: Request accessible parking near entrance, handrails, braille signage.

Guide dog: Landlord must allow service animal. Not classified as pet.

Companion dog: Reasonable accommodation if disability-related.

Accessible apartment features: Accessible bathroom, kitchen layout.

**Neurodivergent:**

Sensory accessibility: Quiet unit away from noise if noise triggers.

Flexible lease: Request if need flexibility due to inconsistent work capacity.

Extra support during application: Request plain language explanation of requirements.

**Psychosocial/Mental Health Disability:**

Service dog: Reasonable accommodation if trained for psychiatric alert/support.

Quiet environment: Request placement away from high-traffic areas.

Landlord understanding: Disclosure not required but may help explain needs.

**Hidden/Invisible Disabilities:**

Accommodation doesn't require visible disability.

State medical need clearly in request.

Emotional support animal: If prescribed by healthcare provider, reasonable accommodation.

**Mobility Disabilities:**

Accessibility requirements: Ground floor unit, accessible parking, ramp, accessible bathroom.

Lift-equipped bathroom: Request grab bars, adjustable shower, raised toilet.

Elevator building: If mobility aid user, request building with elevator.

**Multiply-Marginalized:**

Some PHAs prioritize certain populations (domestic violence survivors, homeless, elderly/disabled).

Check local PHA preferences.

## For Multiple Marginalized People

**Domestic Violence Survivors:** Emergency preference sometimes available. Lease protections exist (may allow tenant to break lease without penalty if leaving violent situation).

**Formerly Incarcerated:** Eligible for Section 8. Some PHAs have barriers; others supportive.

**LGBTQ+:** No discrimination allowed. Lease jointly with same-sex partner same as opposite-sex.

**Gender-Nonconforming:** Housing application gender identity respected. Request at application if concerned.

**Immigrants:** Legal residents eligible. Undocumented cannot access.

**Formerly Homeless:** Often priority category. Check local PHA.

## Public Housing vs. Section 8

**Public Housing:**
- You live in PHA-owned building
- Income limits similar
- Rent formula identical (30% of income)
- Limited supply
- Long waiting lists
- Landlord management by PHA
- All disabled people eligible

**Section 8 Voucher:**
- You choose apartment; landlord owns
- Income limits similar
- Rent formula identical
- Larger apartment supply
- Still waiting lists but more options
- Landlord is private
- Disabled people given preference in many areas

Most people prefer Section 8 (more choice in apartment and location).

Public housing useful for those without long wait.

## How to Apply for Section 8

**Step 1: Locate Local PHA**

Google "[Your City/County] Public Housing Authority."

Or: Visit HUD.gov, search your city.

PHA handles Section 8 for your area.

**Step 2: Check Waiting List Status**

Visit PHA website. Check if accepting applications.

Some PHAs closed to new applicants (waiting list full).

Others accept year-round.

**Step 3: Gather Documents**
- ID
- Social Security number
- Proof of income (pay stub, SSA statement)
- Proof of citizenship/legal residency
- Birth certificates (all household members)
- Contact information

**Step 4: Complete Application**

Online (many PHAs offer) or in person.

Takes 30–60 minutes.

Provide household members, income, disability status.

Request disability preference if applicable.

Request reasonable accommodation if need application accessible.

**Step 5: Wait**

Added to waiting list (may take weeks to process application).

Estimated wait time provided (check PHA website for current estimate).

PHA will contact when voucher available.

**Step 6: Receive Voucher**

Contact PHA office.

Attend briefing (explanation of program).

Receive voucher (permission to search for apartment).

Have 60–120 days to find apartment.

**Step 7: Search and Lease**

Find apartment within FMR limit.

Landlord accepts voucher.

PHA inspects (housing quality standards).

Lease signed.

PHA starts subsidy.
