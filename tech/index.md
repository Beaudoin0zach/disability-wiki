# Technology & Digital Access

Technology can be both a lifeline and a barrier for disabled people.

This section focuses on:

- Making websites, apps, and documents accessible  
- Using built-in accessibility features on phones, tablets, and computers  
- Communication access and AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication)  
- Accessible and inclusive gaming  
- Accessible social media practices  
- The bigger picture of digital disability justice

It’s meant for:

- Disabled people figuring out tools that actually work for them  
- Developers, designers, and content creators who want to build access in from the start  
- Advocates, educators, and professionals trying to push their organizations to do better

## How to Use This Section

- If you’re a **user**:  
  - Look for practical tips, lists of features, and language you can copy into accommodation requests.  
  - Share what works or doesn’t work for you in different tools and platforms.

- If you’re a **developer or designer**:  
  - Use these pages as a starting point, not an end point.  
  - Link to formal standards (like WCAG) and platform guidelines, then translate them into everyday language.

- If you’re an **advocate or professional**:  
  - Pull phrases, examples, and checklists to push for better digital access where you work.

## How You Can Contribute

Helpful contributions could include:

- Examples of accessible and inaccessible designs, with screenshots (redacted where needed)  
- How-to guides for specific platforms or tools  
- Links to open-source accessibility resources  
- Explanations of access barriers that are often ignored (e.g., cognitive load, motion sensitivity, executive function demands)

Try to:

- Use clear headings and short paragraphs  
- Mention when something is specific to a region (e.g., a particular law)  
- Focus on what disabled people actually experience, not just compliance checklists
