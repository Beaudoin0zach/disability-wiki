# Insurance Navigation: Understanding Healthcare Coverage

Healthcare in the US requires insurance. Understanding your insurance is critical—it determines what healthcare you can afford and access.

**Key Point:** Insurance confusing by design. You're not stupid if you don't understand it. System intentionally complex to limit claims.

## Insurance in the US

**Basic Concept:** Insurance company and provider negotiate prices. You pay premium (monthly cost), copay (per-visit), deductible (yearly amount before insurance pays), and coinsurance (percentage you pay after deductible).

**Insurance doesn't mean free healthcare.** Even insured people pay out-of-pocket.

## Types of Insurance

### Medicare (Federal)

**Who qualifies:**

- Disabled people (any age) on SSDI after 24 months
- People 65+
- People with End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD)
- People with ALS

**Coverage:**

- Part A: Hospital care
- Part B: Doctor visits, outpatient care
- Part D: Prescription drugs

**Costs:**

- Part B premium: $176–230/month (2024)
- Deductible: $1,600 hospital (2024)
- Copays/coinsurance: 20% of cost after deductible

**For low-income:**

- Medicare Savings Programs (MSP): Reduce premiums, deductibles, copays
- Extra Help: Reduce prescription drug costs
- Apply through SSA or Medicaid office

**Strengths:**

- Automatic at 24 months on SSDI
- Covers nationwide
- Stable benefits

**Weaknesses:**

- High deductibles
- Copays add up
- Prescription drug coverage gap ("donut hole")
- Limited coverage (dental, vision, hearing aids not covered)

### Medicaid (State)

**Who qualifies:**

Income below 100–300% of federal poverty level (varies by state).

Specific categories (disabled, elderly, parent of minor, pregnant, child).

**Coverage:**

Varies by state. Mandatory coverage includes:
- Doctor visits
- Hospital care
- Prescription drugs

Optional coverage by state:
- Dental
- Vision
- Hearing aids
- Mental health services
- Personal care services

**Costs:**

Usually free or very low copays ($1–4) for disabled people.

Sometimes small copays for services, but often none.

**For disabled people:**

Automatic if on SSI in most states.

Can qualify separately if low-income (not on SSI).

**Strengths:**

- Very low costs
- Covers many services optional in other insurance
- Some states generous

**Weaknesses:**

- Provider networks smaller (fewer accept Medicaid)
- Income limits low (forces poverty)
- Services limited in restrictive states
- Stigma (some providers treat Medicaid patients poorly)
- State-to-state variation huge

### Private Insurance

**From:**

- Employer (most common)
- Individual purchase
- Healthcare.gov marketplace

**Coverage:**

Varies by plan. Basic coverage includes:
- Doctor visits
- Hospital care
- Prescription drugs

Specific coverage depends on plan chosen.

**Costs:**

- Premium: $200–1,500+/month (varies by plan, age, location)
- Deductible: $500–5,000+/year
- Copay: $20–50 per visit
- Coinsurance: 10–40% after deductible
- Out-of-pocket maximum: $5,000–$10,000+/year

**Strengths:**

- Wide provider networks
- Usually good coverage
- Choice of plans

**Weaknesses:**

- Expensive
- High deductibles
- Pre-existing condition exclusions (now illegal but some work-arounds)
- Tied to employment (lose job = lose insurance)

### Veterans Healthcare

**Who qualifies:**

Military service with honorable discharge.

Service-connected disability priority.

Non-service-connected also eligible.

**Coverage:**

VA hospital care, doctor visits, prescriptions, mental health, and specialty care.

**Costs:**

Usually free for high-rating disabled veterans.

Copays for lower-rated veterans ($15–50).

**Strengths:**

- Comprehensive
- Often free/low-cost
- Specializes in veteran needs (PTSD, military sexual trauma, TBI)

**Weaknesses:**

- Limited provider network (must use VA)
- Long wait times
- Not available everywhere
- Must manage care through VA

### Uninsured or Underinsured

Many disabled people fall into this category.

**Limited options:**

- Community Health Centers: Low-cost/free care
- Hospital charity care: Hospitals required to provide some uninsured care
- Public clinics: City/county health departments
- Medicaid emergency coverage: Emergency care covered even if not eligible for Medicaid
- Medication assistance programs: Pharmaceutical companies offer free/reduced medications

## Understanding Your Insurance: Key Terms

### Premium

What you pay monthly for insurance. Non-refundable even if don't use care.

Example: $300/month premium = $3,600/year.

### Deductible

Amount you must pay out-of-pocket before insurance starts paying.

Example: $2,000 deductible. You pay first $2,000 of medical costs. After $2,000, insurance helps pay (you still pay copay/coinsurance).

**Deductible resets yearly** (usually January).

### Copay

Fixed amount you pay per visit.

Example: $30 copay = you pay $30 per doctor visit, insurance pays rest.

Different copay for different services:
- Doctor visit: $30
- Specialist: $50
- Emergency room: $250
- Prescriptions: $10–50 depending on drug

### Coinsurance

Percentage you pay after meeting deductible.

Example: 20% coinsurance = you pay 20%, insurance pays 80%, after deductible met.

High coinsurance % = more you pay.

### Out-of-Pocket Maximum

Yearly limit on what you pay. After hitting maximum, insurance pays 100%.

Example: $5,000 out-of-pocket maximum. After you've paid $5,000 in copays/coinsurance/deductible, insurance covers 100% rest of year.

**Critical for disabled people:** Hit out-of-pocket maximum often (with chronic illness, many doctor visits). After hitting max, care free rest of year.

### Provider Network

List of doctors, hospitals, and healthcare facilities insurance covers.

**In-network:** Providers insurance has contracted with. You pay less.

**Out-of-network:** Providers not in insurance network. You pay more (sometimes lots more).

**Example:**

In-network doctor visit: $30 copay  
Out-of-network doctor visit: You pay full price (~$150–300), insurance reimburses small amount

**Important:** Always verify provider is in-network before going. Surprise out-of-network bills common.

### Formulary

List of medications insurance covers.

Insurance divides medications into tiers:

- Tier 1 (generic): $5–10 copay
- Tier 2 (name-brand): $30–50 copay
- Tier 3 (specialty): $75–150+ copay
- Not covered: You pay full price

**Problem:** Your needed medication might not be on formulary, or be high tier (expensive copay).

**Solution:** Ask for prior authorization (insurance pre-approves medication), request formulary exception (insurance covers off-formulary medication), or ask doctor for generic alternative.

## How to Understand Your Specific Insurance

### Find Your Insurance Documents

Look for:

- Insurance card (shows policy number, group number, coverage information)
- Explanation of Benefits (EOB) - shows what insurance paid for your claims
- Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) - document explaining what's covered
- Insurance company website - detailed coverage information
- Insurance company customer service - call to ask questions

### Find Answers to Key Questions

**Coverage Questions:**

"Is [specific doctor/hospital] in my network?"

"Does my plan cover [specific medication/service]?"

"What's my deductible for this year?"

"How much have I paid toward my deductible?"

"What's my out-of-pocket maximum?"

"Do I need prior authorization for [service]?"

**Cost Questions:**

"How much will [service] cost me?"

"What's my copay for [service]?"

"What if I go out-of-network?"

### Read Your Explanation of Benefits (EOB)

Insurance sends EOB after each claim. Shows:

- Service provided
- Provider charged amount
- Insurance allowed amount (negotiated price)
- What insurance paid
- What you owe

**How to read:**

Check you received service listed. Check dates match your appointments.

If charge seems wrong, call insurance.

Keep copies: Important for tracking costs, fighting denials, appealing.

## Prescription Drug Coverage

Medications often significant healthcare cost.

**Understanding drug tiers:**

Insurance negotiates prices with pharmaceutical companies. Drugs divided into tiers by cost.

Tier 1 (generic): Cheapest copay ($5–15)
Tier 2 (name-brand): Medium copay ($30–50)
Tier 3 (specialty): High copay ($75–200+)
Tier 4 (ultra-specialty): Very high copay ($250–500+)

**Problem:** Your medication might be high tier even though generic available.

**Solution:**

- Ask doctor for generic alternative
- Request prior authorization (insurance may approve despite high tier)
- Use Goodrx.com to compare prices (sometimes cheaper paying out-of-pocket at pharmacy than copay)
- Use manufacturer coupon (pharmaceutical companies offer free/reduced drugs)
- Use 340B program (eligible people get deep discounts)
- Apply for Prescription Assistance Programs (free/reduced drugs for low-income)

**Medication Assistance Programs:**

Pharmaceutical companies offer free/reduced medications to low-income people.

Search Needymeds.org or 1800Flowers prescription assistance site.

Call drug manufacturer (number on bottle) to ask about assistance.

Often free if income low enough.

## Prior Authorization

Insurance requires "approval" before covering some services.

**Common services requiring prior authorization:**

- Specialist visits
- Surgeries
- Expensive medications
- Therapy (number of visits)
- Medical equipment

**How it works:**

1. Doctor requests prior authorization from insurance
2. Insurance reviews and approves/denies
3. You can proceed (if approved) or appeal (if denied)

**Delays:** Prior auth process delays care. Can take days to weeks.

**If urgent:** Emphasize urgency. Insurance may expedite review.

**If denied:** Doctor can appeal or request expedited review.

## Fighting Insurance Denials

Insurance often denies claims incorrectly.

**If claim denied:**

**Step 1: Understand reason**

Insurance provides explanation. Common reasons:
- Service not covered by your plan
- Service deemed not medically necessary
- Prior authorization not obtained
- Out-of-network provider
- Claim filed incorrectly

Read reason carefully.

**Step 2: Appeal**

Contact insurance (number on denial letter).

Request appeal (formal process to reconsider denial).

Provide additional documentation:
- Doctor's letter explaining medical necessity
- Medical records supporting treatment
- Research showing treatment standard for your condition

**Step 3: External Review**

If insurance denies appeal: Request external review (independent reviewer evaluates).

Your state insurance commissioner can mandate external review.

Many appeals won at this stage.

**Step 4: Complaint**

File complaint with your state insurance commissioner.

Can result in fine against insurance company.

**Work with advocate:**

- Hospital patient advocate
- Patient Advocate Foundation
- Disability Rights [Your State]
- Insurance complaint attorney

## Changing Insurance

**If need to change insurance:**

Options:
- Switch employer (change jobs = change insurance)
- Marketplace (healthcare.gov): Open enrollment October–December each year
- Medicaid: Apply anytime if eligible
- Medicare: Automatic if eligible
- COBRA: Continue employer insurance 18 months after job loss (expensive—you pay full premium + admin fee)

**Timing matters:** Gaps in coverage dangerous for chronically ill disabled people.

Plan transitions carefully.

## Insurance and Disability Benefits

**Medicare with SSDI:**

Automatic after 24 months on SSDI.

Can have both Medicare and Medicaid (called "dual eligible").

**Medicaid with SSI:**

Automatic at SSI approval in most states.

**State variations:** Some states have different rules. Check with your state Medicaid office.

**Work incentives:** Can work and keep benefits through work incentive programs. Insurance continues.

## Understanding Insurance Discrimination

**Illegal discrimination:**

- Pre-existing condition exclusion (now illegal)
- Refusing coverage based on disability
- Charging more based on disability
- Not providing accessibility (interpreter, accessible materials)

**Happens anyway:** Insurance companies sometimes break law.

**What to do:**

- File complaint with Office for Civil Rights (federal)
- File complaint with state insurance commissioner
- Consult disability rights attorney
- Report to National Association of Insurance Commissioners
