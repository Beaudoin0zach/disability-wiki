# Patient Rights

You have legal and ethical rights during healthcare. Healthcare providers must respect these rights. If your rights violated, you can file complaints.

**Key Point:** Your rights don't disappear when you enter a healthcare setting. You remain a human with legal protections.

## Legal Rights During Healthcare

### Right to Informed Consent

You have right to understand medical treatment before agreeing.

**What this means:**

Provider must explain:
- What condition they believe you have
- What treatment they recommend
- Why they recommend it
- What alternatives exist
- What risks exist with treatment
- What happens if you refuse treatment

You have right to:
- Ask questions
- Take time to decide
- Get information in accessible format (large print, Braille, interpreter)
- Understand before deciding
- Refuse treatment

**Critical:** Consent must be voluntary. No coercion.

**This applies to:**
- Medications
- Procedures
- Tests
- Hospitalizations
- Mental health treatment
- Disability-related treatments

**Exception:** Emergency life-threatening situation. Provider can treat without consent if can't reach you or you unable to communicate.

### Right to Refuse Treatment

You have right to refuse any treatment, medication, or procedure.

Provider may:
- Try to convince you otherwise
- Explain medical consequences
- Document your refusal in medical record
- Request you sign form saying you understand consequences

Provider cannot:
- Force treatment (except emergency, extreme cases)
- Punish you for refusal
- Discharge you immediately for refusing (in most cases)

**Mental health exception:** Some states allow forced commitment and treatment if believe you danger to self/others. But rules vary by state and are limited.

**If you refuse treatment provider insists on:**
- Get second opinion from different provider
- File complaint with hospital administration
- Contact your state patient advocate

### Right to Privacy and Confidentiality

Medical information belongs to you. Providers cannot share without permission.

**What's protected:**

- Medical conditions
- Medications
- Mental health information
- Sexual/reproductive information
- Substance use
- HIV/AIDS status
- Genetic information
- Any health information

**Who can access (without permission):**

- You
- Your healthcare providers (need-to-know basis)
- Your designated healthcare representative/power of attorney
- Insurance company (to process claims)
- Government (in limited circumstances—public health emergency, court order)

**HIPAA:** Federal law protecting privacy (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act).

**Breaking HIPAA:** Can be federal complaint. Provider faces fines, potential license loss.

**In practice:** Privacy breaches happen. Document if occurs.

**What to do if privacy breached:**

- Ask why information was shared
- File complaint with HIPAA Office for Civil Rights
- File complaint with state medical board
- In some cases, can sue for damages

### Right to Access Your Medical Records

You have right to copies of your medical records.

**What you can access:**

- Doctor notes from all visits
- Test results
- Medication lists
- Hospital records
- Mental health records
- Therapy notes

**Provider can refuse:**

- If believe you might harm yourself (rare exception)
- Never as punishment

**How to request:**

Contact provider's medical records department in writing (email, letter).

State: "I request copy of my medical records."

Provider must comply in 10–30 days (varies by state).

Small fee may apply (usually $1–10).

Can request in accessible format (electronic, large print, Braille).

**Why access your records:**

- Verify accuracy
- Review provider's notes (sometimes contain mistakes or biased language)
- Share with other providers
- Document quality of care
- Appeal insurance denials
- Prepare for legal action if needed

### Right to Interpreter Services

If you don't speak English or are Deaf, you have right to interpreter.

**What's covered:**

- Video Remote Interpreter (VRI) for Deaf people
- In-person interpreter
- Written interpretation for Deaf-blind people
- Language interpreters for non-English speakers

**Who pays:**

Provider pays. You cannot be charged for interpreter.

**In practice:** Providers often try to avoid providing interpreters. Insist.

**If provider refuses:**

- Request in writing
- File ADA complaint
- Contact disability rights organization

### Right to Accommodations

Healthcare providers must make reasonable accommodations for disabilities.

**Examples of accommodations:**

**Physical Accessibility:**
- Accessible exam room
- Accessible parking
- Accessible bathroom
- Lowered check-in counter
- Seating without arms (for people whose bodies don't fit standard chairs)

**Sensory Accommodations:**
- Large print materials
- Braille
- Electronic formats
- Verbal explanation
- Captions
- Extra lighting

**Communication Accommodations:**
- Written materials
- Extra time
- Simplified explanation
- Speech-to-text software
- Communication boards
- Quiet space (for neurodivergent people)

**Behavioral Accommodations:**
- Flexible scheduling (can't handle rigid appointment times due to disability)
- Longer appointments
- Limit number of providers seen
- Reduce sensory stimulation
- Allow support person in room

**How to request:**

Tell provider what you need. Can be verbal or written.

Example: "I'm Deaf and need a Video Remote Interpreter for my appointment."

Provider must provide or arrange accommodation without undue burden.

**If provider refuses:**

- Request in writing
- Document refusal
- File ADA complaint
- Contact disability rights organization

### Right to Non-Discrimination

Healthcare providers cannot discriminate based on disability.

**What's prohibited:**

- Refusing to treat you because of disability
- Charging you more because of disability
- Providing lower quality care because of disability
- Assuming you can't make decisions about your care
- Refusing based on false assumption about your disability
- Segregating you from non-disabled patients

**In practice:** Discrimination happens. Documenting important.

**If you experience discrimination:**

- Tell provider that behavior is discriminatory
- File complaint with hospital administration
- File complaint with Office for Civil Rights (federal)
- File complaint with state medical board
- Consult disability rights attorney

### Right to Bring Support Person

You can bring family member, friend, advocate to appointments.

Provider cannot:

- Refuse to allow support person
- Charge for support person
- Ask support person to leave (except safety concerns)
- Require you to make decisions alone if you need support

**Support person can:**

- Help you communicate
- Take notes
- Ask questions
- Be advocate if needed
- Provide emotional support

**Support person cannot:**

- Make medical decisions for you (unless legally designated—power of attorney)
- Force decisions you don't want

## Ethical Rights (Not Necessarily Legal But Important)

### Right to Respectful Treatment

You deserve to be treated with respect. This includes:

- Not asking inappropriate questions (inspiration porn, "how do you have sex," "aren't you depressed about disability")
- Not using inspiration narratives ("you're so brave")
- Not touching your assistive devices without permission
- Not speaking to you like child if adult
- Not assuming you can't understand
- Using correct pronouns and names
- Not outing your disabilities to others without permission

### Right to Your Own Medical Decisions

Medical decisions are yours to make. Provider cannot make them for you.

This includes:

- Treatment choices
- Whether to pursue aggressive or conservative treatment
- Whether to take medications
- Whether to have procedures
- End-of-life decisions
- Reproductive decisions
- Mental health treatment approach

If you lack capacity to make decisions:

- Legal guardian or power of attorney makes decisions
- Should be person you chose, not provider
- Decisions should reflect your values/wishes

### Right to Partnership, Not Paternalism

You deserve provider who partners with you, not one who decides for you.

Good provider:

- Listens to you
- Respects your expertise about your body
- Explains options
- Asks what you want
- Respects your decisions
- Collaborates on treatment plan

Bad provider:

- Tells you what you'll do
- Dismisses your input
- Makes decisions without you
- Assumes they know better
- Doesn't listen

## Rights for Specific Disabilities

### Mental Health Disability

**Right to:**

- Refusal of forced medication (with exceptions in crisis)
- Therapy approach of your choice
- Therapist of your choice (within insurance network)
- Voluntary hospitalization (emergency forced commitment exists but limited)
- Informed consent for psychiatric medications (including risks)
- Privacy of mental health records (extra protected under federal law)
- Peer support alternatives to professional treatment
- Second opinion on diagnosis/treatment plan

**Challenges:** States allow involuntary commitment if deemed danger to self/others. Rules vary. Know your state laws.

### Substance Use

**Right to:**

- Non-judgmental, evidence-based treatment
- Choice of treatment approach (medication-assisted, abstinence, harm reduction)
- Privacy from criminal charges (some protections)
- Healthcare despite substance use
- Refusal of forced treatment (with limited exceptions)

**Challenges:** Criminal system and healthcare system sometimes at odds. Legal representation important.

### Infectious Disease (HIV, Hepatitis C, TB)

**Right to:**

- Confidential treatment
- Non-discriminatory care
- Privacy (special protections for HIV)
- Informed consent for treatment
- Refusal of treatment (you decide approach)

**Challenges:** Stigma affects care. Seek experienced providers.

### Reproductive Disability

**Right to:**

- Information about reproductive options
- Voluntary sterilization only (cannot be forced)
- Contraceptive choice of your choice
- Pregnancy support (if choose pregnancy)
- Refusal of pregnancy (abortion access varies by state)
- Parenting support

**Historical trauma:** Forced sterilization of disabled people (especially women of color, women with intellectual disabilities). Ongoing organizing against this.

**Challenge:** Providers may try to discourage disabled people from having children. This is your right to decide.

## What to Do If Rights Violated

### Step 1: Speak to Provider

Sometimes problems are misunderstandings.

Tell provider: "I felt [my right] was violated when [specific incident]."

Provider may apologize and correct behavior.

If resolves, may not need further action.

### Step 2: Escalate Within Healthcare System

Speak to patient advocate (hospital required to have one).

File complaint with hospital administration.

Request meeting with provider's supervisor.

Patient advocate can help navigate complaint process.

### Step 3: File External Complaints

**Office for Civil Rights (Federal):**

File ADA complaint: www.hhs.gov/ocr

Complaint about disability discrimination in healthcare.

Federal investigation.

No cost.

Can sue for damages.

**State Medical Board:**

Each state has medical board.

File complaint about provider conduct.

Board investigates.

Can result in license suspension/revocation.

**State Attorney General:**

Consumer protection office.

Can file complaints about healthcare facility.

**Insurance Company:**

File complaint with your insurance about quality of care.

Insurance may deny claims for providers violating standards.

### Step 4: Legal Action

Consult disability rights attorney if:

- Significant harm occurred
- Provider violated rights egregiously
- Other complaints didn't resolve issue

Many disability rights attorneys free/low-cost.

Might recover damages if successful.

## Documentation for Rights Violations

If you believe rights violated:

**Document:**

- Date and time of incident
- What happened (specific behavior/refusal)
- Which right you believe violated
- Names of people involved
- Any witnesses
- Provider response

**Save:**

- Medical records
- Appointment notes
- Communication (emails, letters)
- Prescription documentation
- Insurance records

Documentation strengthens complaint.

## Disability Type Specific Rights

**Deaf/Hard of Hearing:**

Right to interpreter at all medical appointments (often violated—must insist).

Right to communication method of choice (ASL, lip reading, written).

**Blind/Low Vision:**

Right to accessible materials (large print, Braille, electronic).

Right to verbal description of visual information.

**Neurodivergent:**

Right to accommodations for executive dysfunction (reminders, flexible scheduling).

Right to sensory accommodations (quiet spaces, fragrance-free).

**Psychosocial/Mental Health:**

Right to trauma-informed care.

Right to refuse forced medications (with limits).

Right to refuse hospitalization (with limits).

**Hidden/Invisible:**

Right to be believed even if disability not visually obvious.

Right to prove disability medically (provider cannot assume you're lying).

## Global Perspective & Intersectionality

**Western Context:** US, Canada, Europe have legal frameworks protecting patient rights (ADA, legal informed consent, etc.). But enforcement weak. Discrimination common.

**Global South Context:** Patient rights often not protected in law. Disabled people have minimal healthcare access. Organizing focuses on basic care access, not rights protections.

**Intersectionality:**

**Race:** Medical racism means providers more likely to dismiss concerns, assume drug-seeking (especially Black patients). Right to be believed not equal across races.

**Immigration:** Undocumented people fear healthcare contact (deportation risk). Rights technically exist but accessing them risky.

**Gender/LGBTQ+:** Trans disabled people face discrimination on both grounds. Right to chosen pronouns/names sometimes violated.

**Class:** Medicaid patients sometimes experience discrimination (assumptions about drug use, reliability, cleanliness). Right to respectful treatment not equal across economic classes.
