# Pain Management

Chronic pain affects millions of disabled people. This page explores pain management strategies, approaches, and rights.

**Key Point:** Your pain is real. You deserve support managing it. No single "right" approach—find what works for you.

## Understanding Chronic Pain

Chronic pain: Pain lasting 3+ months (sometimes defined as 6+ months). Persistent, often unpredictable.

Chronic pain differs from acute pain (recent injury):

- Acute pain: Protective signal. Goes away with healing.
- Chronic pain: Becomes condition itself. Not always protective. Ongoing.

**Complexity:**

Pain involves:
- Physical (tissue damage, inflammation)
- Neurological (nerve signals, pain processing)
- Psychological (mood, stress, trauma)
- Social (isolation, activity limitation)

Effective pain management addresses all dimensions.

## Approaches to Pain Management

### Medical Approaches

**Pain Medications:**

**Nonopioid pain relievers:**
- Ibuprofen, naproxen (NSAIDs): Reduce inflammation, pain. GI side effects.
- Acetaminophen: Pain relief without anti-inflammatory. Less effective for many conditions.
- Topical pain relievers: Creams, patches applied to skin.

**Opioids:**
- Morphine, oxycodone, hydrocodone, methadone: Powerful pain relief. Risk of dependence/addiction. Constipation common.
- Prescription opioids: Only for moderate-severe pain. Often restricted due to addiction concerns.
- Complex situation: Opioids effective for many but over-prescribed for some, under-prescribed for others. Stigma around opioid use.

**Adjuvant medications** (not designed for pain but helpful):
- Antidepressants (amitriptyline, duloxetine): Helpful for nerve pain
- Anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin): Helpful for nerve pain
- Muscle relaxants: Helpful for muscle tension

**Other medications:**
- Ketamine: Emerging treatment for chronic pain
- Cannabis: Some evidence for pain relief (legal status varies by state)

**Injectable treatments:**
- Steroid injections: Reduce inflammation. Temporary relief.
- Botox: Muscle relaxation. Some pain conditions.
- Nerve blocks: Numb specific nerves. Temporary.

**Surgical approaches:**
- Spinal cord stimulation: Device sends electrical signals blocking pain
- Pain pump: Device delivers medication directly to spinal cord
- Surgery on joints, nerves: Sometimes helpful, sometimes not

### Physical Approaches

**Physical Therapy:**

Professional guidance on movement, stretching, strengthening. Helps maintain function.

Effective for:
- Joint pain
- Back/neck pain
- Post-surgery rehabilitation
- Injury prevention

**Rest vs. Movement:**

Balance important. Too much activity flares pain. Too little leads to deconditioning (loss of strength/flexibility).

Pacing: Gradually increase activity. Respect pain signals.

**Heat/Cold:**

Heat: Relaxes muscles, reduces pain. Good for muscle tension.

Cold: Reduces inflammation. Good for acute injury, swelling.

Alternating sometimes helpful.

**Massage/Bodywork:**

Massage, acupuncture, other bodywork help some people. Evidence mixed but low risk.

Find practitioners understanding chronic pain.

**Movement Therapies:**

Yoga, tai chi, gentle movement: Maintain flexibility, build strength, improve mood. Modified for pain acceptable.

**Accessibility important:** Not all classes/practitioners understand pain limitations. Find disability-friendly options.

### Behavioral Approaches

**Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy for Pain (CBT-P):**

Changes relationship with pain:
- Addresses unhelpful thoughts ("pain means permanent damage")
- Behavioral changes (gradually increase activity)
- Coping strategies

Effective for changing how pain impacts life.

**Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT):**

Accept pain exists. Commit to valued life despite pain.

Reduces pain-focused suffering. Improves function.

**Mindfulness/Meditation:**

Observe pain without judgment. Reduce emotional reactivity to pain.

Some evidence for reducing pain perception.

**Sleep Improvement:**

Pain and poor sleep cycle (pain causes insomnia, poor sleep worsens pain).

Good sleep hygiene important.

Sleep medication sometimes necessary.

**Stress Management:**

Stress worsens pain. Stress management helpful:
- Meditation
- Breathing exercises
- Progressive muscle relaxation
- Social connection

**Pacing Strategies:**

Pacing: Activity management to prevent overactivity, maintain function.

"Spoon theory" (common disability framework): Limited energy. Budget activities.

Not "push through pain" (harmful) nor "rest constantly" (deconditioning). Middle path.

### Holistic Approaches

**Nutrition:**

Anti-inflammatory diet: Some evidence for pain reduction.
- Whole grains
- Vegetables, fruits
- Omega-3 rich foods
- Limit processed foods, sugar

**Exercise:**

Regular gentle exercise: Improves mood, maintains function, may reduce pain.

Painful but helpful (exercise-induced pain different from chronic pain flare).

**Social Connection:**

Isolation worsens pain. Social connection supportive.

Peer support groups specifically for pain: Invaluable.

**Meaning/Purpose:**

Living for meaningful activities despite pain: Improves quality of life.

Doesn't cure pain but makes pain more bearable.

**Spirituality/Culture:**

Many find meaning in spiritual/cultural practices.

Pain management culturally informed.

## Pain Disability Reality

**Functioning with pain:**

Chronic pain is invisible (to others, sometimes to self). Forced to make impossible choices:

- Work or rest?
- See friends or preserve energy?
- Medical appointment or pain flare prevention?
- Medication side effects or more pain?

**Medical limitations:**

Opioid restrictions: Many providers reluctant to prescribe. Fear of addiction, legal consequences.

Result: Many people undertreated. In pain. No relief available.

**Stigma:**

Pain sometimes dismissed ("not real," "in your head," "you're just seeking pills").

Providers sometimes assume drug-seeking rather than pain.

**Disability Justice Perspective:**

Pain is disability. Disabled people deserve support, accommodations, community.

Not "overcome pain" narrative. Rather: "How do we live full lives with pain?"

## Pain Management Rights

**Right to pain assessment:**

Providers should ask about and assess pain.

Proper documentation.

**Right to pain treatment:**

Adequate pain management is standard of care.

Providers should offer evidence-based treatment.

**Right to options:**

Multiple approaches exist. You have right to know options and choose.

Shared decision-making (you and provider decide together).

**Right to refuse:**

Can refuse medication, therapy, approach.

Provider can express concern but cannot force.

**Right to second opinion:**

If provider inadequate, can see different provider.

Especially if pain undertreated.

## Pain and Mental Health

**Relationship:**

Pain causes depression, anxiety. Sadness, fear, frustration are normal responses to chronic pain.

Depression and anxiety can worsen pain perception.

Bidirectional: Address both.

**When to seek mental health support:**

Significant depression/anxiety alongside pain.

Difficulty coping with pain impact.

Suicidal thoughts (pain can be unbearable).

Therapy + pain management together often most effective.

## Finding Pain Management Specialists

**Types:**

**Pain Management Specialist (MD, DO):**

Medical doctor specializing in pain. Knowledgeable about medications, procedures.

**Physiatrist** (Physical Medicine & Rehabilitation):

Doctor specializing in rehabilitation including pain management.

**Anesthesiologist (Pain Management):**

Some anesthesiologists specialize in pain medicine.

**Physical Medicine Provider:**

Physical therapist, chiropractor, acupuncturist (varies in training/licensing).

**Where to find:**

- Your insurance provider directory
- Hospital specialist referrals
- University medical centers
- Google "pain management specialist [your city]"
- Ask your primary care doctor for referral

**What to ask:**

"Are you familiar with [your condition]?"

"What's your approach to pain management?" (Should include multimodal—multiple approaches, not just medication)

"Will you work with my other providers?"

"Do you understand disability?" (Important for respectful care)

## Pain Flares and Disability

Many chronic pain conditions unpredictable (flares/bad days).

**Managing flares:**

- Rest when needed (not optional)
- Medication adjustments if available
- Heat/cold
- Gentle movement if possible
- Accepting limitation temporarily
- Planning recovery time

**Work/school with flares:**

Employers/schools sometimes unsupportive of flares.

Right to accommodation (flexible schedule, work from home option, etc.).

Can request FMLA leave (unpaid) for flares.

**Communicating to others:**

People sometimes don't understand invisible flares.

"I'm having a pain flare" valid reason to change plans.

## Pain and Disability Justice

**Disability justice framework:**

- Recognizes pain as valid disability
- Values disabled people with pain
- Advocates for pain management access
- Centers multiply-marginalized people with pain
- Sees pain in context (poverty, racism, trauma)
- Builds community, not just individual treatment

**Organizing around pain:**

- Advocating for opioid access for those who need
- Fighting pain treatment denial
- Building peer support
- Demanding accessible pain management
- Recognizing pain's political/social dimensions

## Pain, Trauma, and Disability

Many disabled people have trauma history (medical trauma, abuse, neglect).

Trauma can:
- Increase pain perception
- Complicate pain management
- Make medical care triggering

**Trauma-informed pain management:**

- Providers aware of trauma
- Respects bodily autonomy
- Doesn't force treatments
- Allows choice and control

## Assistive Tech for Pain Management

**Devices:**

- TENS units (electrical stimulation)
- Heat/cold packs (accessible versions)
- Positioning aids (pillows, wedges)
- Accessibility aids (reaching tools, etc.—reducing strain)

**Funding:**

Medicare, Medicaid, insurance cover some.

Vocational rehab may fund if needed for work.

See [[Assistive Tech Funding]] for more.

## Medication and Pain: Complex Issues

**Opioid Crisis Context:**

Rising addiction rates from prescription opioids led to restrictions.

Result: Both bad (over-prescribing, addiction) and harmful (under-treatment of legitimate pain).

Many people with chronic pain now struggle to get pain medication.

**Substance Use and Pain:**

Many disabled people with pain history use substances (alcohol, drugs) for pain relief.

Not necessarily addiction but concerning pattern.

Medication-assisted treatment option.

**Finding balanced providers:**

Balance between:
- Not dismissing pain needs
- Not over-prescribing
- Trying multimodal approaches first
- Providing access when medication needed

Increasingly difficult to find.

## Global Perspective

**Western:** Advanced pain management options available (expensive, unequal access).

**Global South:** Pain management often unavailable. Opioids (most effective for severe pain) restricted/unavailable in most countries. Access disparities massive.
