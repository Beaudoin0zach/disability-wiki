# Home Health Care: In-Home Support Services

Home health care—nurses, personal care assistants (PCAs), and other support in your home—enables many disabled people to live independently in community rather than institutions.

**Types of in-home care:**

- Personal care assistance (bathing, dressing, toileting, eating, grooming)
- Nursing care (wound care, medication management, catheter care, etc.)
- Homemaking (meal prep, cleaning, laundry)
- Therapy services (physical, occupational, speech therapy at home)
- Mental health support
- Companion care (social engagement, safety)

## Who Provides Home Care

**Nurses (RN, LPN):**

Registered Nurse (RN) or Licensed Practical Nurse (LPN).

Medical skills (medication administration, wound care, catheter management, etc.).

More education/skills than PCA.

Higher cost than PCA.

**Personal Care Assistants (PCAs):**

Training varies (minimal to substantial).

Assist with activities of daily living (bathing, dressing, toileting, eating).

Cannot do medical tasks (varies by state regulation).

Lower cost than nurses.

**Home Health Aides (HHAs):**

Trained in basic care. Less skilled than nurses.

Assist with bathing, dressing, toileting, mobility.

Can do basic household tasks.

**Homemakers/Chore Workers:**

Meal preparation, cleaning, laundry.

No personal care.

**Therapists:**

Physical, occupational, speech therapists providing therapy in home.

Usually covered by insurance for post-hospitalization rehabilitation.

**Case Managers:**

Coordinate services, manage care plan, advocate.

Sometimes included in home care services.

## Accessing Home Care

### Through Insurance/Medicare/Medicaid

**Medicare Home Health:**

Covered after hospitalization or skilled nursing facility (SNF) stay.

Homebound (unable to leave home without difficulty).

Physician orders home care.

Includes:
- Nursing care
- Therapy
- Homemaking (limited, must be medically necessary)
- Equipment

Typically short-term (weeks/months) post-hospitalization.

Medicaid Home Services:

Varies by state. Many states cover:
- Personal care services
- Homemaking
- Companion care

Often must be homebound, low-income, Medicaid-eligible.

**Prior authorization required.**

### Private Pay

Hire and pay providers directly (or through agencies).

**Costs:**

PCA: $20–25/hour typical
Nurse: $25–40/hour or more
Agency placement (adds markup): 25–50% more

Adding up: 8 hours/day care = $160–200/day out-of-pocket = $4,800–6,000/month.

Unaffordable for most disabled people.

### Waiver Programs

Some state Medicaid waivers cover personal care services:

Participants receive funding (dollar amount per month).

Can hire own PCA or use agency.

More flexible than traditional Medicaid home care.

Long waiting lists (sometimes years).

Check if eligible for state waiver program.

### Employer Option (Work-Related)

If employed, employer accommodation may include:

Personal care assistant at work.

Employer pays (required accommodation under ADA).

**Vocational Rehabilitation** may fund PCA for work if employment goal.

### Family and Informal Support

Many disabled people rely on family:

- Parent, spouse, adult child providing care
- Usually unpaid
- Unpaid caregiver burden significant
- Unsustainable if family not available/able

Some states have "In-Home Supportive Services" (IHSS) that pay family members:

California, some other states.

Low payment ($15–20/hour) but something.

## Rights with Home Care Providers

### Right to Privacy

Home care workers enter your private space.

You have right to:
- Privacy of medical information
- Respect of your home
- Control of your space (workers don't have access when not working)

### Right to Safe Care

You have right to:
- Proper training of workers
- Background checks
- Competent care
- Reporting of safety concerns
- Investigation of abuse/neglect

If abuse/neglect: Report to:
- Healthcare provider
- Adult Protective Services (APS)
- Police if crime

### Right to Choice

Medicare home health: Limited choice (insurance may restrict providers).

Private pay: More choice (you choose provider).

Waiver programs: Usually can choose provider.

**Right to fire provider:** If not working, can switch.

### Right to Autonomy

You direct your care, not worker.

Worker should follow your instructions:
- How you like things done
- Your schedule
- Your preferences

You're the boss (in private pay situation especially).

### Right to Accessibility

Home care providers must accommodate your disabilities:
- Communicate accessibly
- Accommodate your needs
- Understand your disabilities

If provider dismissive of disability, not good fit.

## Common Home Care Challenges

**Inadequate Hours:**

Medicaid covers limited hours (often 4–8 hours/day). If need more, gap unfunded.

Many disabled people underfunded for their actual needs.

**Provider Reliability:**

Workers don't show up, call in sick, quit unexpectedly.

Disrupts care, forces switching providers.

If private pay: Must quickly find replacement.

If Medicaid: May not have backup, creates crisis.

**Low Wages:**

Home care workers underpaid ($15–20/hour common).

Results in high turnover, poorly trained workers.

Disabled people lose relationships with good workers.

**Personality Fit:**

Not every worker compatible with every disabled person.

Finding good match challenging.

**Medical Complexity:**

PCA cannot do medical tasks (in many states).

If need nursing, more expensive.

Insurance limiting nursing hours.

**Intimacy and Dignity:**

Having workers assist with bathing, toileting requires vulnerability.

Finding workers respectful of dignity important.

**Abuse Risk:**

Private home = isolation. Abuse/neglect risk if worker problematic.

Safeguarding measures important:
- Background checks
- Training on respect
- Monitoring
- Right to report

## Hiring Your Own PCA (Private Pay)

If using private pay or waiver program allowing own hiring:

**Step 1: Determine needs**

Hours needed per day/week.

Tasks needed (personal care, homemaking, etc.).

**Step 2: Set wage**

Research local PCA rates.

Offer competitive wage (retention matters).

Usually $18–25/hour.

**Step 3: Recruit**

Post job:
- Craigslist
- Care.com (vetting variable)
- Local disability community
- Word of mouth

Interview candidates.

Background check (critical).

**Step 4: Hire and train**

Hire directly (private employer situation).

Train on:
- Your specific needs/preferences
- Medical/safety issues if applicable
- Your boundaries
- Your schedule/expectations

**Step 5: Ongoing management**

Pay consistently.

Provide feedback (what's working, what isn't).

Fire if not working (right to terminate).

Treat fairly (work is hard, low-paid).

## Working with Agencies

If using home care agency:

**Benefits:**

Agency handles recruitment, background checks, training, payroll, taxes, worker comp insurance.

Backup workers if regular calls in sick.

Liability/legal protection (agency responsible for worker conduct).

**Drawbacks:**

More expensive (agency markup).

Less control over specific worker.

Workers may be less invested (don't work for you, work for agency).

Turnover high.

**How it works:**

1. Contact agency
2. Agency assesses needs
3. Agency provides care plan and cost estimate
4. You approve
5. Agency assigns workers
6. Regular check-ins on care quality

**Questioning Agency:**

Where does money go? (How much goes to worker vs. agency profit?)

What's worker training?

How do they handle backup if worker unavailable?

What's process for complaint/change workers?

What guarantees do they offer?

## Medicaid Home Services Process

If Medicaid covers home care in your state:

**Step 1: Apply for Medicaid (if not already)**

Establish Medicaid eligibility.

**Step 2: Request home care assessment**

Contact Medicaid.

Request home and community-based services assessment.

**Step 3: Assessment**

Social worker assesses:
- Medical/functional needs
- Home environment
- Support system
- Services needed

**Step 4: Care plan development**

If approved: Care plan developed with you.

Hours of service, type of service, frequency determined.

**Step 5: Provider assignment**

Medicaid assigns provider (or you choose if allowed).

Services begin.

**Step 6: Ongoing review**

Annual/periodic review of needs.

Adjustment as needed.

## Funding Your Own PCA (IHSS Models)

Some states (California, others) have "In-Home Supportive Services" (IHSS):

**How it works:**

- Disabled person determined to need care
- Receives monthly stipend for hours of care
- Can hire own PCA (can be family member)
- Disabled person is "employer" of PCA
- Medicaid pays worker directly (or reimburses)

**Benefits:**

- Flexibility in choosing worker (family, friends, community member)
- Can adjust hours as needed
- Less bureaucratic than traditional home care

**Drawbacks:**

- Very low payment ($15–18/hour typical)
- You manage employment (hiring, firing, scheduling)
- Worker must be willing to work for low wage
- Not available in all states

**If available in your state:**

Contact your state Medicaid office about IHSS eligibility.

Often must be very low income.

## Home Care and Work

**Working while receiving home care:**

If Medicaid pays for home care: Must work under income limits (home care doesn't affect income limits if structured correctly).

If private pay: No income restrictions.

If waiver program: Rules vary by state.

## Caregiver Burden and Support

**If family caring for you:**

Caregiver burden serious. Isolation, burnout, health problems common.

**Supporting your caregiver:**

- If can afford, pay them (even small amount)
- Recognize their work
- Ensure their breaks/rest
- Don't guilt-trip about care needs
- Support their wellbeing

**Caregiver resources:**

- Caregiver support groups
- Respite care (temporary backup care)
- Counseling for caregiver stress
- AARP Caregiver resources
- National Alliance for Caregiving

**If caregiver becomes unable:**

Plan for transition to professional care.

Build relationships with other community support.

Don't put all eggs in one basket (single caregiver).

## Rights Violations in Home Care

If experiencing abuse, neglect, or rights violations:

**Report to:**

- Adult Protective Services (if elderly or incapacitated)
- Long-Term Care Ombudsman (if residential facility)
- Police (if crime)
- Healthcare provider
- Insurance

Document:

- Specific incidents
- Dates/times
- Witnesses
- Injuries/harm
- Previous reports

Keep records for legal action if needed.

## Home Care and Privacy

**Important:** Workers have access to your home.

**Privacy safeguards:**

- Clear contract stating confidentiality
- Background checks
- Training on privacy
- Right to privacy (workers don't live in your home, don't have keys)
- Right to refuse worker if privacy concerns

## Home Care Equipment and Modifications

Home care often works best with:

- Accessible bathroom (grab bars, roll-in shower, raised toilet)
- Accessible bedroom (accessible bed, accessible furniture)
- Accessible kitchen (if worker preparing meals)
- Equipment (transfer equipment, mobility aids)

See [[Home Modifications]] for accessibility details.

Insurance may fund some modifications if home care needed.

## International Perspective

**Global South:** Most disabled people cannot afford any home care. Family provides unpaid care.

**Western:** Medicaid/Medicare coverage varies. Insurance-based system inequitable.

**Model differences:**

- Waiver model (funding individual for care): More flexible, person-centered
- Agency model (funding agencies): More regulated, less personal choice
