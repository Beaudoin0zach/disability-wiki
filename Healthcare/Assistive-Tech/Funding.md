# Assistive Tech Funding: Paying for Equipment and Devices

Assistive technology (AT)—equipment and devices enabling participation—often expensive. This page explains how to pay for it.

**Examples of AT:**
- Wheelchairs, walkers, canes, scooters
- Hearing aids, cochlear implants
- Screen readers, speech-to-text software
- Braille displays, magnifiers
- AAC (augmentative and alternative communication) devices
- Smart home devices
- Adapted kitchen tools
- Vehicle modifications
- Prosthetics and orthotics

## Funding Sources for Assistive Technology

### Medicare

For Medicare recipients (disabled 24+ months on SSDI, seniors 65+):

**Coverage:**

Durable Medical Equipment (DME):
- Wheelchairs, walkers, canes
- Oxygen, CPAP
- Hospital beds
- Commodes
- Lifts

Prescription items:
- Hearing aids (limited)
- Prosthetics (some)
- Orthotics

**Not covered:**

- Most AAC devices
- Screen readers, software
- Personal computers
- Routine glasses/contacts
- Cochlear implants (sometimes)

**How to access:**

Ask doctor to prescribe equipment. Doctor contacts Medicare for prior authorization. Medicare covers 80% after deductible (you pay 20%).

**Cost:**

Deductible: $1,600/year (2024)  
Copay: 20% after deductible

Many disabled people hit deductible with multiple needs.

### Medicaid

For Medicaid recipients (varies by state):

**Coverage varies dramatically by state:**

Generous states cover:
- Wheelchairs, mobility aids
- Hearing aids
- Vision devices
- Personal care equipment
- Home modifications
- Some technology

Restrictive states cover:
- Basic mobility aids only
- Limited or no hearing aids
- Limited vision devices

**How to access:**

Ask doctor to prescribe. Check your state Medicaid coverage. State processes prior authorization.

**Cost:**

Usually free or minimal copay for disabled people.

**Best to check with your state Medicaid office about specific AT coverage.**

### Veterans Administration

For veterans with service-connected disability:

**Coverage:**

VA covers assistive technology as medically necessary:
- Prosthetics, orthotics
- Hearing aids (100% if high rating)
- Mobility aids
- Adaptive equipment
- Some technology (case-by-case)

**How to access:**

Ask VA provider to prescribe. VA covers if deemed medically necessary.

**Cost:**

Usually free for high-rating disabled veterans.

### Private Insurance

Depends on specific plan. Check your plan's coverage details.

**Generally:**

Some plans cover:
- Durable medical equipment (wheelchairs, walkers)
- Hearing aids (some plans)
- Prosthetics (some plans)

**Many exclude:**

- Software/computers
- AAC devices
- Vehicle modifications
- Home modifications

**How to access:**

Doctor prescribes. Contact insurance for prior authorization. Plan covers percentage (varies).

### Vocational Rehabilitation (VR)

Every state has vocational rehabilitation program. Can fund AT for work:

**Coverage:**

VR funds assistive technology needed for employment:
- Wheelchairs, mobility aids (if needed for work)
- Computer equipment and software
- AAC devices (if needed for communication at work)
- Hearing aids, cochlear implants
- Vehicle modifications
- Workplace modifications

**Who qualifies:**

- Documented disability
- Barrier to employment from disability
- Goal of employment or self-employment

**How to access:**

Contact state VR (often called Division of Vocational Rehabilitation or similar).

Request assessment.

Work with VR counselor to develop employment plan.

VR funds technology needed per plan.

**Important:** VR for work-related needs. Can't fund AT for daily living (home modifications, etc.) unless related to work.

### State Assistive Technology Programs

Many states have AT funding specifically:

**Coverage:**

Low-interest loans for AT.

Sometimes grants for low-income people.

Device lending libraries (borrow before buying).

Refurbished equipment (lower cost).

**Who qualifies:**

Usually low-income disabled people.

Residency in state required.

**How to find:**

Contact state disability agency.

Ask about AT funding program.

Each state different—research yours.

### School-Based Funding (K-12, Higher Ed)

For students:

**K-12:**

Schools required to provide AT needed for IEP (Individualized Education Plan).

School determines needs and funds.

Can appeal if school denies needed AT.

**Higher Ed:**

Disability services office funds AT for accessibility (magnifiers, screen readers, note-taking devices, etc.).

Some programs more generous than others.

Cost to student varies by school.

**How to access:**

Contact disability services office at school.

Provide documentation of disability.

Request AT assessment.

Office funds or arranges funding.

### Pharmaceutical/Device Company Patient Assistance

Many AT companies offer assistance for low-income people:

**Common programs:**

Hearing aid manufacturers offer low-income programs.

Prosthetic companies offer sliding scales.

Wheelchair manufacturers offer discounts.

Software companies offer reduced prices (especially educational discounts).

**How to access:**

Contact manufacturer directly (number on device).

Ask about assistance/discount programs.

Provide income documentation if requested.

Often free or deeply discounted.

### Non-Profit Organizations

Disability-specific organizations sometimes fund AT:

**How to find:**

Contact disability organization for your condition.

Ask if they have equipment assistance program.

Many maintain lists of donors/programs.

Some provide small grants.

### Workplace Accommodation

If employed:

**ADA Requirement:**

Employer must provide reasonable accommodations for disabled employees.

AT needed for work often considered accommodation.

Employer pays, not you.

**Examples:**

- Screen reader (software)
- Ergonomic keyboard
- Accessible desk
- Height-adjustable workspace
- Phone amplifier
- Accessible software

**How to access:**

Request in writing to HR.

Explain accommodation needed.

Provide documentation if needed.

Employer must accommodate.

If employer refuses:

File ADA complaint with EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission).

Consult employment law attorney.

### Medicaid Waiver Programs

Some state Medicaid waivers fund:

- Home modifications (accessibility)
- Assistive technology
- Personal care equipment
- Communication devices

Varies by state and waiver program.

Check if eligible for state waiver program.

### DIY/Low-Cost Options

If expensive AT unaffordable:

**Accessibility in technology already available:**

- iOS/Mac built-in accessibility features (free)
- Android built-in accessibility features (free)
- Windows accessibility features (free, if have Windows)
- Free screen readers (NVDA for Windows)
- Free speech-to-text (built into most phones)
- Free magnifiers (built into phones, computers)

**Low-cost alternatives:**

- Used equipment (lower cost than new)
- Generic versions (wheelchair, walker, etc. often cheaper than name-brand)
- Open-source software (free alternatives)
- Community tool libraries (borrow equipment)

**Device lending libraries:**

Many communities have lending libraries where can borrow:
- Wheelchairs
- Walkers
- Canes
- Crutches
- Grab bars
- Commodes
- Other equipment

Return after use. Free. Contact local disability organization.

## Specific AT Funding by Type

### Wheelchairs and Mobility Aids

**Medicare:** Covers if medically necessary (prior auth required)

**Medicaid:** Most states cover

**Vocational Rehab:** Covers if needed for work

**Workplace:** Employer accommodates

**Insurance:** Depends on plan

**DIY:** Used wheelchairs, basic walkers often affordable

### Hearing Aids

**Medicare:** Limited coverage (not all types)

**Medicaid:** Many states cover (some don't)

**Veterans:** VA covers if service-connected

**Vocational Rehab:** Covers if needed for work

**Manufacturer programs:** Most offer low-income assistance

**Workplace:** Accommodation if needed for work

**Cost:** $1,000–6,000+ per pair new. Refurbished cheaper. Some assistance programs free.

### Screen Readers/AAC Devices

**Medicare:** Usually not covered (technology exclusion)

**Medicaid:** Some states cover (waiver programs)

**Vocational Rehab:** Covers if needed for work

**School:** Schools must provide if needed for education

**Workplace:** Employer pays as accommodation

**Free/low-cost:**

- NVDA (free screen reader for Windows)
- VoiceOver (built-in Mac/iPhone)
- TalkBack (built-in Android)
- AT&T Speech Assistant (AAC, free)
- JAWs ($90/month student, $1000+ professional—expensive but most powerful)

### Vision Devices

**Medicare:** Limited (magnifiers sometimes, not routine glasses/contacts)

**Medicaid:** Varies by state (some cover, some don't)

**Vocational Rehab:** Covers if needed for work

**Workplace:** Accommodation if needed for work

**Low-cost:**

- High-power readers (drug store, cheap)
- Phone magnification (free, built-in)
- Computer magnification software (often free or cheap)

### Vehicle Modifications

**Medicare/Medicaid:** Rarely cover

**Vocational Rehab:** Covers if needed for work transportation

**Workplace:** Employer may cover if needed for job

**Financing:**

- Car manufacturer programs (some offer discounts)
- Loan: Vehicle financing may cover modifications
- Non-profit grants: Some organizations fund vehicle mods
- Out-of-pocket: Many can't afford and rely on public transit

### Home Modifications

**Medicaid:** Some states cover through waiver programs (ramps, grab bars, etc.)

**Vocational Rehab:** Only if modification needed for work

**Workplace:** N/A (home, not work)

**DIY/Low-cost:**

- Grab bars (affordable)
- Ramps (can build or inexpensive contractors)
- Accessible bathroom modifications (varies cost)
- Door openers (expensive technology)
- Smart home devices (increasingly affordable)

**Non-profit grants:** Some disability organizations fund home modifications

**Fair Housing Act:** Landlords required to allow reasonable modifications. Some allow you to install, remove on leaving. Others require landlord approval.

## Navigating the System

**Step 1: Determine what you need**

List AT you need for:
- Work/school
- Daily living
- Independence

Be specific (not just "wheelchair"—specify type, features needed).

**Step 2: Research funding sources**

Determine which you're eligible for:
- Medicare? Medicaid? Insurance? VR? School?
- Often multiple sources

**Step 3: Get medical documentation**

Doctor or specialist writes prescription/letter stating:
- AT needed
- Why medically necessary
- Specific device/type recommended
- Expected duration

**Step 4: Submit for prior authorization**

Contact funding source (insurance, Medicare, Medicaid, VR, school).

Submit:
- Prescription/medical letter
- Device specifications
- Cost estimate

Funding source approves/denies.

**Step 5: Obtain device**

If approved, get device from:
- Durable Medical Equipment (DME) supplier
- Specialty retailer
- Manufacturer

Work with supplier on best option.

**Step 6: Arrange payment**

- Funding source pays supplier directly (ideal)
- You pay, get reimbursed
- Insurance/funding pays percentage, you pay percentage

## Appeals if Denied

If funding source denies AT:

**Step 1: Understand reason**

Funding source provides explanation.

Common reasons:
- Not medically necessary (disputable)
- Not covered by plan (may appeal)
- Specific device not approved (different device may be)
- Prescribed by wrong type of provider

**Step 2: Appeal**

Request appeal in writing.

Provide additional documentation:
- More detailed medical necessity letter
- Research showing device effectiveness
- Second medical opinion if helpful
- Documentation showing functional need

**Step 3: External review**

If appeal denied, request external/independent review.

External reviewer evaluates appeal.

Many approvals at this stage.

**Step 4: Complaint**

If all internal/external options exhausted:

File complaint with your state insurance commissioner (if insurance).

File complaint with your state Medicaid office (if Medicaid).

File complaint with school district (if school-based).

## Assistive Technology Funding Resources

**Federal/National:**

- AT Device Loan Program (varies by state): Google "assistive technology loan [your state]"
- Vocational Rehabilitation: Contact state VR office
- Medicare: Medicare.gov, call 1-800-MEDICARE
- Medicaid: Medicaid.gov, state Medicaid office

**Non-Profits:**

- National Assistive Technology Research Institute (NARI): Research on AT funding
- Disability-specific organizations (often have AT funding info)
- Independent Living Centers: Often know local funding
- State disability agencies: Often have AT programs

**Device-Specific:**

- Hearing Loss Association of America: Hearing aid funding
- American Foundation for the Blind: Vision device funding
- AAC organizations: Speech device funding
