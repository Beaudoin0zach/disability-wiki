# Indigenous Disability Perspectives

> **A note before you read.** This page was compiled by non-Indigenous editors from public sources. Wherever it touches Indigenous knowledge or specific community practice, the authoritative voices are Indigenous people themselves — and knowledge is often specific to one nation and should not be flattened into a single "Indigenous" view. Treat the framework sections as **pointers toward Indigenous-authored work**, not the final word. We'd welcome review by Indigenous disabled readers.

---

## Indigenous knowledge and leadership first

Long before "disability" was a category, Indigenous peoples had their own ways of understanding bodymind difference — and those ways are alive today.

**Difference understood relationally, not as deficit.** Many Indigenous knowledge systems locate a person's worth in their relationships and contributions to the community, not in a checklist of what a body can or cannot do. Diné scholar **Dr. Sandra Yellowhorse** describes disability through *k'é* — a Diné way of life "rooted in relationships," centering interdependence and care. In this framing, people are *relations first*. This resonates with the disability-justice values at the heart of this wiki: interdependence over forced independence, collective access, "most-impacted lead."

**Many Indigenous languages had no equivalent to "disability."** A recurring observation in Indigenous-authored writing is that many Native languages have no direct word for "disabled" — described not as a gap but as evidence of a worldview focused less on what a person can't do and more on who they are and how the community supports them. *(This is community-specific; it's true of many languages but shouldn't be asserted as universal across the world's thousands of Indigenous peoples.)*

**Indigenous disabled leadership is organized and ongoing:**
- **[First Peoples Disability Network](https://fpdn.org.au/)** (Australia) — the national voice of and for First Nations people with disability, *governed by* Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander disabled people.
- **[Indigenous Disability Canada / BCANDS](https://www.bcands.bc.ca/)** — a national Indigenous-led nonprofit serving First Nations, Métis, and Inuit peoples with disabilities; holds UN consultative status.

## Sovereignty is a disability-justice issue

Access to culturally safe healthcare, clean water, and traditional food systems is mediated by land and sovereignty. Where these are taken or degraded, disability rises — which is why Indigenous disability advocates frame **self-determination, not just "services,"** as the goal. Solutions designed *for* Indigenous communities are not the same as solutions led *by* them.

## Colonial harm (told honestly)

This is real context, not the center of the story — but it must be accurate.

- **Disproportionate prevalence.** Indigenous peoples report among the highest disability rates anywhere: American Indian/Alaska Native adults report the **highest rate of any racial group in the U.S.** (around a third or more, depending on the survey and year); Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians and First Nations people in Canada show similarly elevated rates. *Every figure here is an estimate, often collected by colonial governments using non-Indigenous definitions, and frequently excludes on-reserve and remote populations — so the numbers likely undercount and don't map onto Indigenous understandings of difference.*
- **Forced sterilization.** A 1976 U.S. government investigation (General Accounting Office — now the Government Accountability Office — report **HRD-77-3**) found that the Indian Health Service had performed sterilizations without obtaining consent that met federal informed-consent regulations: consent forms were incomplete or failed to document voluntary, informed consent in the four IHS areas reviewed for 1973–1976. Scholars estimate that coerced or non-consensual sterilizations of Native women in this era numbered in the thousands — part of a wider history of coerced sterilization. *(Recent state-level investigations show this isn't a closed chapter.)*
- **Residential and boarding schools.** Peer-reviewed reviews document lasting health harms — trauma, psychosomatic conditions, loss of language and culture — transmitted across generations.
- **Land and environment.** Disability scholarship in conversation with Indigenous theory frames harm to land and harm to bodyminds as connected — which is why sovereignty over land and health systems is itself a disability-justice issue.

Each of these systems was also met with resistance, lawsuits, and survivors who refused to disappear — that resistance is as much the history as the harm.

## Organizations and resources

- **[First Peoples Disability Network](https://fpdn.org.au/)** (Australia) — by and for First Nations disabled people.
- **[Indigenous Disability Canada / BCANDS](https://www.bcands.bc.ca/)** — First Nations, Métis & Inuit disability services and advocacy.
- **[National Indian Council on Aging — Disability resources](https://www.nicoa.org/programs/technical-assistance-and-resource-center/disabilities/)** — disability toolkit for Native communities.
- **[Association on American Indian Affairs](https://www.indian-affairs.org/)** — publishes Indigenous-authored writing on disability and culture (including Dr. Yellowhorse's work).
- **[Disability Visibility Project](https://disabilityvisibilityproject.com/)** — amplifies first-person disabled writing, including Indigenous authors.
